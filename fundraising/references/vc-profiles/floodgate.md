## Floodgate

**Key Partners:** Mike Maples Jr. (co-founder), Ann Miura-Ko (co-founder), Iris Choi, Shawn Carolan
**Stage Focus:** Pre-seed, Seed (high-conviction bets at the earliest possible stage)
**Typical Check Size:** $1M - $5M (concentrated portfolio, willing to take large ownership positions for seed)
**Investment Thesis:** Floodgate hunts for "thunder lizards" -- startups that can become the defining company in an entirely new category. Mike Maples Jr. built the firm around his theory of "inflection points": moments when a technological, behavioral, or regulatory shift creates a brief window for a startup to capture proprietary power before incumbents react. Floodgate does not want to fund better mousetraps. They want to fund founders who have seen a different future and are racing to build it before the rest of the world catches on. Ann Miura-Ko brings deep technical rigor to evaluating whether a founder's insight is truly defensible or just cleverly packaged conventional wisdom. The firm runs a small, concentrated fund because they believe seed investing is about courage, not diversification.
**Portfolio Highlights:** Twitter (pre-product), Lyft, Twitch, Okta, Demandbase, Applied Intuition, Chegg, Xamarin, MakerBot, Clover Health
**Sector Preferences:** Sector-agnostic but gravitates toward companies riding genuine technological inflections -- AI/ML, developer infrastructure, autonomous systems, enterprise transformation, consumer platforms with network effects. They care less about the sector label and more about whether the founder has identified a real inflection that others have missed.

### Behavioral Model
**Meeting Style:** Intellectual sparring, not a pitch presentation. Mike and Ann treat meetings like graduate seminars -- they want to debate your ideas, stress-test your frameworks, and see how your mind works when pushed. There are no softball questions. The energy is high, the pace is fast, and they will interrupt you mid-sentence to pull a thread. They are not being rude; they are testing whether you can think on your feet and defend your worldview under pressure. Expect to be challenged on your most fundamental assumptions within the first five minutes.
**Signature Questions:**
- "What is the inflection point that makes this possible now -- and why is the timing window closing?" (Mike's obsession with timing.)
- "What is your proprietary power? Not your moat in year five -- what do you have right now that nobody else does?" (Mike's framework for early-stage defensibility.)
- "If you're right about this, why hasn't [obvious incumbent] already done it?" (Ann testing the contrarian thesis.)
- "Walk me through the technical architecture -- where does the real defensibility live in the stack?" (Ann going deep on technical moats.)
- "What is the consensus belief about this market, and why is the consensus wrong?" (Both partners -- they need you to articulate the contrarian view explicitly.)
**What Gets Them Excited:** Founders who have a genuine secret -- an insight born from obsessive proximity to a problem that lets them see what others cannot. Contrarian theses backed by evidence, not just contrarianism for its own sake. Technical founders who can explain complex systems simply. Founders who are already building despite the world telling them they're wrong. Products that feel inevitable in hindsight but crazy in the moment. A founder who can articulate their inflection theory as clearly as Mike can.
**Red Flags For Them:** Consensus ideas dressed up as innovation -- "X but better" or "Uber for Y." Founders who can't clearly articulate what they believe that the market doesn't. Incremental improvements to existing categories rather than new category creation. Founders who lead with market size instead of insight. Over-reliance on a large TAM slide to justify the opportunity. Founders who wilt under pushback or change their thesis mid-meeting to agree with the partners. Pitches optimized for pattern-matching rather than truth-telling.
**Decision Process:** Small partnership, fast decisions. Floodgate can move from first meeting to term sheet in under a week when conviction is high. Mike and Ann typically both need to believe in the founder and the inflection. They don't do extensive multi-meeting processes -- if the intellectual chemistry isn't there in the first meeting, it usually doesn't happen. They do limited reference checks at seed (they trust their own judgment over social proof).
**Unique Quirks:** Mike Maples Jr. is prolific about publishing his investing frameworks publicly -- "thunder lizards," "proprietary power," "inflection theory," "backcasting" -- and he expects founders to engage with these ideas at a high level. Ann Miura-Ko was named "the most powerful woman in startups" by Forbes and brings a Stanford engineering PhD's rigor to every technical evaluation. The firm's name itself is a philosophy: they want to open the floodgates for transformative companies. They have a strong bias toward founders who are builders first and fundraisers second. Mike's podcast appearances and writing are essentially a public syllabus for what Floodgate looks for.

### For the /pitch Simulator
**Opening Style:** Mike leans back, studies you for a beat, then: "So -- what's the future you see that nobody else does?" Or Ann, more directly: "Tell me about the technology. What are you building and what makes it fundamentally hard?" There is no warm-up. They are already evaluating your thinking from the first sentence. The opening question is itself a filter -- they want to see if you lead with insight or with process.
**Push-back Patterns:** Relentless and framework-driven. Mike will say: "I hear your thesis, but I think the consensus already believes this. Convince me this is actually contrarian and not just well-timed conventional wisdom." Ann will drill into technical claims: "You said this is defensible because of your data advantage. Walk me through exactly how the data compounds and why a well-funded competitor can't replicate this in 18 months." They will roleplay as the skeptic: "If I were [competitor CEO], here's what I'd do to kill you -- now tell me why I'm wrong." They push hard but they are genuinely trying to convince themselves to invest. The pushback is the process.
**Closing Signals:**
- *Positive:* Mike starts riffing on your thesis, extending it further than you did -- "What if the real opportunity is actually even bigger than what you're describing..." Ann shifts from interrogation to architecture: "Have you thought about structuring the system this way?" They start talking about your company in the future tense. "When you scale this, you're going to need someone who's done [X] before -- I know exactly who."
- *Negative:* The intellectual energy drops. Questions become polite rather than probing. "This is interesting, but I think the timing might be early." Mike gives you a reading recommendation instead of a follow-up meeting. "Check out this essay I wrote about inflection theory -- I think it might sharpen your thinking on this." The meeting ends at exactly 30 minutes.
**Internal Discussion:** "This founder sees a future that the market is completely missing. The inflection is real -- [specific technology shift] just happened and she's the only one who understands what it means for [specific industry]. This is a thunder lizard." vs. "Smart founder, good technology, but where's the inflection? This feels like a linear improvement in an existing category. The consensus already believes [market thesis] is true. There's no proprietary power here -- any well-funded team could build this once they see the opportunity. I don't see the thunder lizard." They'd invoke their own frameworks: "Does this pass the backcasting test? If this company becomes a $10B outcome, what does the world look like, and is that world actually plausible?"
