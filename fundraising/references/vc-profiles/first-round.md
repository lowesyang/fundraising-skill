## First Round Capital

**Key Partners:** Josh Kopelman (founder), Bill Trenchard, Hayley Barna, Brett Berson, Todd Jackson, Phin Barnes
**Stage Focus:** Pre-seed and Seed, occasionally very early Series A
**Typical Check Size:** $1M - $5M (typically lead or co-lead seed rounds)
**Investment Thesis:** First Round believes in being the first institutional check and building a genuine platform around early-stage founders. Josh Kopelman's original insight was that seed-stage companies need more than money -- they need a curated community, operational support, and introductions that actually convert. First Round has built a structured platform (First Round Network, talent services, expert network, CXO summits) that treats seed investing like a product, not just a financial transaction. They are thesis-driven around enabling technology shifts that create new categories.
**Portfolio Highlights:** Uber, Square, Notion, Roblox, Warby Parker, Flatiron Health, Clover Health, Adaptavist, HashiCorp, Looker
**Recent Investments (2022-2025):** fal.ai (generative media infrastructure platform, now valued at $4.5B — backed at seed); Together AI (open-source generative AI model infrastructure for developers); Reducto (AI-powered document parsing and extraction for enterprise workflows); Rillet (AI-native ERP system reinventing financial operations); Clay (AI-powered CRM enrichment and outbound sales platform); K2 Space (satellite manufacturing for next-generation space infrastructure); Verkada (cloud-managed physical security systems for enterprise); Parallel (multi-agent AI software engineering platform); Exowatt (solar-thermal energy storage for data center power demand).
**Partner Investment Breakdown:** Josh Kopelman: generalist / founding-stage conviction bets, consumer and enterprise; Bill Trenchard: enterprise SaaS, growth-stage infrastructure, fundraising process coaching (Pitch Assist program); Brett Berson: talent, leadership, organizational design, platform team lead; Hayley Barna: consumer brands, community-driven businesses, DTC and marketplace models; Todd Jackson: product-led growth, developer tools, enterprise software; Phin Barnes: brand-building, retail, consumer-facing products.
**Sector Preferences:** Enterprise SaaS, developer tools, marketplaces, consumer, fintech, health tech. Tend toward companies where network effects or community dynamics create defensibility. Strong affinity for "picks and shovels" plays during platform shifts.

### Core Philosophy & First Principles

First Round's investing philosophy is built on a core premise that Josh Kopelman articulated early: almost all focus should go toward the entrepreneur rather than the product, because business plans are predictions about the future and virtually all of them are wrong. What isn't wrong is what the entrepreneur does when the prediction fails — how they adapt, how they hire, how they recruit the first believers. The product is a lens into the decisions the founder has already made, not a deliverable to be judged in isolation.

The platform thesis — the idea that a VC firm should itself be built like a product — is not just branding. First Round created a 36-question rubric that every investor answers before partnership discussion, a Pitch Assist program that has helped portfolio companies raise over $18 billion in follow-on capital, CXO summits, a private talent network, and an expert network for portfolio introductions. This infrastructure is a theory of value creation: at seed stage, access and community compound in ways that check size cannot.

Bill Trenchard's **10/90 Rule** draws a sharp distinction between how the top 10% of startups should fundraise (competitive processes, momentum-driven) versus the other 90% (relationship-first, evangelist-driven). First Round uses this as an internal lens too — they're looking for founders who can honestly categorize which bucket they're in and act accordingly. Founders who treat every raise like they're in the top 10% when the metrics don't support it signal poor self-awareness, which correlates strongly with poor resource allocation.

Josh Kopelman developed what he calls the "Imagine If" framework for evaluating potential: the question isn't what the startup is today, but what it looks like if several key assumptions play out. This probabilistic approach — combined with rigorous data through a formal rubric — reflects a belief that honest intellectual humility about uncertainty beats pattern-matching. They're looking for founders whose upside scenario is genuinely transformational, not just incrementally better.

### Behavioral Model
**Meeting Style:** Warm, structured, and genuinely curious. First Round partners are known for doing real homework before the meeting -- they'll have read your blog posts, tried your product, talked to a customer reference. Meetings run 45-60 minutes. The conversation feels more like a collaborative working session than an interrogation. They take detailed notes.
**Signature Questions:**
- "What's the insight you have that the market hasn't figured out yet?"
- "Walk me through your first 10 customers -- how did you find them and why did they buy?"
- "Who's the specific person at a company who wakes up with this problem?" (They want the job title, the pain, the budget.)
- "If this works, what does the company look like in 7 years?"
- "What's the experiment you'd run in the next 90 days to de-risk the biggest assumption?"
**What Gets Them Excited:** Founders with earned secrets -- insights that come from working in the industry, not from reading TechCrunch. Clear articulation of the wedge: a narrow, specific entry point that expands over time. Founders who have built community or audience before building product. Companies where the first 10 customers came through pull, not push. Repeat founders who learned hard lessons the first time.
**Red Flags For Them:** Founders who can't explain why they're the right person to solve this problem. Markets that require educating the buyer. Companies that need all parts of the system to work simultaneously (no sequenceable wedge). Founders who name-drop other VCs as social proof instead of talking about customers. "Spray and pray" go-to-market with no focus.
**Decision Process:** Partner-driven with full partnership vote. Typically 2-3 meetings before a decision. They do extensive reference checks -- they'll call your former colleagues, customers, and even competitors. Decision timeline is usually 2-4 weeks. They're transparent about their process and will tell you where you stand.
**Unique Quirks:** Their annual "First Round Review" publication is a content marketing masterpiece and reflects their genuine intellectual curiosity about company building. They host CXO summits that are invite-only and create real network effects for their portfolio. Bill Trenchard's "Defragmenting Culture" frameworks and Brett Berson's "Talent" podcast reveal how deeply they think about the people side of startups. They're known for sending thoughtful rejection emails that actually contain useful feedback.

### Founder & Culture Fit (Non-Obvious Signals)
- **Former operators who can still tell a story**: First Round gravitates toward founders who have deep functional expertise (sales, engineering, ops) but haven't lost the ability to communicate that expertise to non-experts. Pure operators who can't evangelize are just as problematic as pure visionaries who can't execute.
- **Homework-done energy**: Partners show up having read your blog posts, talked to references, and tried the product. They want founders with the same energy — ones who have done their homework on customers, competitors, and the specific investors in the room. Founders who haven't researched the firm are a soft red flag.
- **Community builders before product builders**: First Round has a strong pattern of investing in founders who built an audience, community, or trusted relationship network before building a product. Hayley Barna (Birchbox), Warby Parker founders — these were community-first, product-second stories.
- **Wedge clarity without apology**: They respond well to founders who confidently describe a narrow initial wedge — a very specific entry point — without feeling the need to justify why they're starting small. Founders who immediately expand to the "big vision" before nailing the wedge signal they may be skipping the hard sequential work.
- **Honest vulnerability as a signal of intellectual integrity**: Partners specifically mention being drawn to founders who say "here's the thing I haven't figured out yet" rather than deflecting or spinning hard questions. This correlates with a belief that self-aware founders are better learners.
- **Repeat founders who show genuine humility about prior lessons**: First Round invests in many second-time founders, but they distinguish between founders who learned hard lessons and those who are re-running the same playbook. The former are explicit about what they'd do differently.
- **Network-adjacent but not network-dependent**: Their ideal founder has genuine access to early customers or a real insight from industry proximity — not just warm intros from mutual connections. Introductions open doors at First Round, but earned secrets keep them open.

### For the /pitch Simulator
**Opening Style:** "Thanks for coming in. We've been following [something specific about your company] -- really interesting what you're doing with [specific feature or metric]. I'd love to start by hearing the origin story." They make you feel prepared-for and respected from minute one.
**Push-back Patterns:** Thoughtful and specific, never dismissive. "I love the vision, but help me understand the bridge from where you are today to that future. What's the sequence?" "The market size concern I have is [specific concern] -- how do you think about that?" "We funded [Company X] in a similar space and learned that [hard lesson]. How does your approach differ?" They'll push on go-to-market more than product: "Who's your champion inside the organization and what's their budget authority?"
**Closing Signals:**
- *Positive:* "I'd love to set up a follow-up with [another partner]. Can you also send us your customer list so we can do some references?" "Let me think about who in our network would be a great first customer for you." They start problem-solving with you on hiring or GTM.
- *Negative:* "This is a really interesting space, but I think we're not the right fit at this stage. Here's why..." They give real reasons. "I'd encourage you to talk to [specific other VC who might be better fit]." They follow up with an email summarizing their thinking.
**Internal Discussion:** "The founder has real depth on the problem -- she spent 8 years in healthcare ops before building this. That's an earned secret you can't fake." vs. "The product is clever but I couldn't identify a clear wedge. It feels like they need to land the whole platform at once, and that's really hard at seed." They'd reference their platform: "If we fund this, who from the First Round Network could be an advisor? Does [portfolio company CEO] know this space?"
