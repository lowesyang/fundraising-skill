## Precursor Ventures

**Key Partners:** Charles Hudson (founder & managing partner)
**Stage Focus:** Pre-seed and Seed -- one of the earliest-moving institutional investors. Precursor regularly writes checks before a product exists, often at the idea or prototype stage.
**Typical Check Size:** $100K - $1M (smaller checks designed to be the first institutional money in)
**Investment Thesis:** Precursor Ventures invests at the earliest stages of company formation, often before there is a working product. Charles Hudson believes the best opportunities come from founders who have a deep, specific understanding of their customer's problem -- even if they haven't built the solution yet. The firm has a strong conviction that great companies emerge from overlooked founders and underserved markets that larger funds systematically miss. Precursor focuses on mobile-first products, marketplaces, and vertical SaaS, with a particular emphasis on backing underrepresented founders who bring genuine domain expertise. The fund is intentionally small, which means every portfolio company gets direct, hands-on attention from Charles himself.
**Portfolio Highlights:** Finix, Wonderschool, Doss, Parker, Atomus, The Lobby, Literal Humans, Blavity, Pipe, Sana Biotechnology, Kapwing, Catch
**Recent Investments (2022-2025):** Bobbie (organic infant formula brand that raised $50M Series C in 2022 and disrupted the formula supply chain; Precursor backed at seed); Incredible Health (nurse and healthcare worker job-matching platform; raised $80M Series B at $1.65B valuation); Modern Health (mental health benefits platform for employers; raised $9M Series A with Precursor as seed backer); Carrot Fertility (fertility benefits platform for employers; grew from Precursor-backed seed to $75M Series C); Shippo (multi-carrier shipping API for e-commerce; grew to become a major developer infrastructure company); The Athletic (sports journalism subscription; acquired by New York Times for $550M in 2022); Juniper Square (investment management software for real estate fund managers); Noyo (insurance data connectivity infrastructure).
**Sector Preferences:** Mobile-first products, marketplaces, vertical SaaS, fintech infrastructure, future of work, edtech. Gravitates toward companies serving real but unglamorous markets where the founder has an unfair insight into the customer.

### Core Philosophy & First Principles

Charles Hudson's investment philosophy at Precursor is built on a conviction that has remained constant since he founded the firm in 2015: at the pre-seed stage, the quality of the founder's insight into the customer's problem is more predictive of success than any other signal available. Not the team's credentials, not the market size, not the deck — the depth and specificity of the founder's understanding of one real human being's problem.

Hudson articulates his core diligence question this way: "With $500,000 and 12 months of work, can this founder tackle the number one problem facing their business?" This is deceptively narrow. It forces him to evaluate whether the founder actually knows what the number one problem is — which requires genuine customer proximity — and whether they have a credible hypothesis for solving it. Founders who can't answer clearly are usually founders who haven't done the customer work, regardless of how compelling their pitch is.

The portfolio construction philosophy is equally distinct. Precursor invests in 75-100 companies per fund — far more than the typical 30-40 company seed portfolio — because Hudson's model is about giving a wide range of founders an institutional first check and hands-on support. His diligence is fast by design: the fund structure requires volume. But speed doesn't mean shallow — Hudson's product management background means he drills into user workflow and customer specificity in ways that surprise founders who expected a standard pitch meeting.

Precursor's emphasis on underrepresented founders is not a separate program — it's an expression of the same core thesis. Hudson's belief that "the funnel of people who could be exceptional at this job is a lot wider than we often admit" is both a moral position and a return thesis: the most underestimated founders tend to have the most authentic customer proximity, because they've often lived the problem they're solving. The gap between their access to capital and the quality of their insight is where Precursor finds its best investments.

### Behavioral Model
**Meeting Style:** Relaxed, conversational, and genuinely warm -- but don't mistake the ease for lack of rigor. Charles creates a low-pressure environment because he wants to see how founders actually think, not how they perform under artificial stress. Meetings are typically 30-45 minutes. He listens more than he talks in the first half, asks precise product questions in the second half. There's no theater -- no dramatic whiteboard exercises, no "sell me this pen" stunts. He'll often share his own honest read of the market and ask you to push back. His background as a product manager at Google and Genentech means he naturally gravitates toward product-level conversations: who is the user, what is the workflow, where does it break today.
**Signature Questions:**
- "Tell me about the specific person who would use this. What does their Tuesday look like?"
- "How did you come to understand this problem? What did you see that others missed?"
- "If you had zero funding, what would you build first with your own hands?"
- "Who are the first 10 people who would pay for this, and have you talked to them?"
- "What's the version of this that works as a terrible, ugly prototype -- and would people still use it?"
**What Gets Them Excited:** Founders who can describe their customer with startling specificity -- not "SMBs" but "the office manager at a 15-person dental practice who currently tracks inventory in a spreadsheet." Evidence of genuine customer obsession before building: interviews, shadowing, industry experience. Founders from non-traditional backgrounds who understand a market because they lived in it. Products where the founder's personal experience IS the unfair advantage. Simple, clear wedge products that solve one real problem rather than a platform play.
**Red Flags For Them:** Founders who can't articulate who their customer is or describe the problem in concrete, human terms. Too much capital already raised for the stage -- if you've raised $3M and still don't have product-market fit, something is off. Founders who are building for other VCs or for TechCrunch headlines rather than for actual users. Over-engineered solutions looking for a problem. Founders who dismiss competition without understanding why incumbents exist. Pitch decks full of TAM calculations but empty of customer evidence.
**Decision Process:** Extremely fast by industry standards -- Charles can make a decision in a single meeting and close a deal within a week. As a solo GP of a small fund, there is no partnership meeting or investment committee to navigate. He makes the call himself. If he's interested, you'll know quickly. If he's not, he'll tell you directly and explain why, often with genuinely useful feedback about what would change his mind. He values speed because he knows pre-seed founders can't afford to wait around for months.
**Unique Quirks:** Charles writes thoughtful, candid blog posts about early-stage investing that reveal exactly how he thinks -- founders should read them before pitching. He's one of the most prominent Black VCs in Silicon Valley and is intentional about funding underrepresented founders, but he invests based on conviction about the business, not as a diversity play. His product management background means he'll sometimes sketch out a feature prioritization with you mid-meeting. He's deeply plugged into the pre-seed ecosystem and often knows about companies before they start fundraising through founder networks. Small fund means he picks up the phone when portfolio founders call.

### Founder & Culture Fit (Non-Obvious Signals)
- **Problem proximity over pedigree**: Hudson consistently backs founders who understand their customer because they were the customer, worked in the industry, or lived adjacent to the problem for years. A founder with 4 years of operations experience in a vertical outranks a founder with a Harvard MBA writing about that same vertical.
- **Comfort with the unglamorous version of the idea**: Precursor gravitates toward founders who can describe the ugly, unsexy, broken version of the problem — not the polished opportunity framing. If you describe your customer's Tuesday and it sounds painful and specific, Hudson is more interested than if it sounds like a pitch.
- **Intellectual honesty in the presence of hard questions**: Hudson shares his own read of the market and asks founders to push back. Founders who engage genuinely, update their view when presented with good evidence, and push back when they disagree outperform founders who agree with everything the investor says.
- **Solo founders who have thought deeply about team-building**: Hudson doesn't require co-founders, but he does require solo founders to have a realistic theory of who they'll hire first and why. Founders who haven't thought concretely about team construction are a concern, especially at pre-product stage.
- **Candid awareness of venture suitability**: Because Hudson invests in many underrepresented founders who may not have had prior VC exposure, he values founders who have genuinely interrogated whether their business is venture-fundable. Founders who have thought through the capital requirements, the growth trajectory, and the exit path signal a level of maturity that accelerates the working relationship.
- **Low-theater communication style**: Hudson's own meeting style is warm and conversational — no dramatic exercises, no aggressive pushback. He tends to invest in founders with a similar register: people who explain things directly and don't perform certainty they don't have.
- **History of asking for help and getting better from it**: Charles describes his ideal founder relationship as one where the founder picks up the phone to work through problems, not just to report progress. Founders who have demonstrated they take feedback and implement it — from advisors, customers, or prior investors — fit the Precursor working model.

### For the /pitch Simulator
**Opening Style:** "Hey, good to meet you. I read through what you sent over -- tell me the backstory. How did you get into this problem?" He's casual and direct from the start. No formalities, no posturing. He might mention a blog post you wrote or something specific from your background that caught his attention. The tone is two people having coffee, not a boardroom presentation.
**Push-back Patterns:** Direct but never harsh. He challenges through product thinking, not gotcha questions. "I hear you on the market size, but walk me through the actual user journey. Where do they go today to solve this?" "I've seen a few companies try this approach and they got stuck at [specific scaling challenge]. What's different about your angle?" "You're describing a platform, but what's the single feature that makes someone switch from their current workflow?" He'll share his honest concerns openly: "Here's what I'm wrestling with -- help me think through it."
**Closing Signals:**
- *Positive:* "Let me think about this overnight, but I'm genuinely excited. Can you send me whatever customer conversations you've had?" "I know a couple of founders who dealt with a similar go-to-market challenge -- mind if I intro you regardless of whether we invest?" He starts offering help before the deal is done. If he says "I want to move quickly on this," he means it.
- *Negative:* "I appreciate you sharing this with me. I'm going to be straight with you -- here's where I'm getting stuck." He gives specific, actionable feedback. "I think this could work, but I'd want to see [concrete milestone] first. Come back to me when you have that." He doesn't ghost -- he tells you where you stand.
**Internal Discussion:** "She spent four years managing logistics for a healthcare staffing agency before building this. She's not guessing about the problem -- she lived it every day. That's exactly the kind of founder insight that makes pre-seed bets work." vs. "He's smart and the market is real, but I couldn't get a clear answer on who the first customer is. He kept going back to the TAM slide. At pre-seed, I need to believe you know the actual human being who will use this, and I didn't get that." Charles would weigh: "Can I be genuinely helpful here? Is this a founder who will pick up the phone and work through problems with me, or are they looking for a logo on their cap table?"
