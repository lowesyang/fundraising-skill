## Lightspeed Venture Partners

**Key Partners:** Ravi Mhatre, Barry Eggers, Arif Janmohamed, Mercedes Bent, Guru Chahal, Gaurav Gupta, Alex Kayyal, Anoushka Vaswani, Michael Mignano, Moritz Baier-Lentz

**Stage Focus:** Seed through Growth. Multi-stage global firm with US, Europe, India, Israel, and Southeast Asia offices.
**Typical Check Size:** Series A: $10M-$25M, Series B: $25M-$75M (large growth fund enables larger checks)
**Investment Thesis:** Lightspeed is thesis-driven across a broad range of sectors, with dedicated partners covering enterprise, consumer, health, fintech, crypto, and gaming. Their core belief is that the best investments come from deep sector expertise paired with genuine founder-partner chemistry. They've built separate practice areas with dedicated funds (Lightspeed India, Lightspeed Europe) that function with local autonomy but share global deal flow and pattern recognition. They believe in the power of founder-led companies and back founders who have an "earned insight" -- typically from years of working in the problem space. The firm has increasingly invested in AI infrastructure and applications, gaming/interactive entertainment, and vertical SaaS.

**Sector Preferences:** Enterprise SaaS, consumer, fintech, health-tech, gaming/entertainment, crypto, AI/ML, cybersecurity. They have one of the broadest sector coverage models among top-tier firms, with dedicated partners for each vertical. Strong in developer tools and infrastructure through Arif Janmohamed, and consumer/social through Michael Mignano.

### Core Philosophy & First Principles

Lightspeed's animating conviction is that the best investments come from depth — depth of commitment, depth of relationships, and depth of belief. The firm explicitly distinguishes itself from "speculators and opportunists" who deploy capital into sectors based on headlines and exits from adjacent investments. In their articulation, Lightspeed creates "together" with founders rather than funding from afar.

The "earned insight" thesis is the firm's most operationally distinctive concept. Lightspeed actively looks for founders who have spent years — typically a full career — working inside the problem they are now solving. A founder who spent seven years in hospital operations before building clinical AI software has earned their insight; a founder who read about the opportunity three months ago has not. This distinction shapes how Lightspeed structures its practice areas: by hiring partners who themselves spent decades in enterprise software, consumer, fintech, gaming, and health before crossing into investing, they ensure that diligence conversations are between practitioners rather than between investor and pitch.

Co-founder Ravi Mhatre has framed AI as the era-defining technology of this generation — one that is "reshaping industries even faster than the platform and architecture shifts observed during the emergence of the internet, mobile phones, and cloud computing." The firm has deployed over $5.5 billion into 165+ AI-native companies, including anchor positions in Anthropic (leading the $3.5B Series E), Mistral, Glean, and Databricks. This is not trend-chasing: Lightspeed made its first AI investment in 2012 and has built the sector expertise needed to distinguish durable AI businesses from feature-layer opportunists.

Lightspeed's global platform — US, Europe, India, Southeast Asia — enables a distinctive version of the earned-insight thesis to operate across geographies: local partners in each region possess the same depth of market knowledge in their territory that US partners have in Silicon Valley, which allows the firm to back "category definers" before they are visible to global investors who lack the local network.

### Behavioral Model
**Meeting Style:** Energetic, relationship-oriented, and substantive. Lightspeed partners are known for building genuine relationships with founders, often meeting multiple times before any deal discussion. Meetings run 45-60 minutes and feel more conversational than interrogative. Partners are well-prepared but lead with curiosity rather than a testing framework. They're genuinely warm -- the firm's culture emphasizes humility and partnership over star-partner dynamics.
**Signature Questions:**
- "What's your earned insight here? What do you know from experience that gives you an edge?"
- "Describe your ideal customer in vivid detail. What's their day look like before and after your product?"
- "How does the business model evolve? What does the revenue mix look like in year 5 vs. year 1?"
- "What's the biggest risk to this business that keeps you up at night?"
- "If a well-funded competitor copies your product feature-for-feature, what happens?"
**What Gets Them Excited:** Founders with deep, personal experience in the problem domain ("earned insight"). Products that fundamentally change the workflow for a specific persona, not just improve it incrementally. Businesses with multiple revenue streams or expansion paths beyond the initial wedge. Global opportunities where Lightspeed's multi-geography presence is an advantage. Gaming and entertainment companies with strong engagement mechanics. Enterprise products with bottom-up adoption and expansion revenue dynamics.
**Red Flags For Them:** Founders who are building in a space they recently "discovered" without deep domain expertise. One-dimensional business models without clear expansion paths. Companies in sectors without a dedicated Lightspeed practice area (they want internal expertise to support the investment). Founders who are dismissive of competition. Companies where the unit economics require massive scale to work. Teams that are fundraising-focused rather than product-focused.
**Decision Process:** Partner-led with investment committee approval. The sponsoring partner builds conviction through multiple meetings, customer diligence, and technical assessment. They're known for being responsive and transparent about their process. Decision timeline: 2-4 weeks for active deals. Each practice area has its own decision rhythm, and cross-sector deals may involve multiple practice leads.
**Unique Quirks:** Their sector-organized practice structure means you're often meeting with the partner who has the deepest expertise in your specific vertical -- they'll know your competitors, your customers' pain points, and the technical landscape in granular detail. The Lightspeed Scout program gives early access to pre-seed founders. Their annual Summer Party is a legendary networking event. They've been consistently early in gaming/entertainment investing (Epic Games, Overwolf). The firm deliberately keeps a lower media profile than peers like a16z or Sequoia, preferring to let portfolio companies be the story. Partners frequently co-invest with angels and seed funds they trust, creating a strong inbound referral network.

### Founder & Culture Fit (Non-Obvious Signals)

- The "earned insight" filter is non-negotiable: Lightspeed listens for whether a founder's domain expertise is lived or learned. Founders who can cite specific operational failures they personally experienced — and how those failures shaped their product decisions — carry far more weight than those who reference industry reports or customer interviews.
- Intellectual perseverance matters more than pedigree. Ravi Mhatre has emphasized "the ability to believe in yourself and not get discouraged easily" as a primary founder characteristic. Lightspeed has backed founders who were turned down by many investors and who iterated through multiple unsuccessful versions before finding product-market fit.
- Relational warmth is a genuine signal, not just politeness. The firm's culture prizes authentic founder-partner chemistry over transactional efficiency; founders who engage partners as people rather than capital sources tend to build stronger relationships that survive board tension.
- Founders who are obsessed with their specific customer persona — who can describe a particular user's daily life in granular detail before they ever raise a metric — resonate strongly. Lightspeed's signature question about a customer's day "before and after" your product is also a culture test.
- Humility about what the founder does not yet know, especially about go-to-market, is seen as a sign of self-awareness rather than weakness. Founders who have all the answers are harder to partner with; founders who know where they need to grow are easier to back and build alongside.
- Operators-turned-founders with functional expertise in the problem (logistics, healthcare, finance) who then built technical skills — rather than purely technical founders who discovered a domain — are slightly over-indexed in Lightspeed's portfolio.
- Founders who think about expansion as an organic consequence of product success rather than a capital deployment event tend to align with Lightspeed's long-term orientation. "What does the revenue mix look like in year 5?" is not just a diligence question — it screens for multi-horizon thinking.

### For the /pitch Simulator
**Opening Style:** "I've been looking forward to this -- [mutual connection] had great things to say about what you're building. Tell me the story. How did you come to this problem?" They lead with warmth and genuine interest in the founder's journey. Or: "I cover [sector] for us and I've been tracking this space closely. Your approach is different from what I've seen -- I want to understand why."
**Push-back Patterns:** Direct but respectful, and often framed as collaborative problem-solving. "I love the vision, but the path from here to there has a scary middle section. How do you cross the chasm from early adopters to mainstream?" "Your biggest competitor just raised $100M. What's your strategy to win despite being outspent?" "The business model today is services-heavy. What's the unlock that makes this a software margin business?" They'll stress-test assumptions: "You're assuming 120% net revenue retention. That's top-decile. What evidence do you have that this sustains as you move down-market?" They push back with empathy, not aggression.
**Closing Signals:**
- *Positive:* "I'd love for you to meet [practice lead partner]. I think there's a lot of alignment." "Can I spend a day with one of your customer-facing teams? I want to see how the product works in the real environment." They start discussing portfolio synergies: "You should meet [portfolio company CEO] -- they could be a customer and a reference." They ask about timeline and competing term sheets.
- *Negative:* "I think you're onto something, but we'd need to see [specific milestone] before we'd have conviction. I don't want to waste your time by stringing this out." "This isn't a fit for our [practice area] thesis right now, but let me introduce you to [other VC] who I think would be a better partner for this stage." They're straightforward and often offer warm intros to other firms when they pass.
**Internal Discussion:** "The founder spent 7 years running supply chain ops at [Fortune 500] before building this. The earned insight is real -- every customer I called confirmed the pain point and said this product is the first one that actually works. The expansion revenue potential is enormous." vs. "Good product, but the founder is a second-time founder in a space she doesn't know deeply. I'm not seeing the earned insight. The competitive landscape is also getting crowded -- three well-funded companies launched in the last 6 months." They'd discuss sector practice fit: "Does [practice lead] want to spend the next 10 years in this category? That's the real question."
