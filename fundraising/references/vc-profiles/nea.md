## New Enterprise Associates (NEA)

**Key Partners:** Scott Sandell (chairman), Tony Florence, Ravi Viswanathan, Liza Landsman, Vanessa Larco, Ali Partovi (venture partner), Mohamad Makhzoumi, Andrew Schoen, Hillary Mickell
**Stage Focus:** All stages -- seed through growth. NEA is one of the few firms large enough to invest at every stage and follow on through subsequent rounds. They write seed checks, lead Series A and B rounds, and participate in growth financings. This breadth means a founder who partners with NEA at seed can potentially have them as an investor through IPO.
**Typical Check Size:** $1M - $100M (seed checks at the low end, growth checks at the high end, with Series A/B typically in the $10M-$50M range)
**Investment Thesis:** NEA looks for category-defining companies with massive total addressable markets and exceptional founding teams. Their thesis is built on the belief that the biggest returns come from companies that create or redefine entire categories rather than incrementally improving existing ones. With ~$25B under management across technology and healthcare, NEA has the scale to be a patient, long-term partner -- they don't need quick exits and can support companies through multiple rounds over many years. Their cross-stage capability means they evaluate companies with a full-lifecycle lens: they ask at seed what the Series A story will look like, and at Series A what the public company narrative could be. NEA invests across both technology and healthcare, and partners have deep domain expertise in specific verticals, allowing them to match founders with investors who genuinely understand their market.
**Portfolio Highlights:** Salesforce, Workday, Cloudflare, Databricks, Robinhood, Plaid, MasterClass, Olo, Tempus AI, Forcepoint, Coursera, Jet.com, Groupon, Uber (early)
**Sector Preferences:** Enterprise software/SaaS, AI and machine learning, fintech, consumer internet, healthcare/biotech, cybersecurity, edtech, data infrastructure. NEA is one of the few firms with a dedicated healthcare practice alongside its technology practice. In AI, they've been very active -- early in Databricks, invested in Tempus AI, and are deploying heavily into AI enterprise applications and infrastructure. They don't chase narrow trends; they look for large, durable categories.

### Behavioral Model
**Meeting Style:** Professional, structured, and thorough. NEA has a large team, so the first meeting is typically with a partner or principal who has domain expertise in your sector. They're well-prepared -- they'll have researched your space and come with informed questions. The conversation is collegial and intellectually curious. They want to understand your category deeply, not just your metrics. Expect them to ask about market structure, competitive dynamics, and your wedge strategy. Unlike speed-focused firms, NEA takes time to build conviction -- you'll meet multiple people on the team, and each will bring a different perspective (sector expertise, operational experience, go-to-market knowledge). The meetings feel more like strategic discussions than interrogations. NEA partners are respectful and engaged; they won't rush you through a 30-minute Zoom call.
**Signature Questions:**
- "What's your category and how big is it? Are you creating a new category or redefining an existing one?"
- "What's the wedge strategy -- how do you get your first 100 customers, and how does the product expand from there?"
- "How does your model compound over time? What gets better as you scale?"
- "What's the competitive landscape? Who else is building here and what's your differentiated advantage?"
- "What does the board look like and how can we help? What do you need from your next investor beyond capital?"
**What Gets Them Excited:** Category-defining potential -- companies that are creating or reshaping an entire market. Massive TAMs ($10B+) where the company has a credible path to significant market share. Strong founding teams with domain expertise and a clear vision for the future. Compounding business models where retention, data, or network effects create durable competitive advantages. AI applications with clear enterprise value and defensible data moats. Healthcare companies leveraging technology to transform clinical workflows or drug discovery. Companies where NEA's cross-stage capability and sector expertise can genuinely add value. Wedge strategies that start narrow and expand -- "land and expand" motions in enterprise, or viral loops in consumer.
**Red Flags For Them:** Companies without a clear category -- if you can't articulate what space you're defining, NEA will struggle to build conviction. Founders who can't articulate competitive positioning or dismiss competitors without substance. Markets that are too small for their fund size (NEA needs to deploy large amounts of capital, so a $500M TAM won't work for a Series A investment). Commodity businesses without defensible advantages. Founders who only want capital and don't want a collaborative board partner (NEA takes board seats and expects to be engaged). Unclear go-to-market strategy -- great technology without a path to distribution.
**Decision Process:** Structured and thorough, typically 3-4 weeks from first meeting to term sheet. The process involves multiple partner meetings -- you'll meet the lead partner, then one or two additional partners for cross-pollination. The deal goes through a partnership vote where the lead partner champions the investment and the broader partnership pressure-tests the thesis. NEA's large partnership means the bar is high -- multiple experienced investors need to believe in the opportunity. This process is slower than Tiger Global or DST, but it means that when NEA commits, the full partnership is behind you. Diligence includes customer calls, market analysis, competitive mapping, and financial model review. They're thorough but respectful of founder time.
**Unique Quirks:** NEA is one of the oldest VC firms in existence (founded 1977), giving it an institutional memory that few firms can match -- they've seen multiple technology cycles and can pattern-match across decades. Their dual technology/healthcare practice is unusual; a healthcare-AI company can get both a tech investor and a healthcare investor from the same firm. NEA's size (~$25B AUM) means they can write follow-on checks through every subsequent round without needing to bring in new investors, providing continuity and reducing fundraising complexity for founders. Ali Partovi, venture partner and co-founder of Neo (a university founder community), gives NEA unique deal flow from top university founders. The firm has a reputation for being "the steady hand" -- not the flashiest brand, but deeply reliable, well-resourced, and consistently in top-tier deals across cycles. They've returned capital through both boom and bust periods, which gives them credibility with founders who value stability over hype.

### For the /pitch Simulator
**Opening Style:** Warm, professional, and domain-aware. "We've been spending a lot of time in [your sector] and your company caught our attention. I'd love to start by understanding how you think about the category you're building in." Or: "Tell me the founding story -- what did you see that others missed, and how did that lead to building this?" NEA partners lead with intellectual curiosity about the market and the founder's insight. They're genuinely interested in understanding the problem space before diving into metrics. The opening feels like the start of a strategic conversation, not an interrogation.
**Push-back Patterns:** Thoughtful, structured, and competitive-landscape focused. "I see three other well-funded companies going after this space -- [Company A] just raised $50M and [Company B] has a strong enterprise motion. Walk me through why you win." "The TAM you're quoting is the top-down number. What's the bottom-up TAM based on your current customer profile and pricing? I want to make sure the math works for our fund." "Your wedge is compelling, but the expansion story is what matters. How do you go from a $50K ACV to a $500K ACV with existing customers?" "I love the technology, but the go-to-market feels early. Who's your ICP and what does the sales motion look like at scale?" NEA push-back is never dismissive -- it's designed to stress-test the thesis because the partner needs to defend it in the partnership meeting. They'll often say: "I need to be able to answer this question when my partners ask."
**Closing Signals:**
- *Positive:* "I'd like to bring you in to meet [another partner name]. They cover [related sector] and I think they'd add a lot to the conversation." Scheduling additional partner meetings is a strong positive signal -- it means the lead partner is building internal consensus. "Let me walk you through what a term sheet from us would look like and how we think about board construction." "What's your timeline? We'd like to be in a position to move within the next couple of weeks." They'll ask about your investor preferences and what you're looking for beyond capital.
- *Negative:* "This is a really interesting company, but I'm not sure we're the right partner at this stage. The market feels early for the kind of check we'd need to write. Let's reconnect when you're further along." "I think you've built something strong, but the competitive dynamics in this space make it hard for us to build conviction right now. If you can show us [specific metric or milestone], we'd love to revisit." They're gracious and specific -- they tell you exactly what would need to change for them to invest. They rarely ghost; they give clear feedback.
**Internal Discussion:** "This is a category-creation opportunity. The founder has deep domain expertise, the wedge strategy is smart -- they're landing with [specific use case] and expanding into [broader platform play]. TAM is $15B+ bottom-up, and they have early enterprise traction with strong NPS. I think this could be a Workday-scale outcome in [sector]. The competitive landscape is crowded, but they have the best product and the strongest team." vs. "I like the founder and the technology is impressive, but I can't get comfortable with the go-to-market. They're selling to mid-market today and the ACV is $30K. For this to be a fund-returner, they need to move upmarket aggressively, and I don't see evidence they can sell to F500 yet. I'd want to see three enterprise logos before we lead." Or: "The market is interesting but it's a $2B TAM. Even if they capture 20%, that's a $400M revenue company. At this valuation, the math doesn't work for Fund XVII. Pass, but let's track -- if the market expands, this could get interesting."
