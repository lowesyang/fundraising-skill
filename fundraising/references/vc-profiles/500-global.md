## 500 Global

**Key Partners:** Christine Tsai (CEO, co-founder), Clayton Bryan, Courtney Powell, Bedy Yang, Khailee Ng
**Stage Focus:** Pre-seed and Seed — one of the most prolific seed investors globally. They operate at scale that most seed funds can't match, investing in 100+ companies per year through accelerator batches and direct investments across 80+ countries.
**Typical Check Size:** $50K - $500K (standard $150K for accelerator program companies, larger checks for direct investments up to $500K)
**Investment Thesis:** 500 Global (formerly 500 Startups, founded by Dave McClure and Christine Tsai) believes great founders exist everywhere, not just Silicon Valley. Their thesis is high-volume, globally distributed seed investing. They back founders who understand their local markets deeply and can build companies that scale regionally or globally. They are contrarian in the most literal sense — while most US VCs cluster in SF/NYC, 500 Global has built investment infrastructure in Southeast Asia, Latin America, the Middle East, Turkey, Japan, and Korea. They believe the next generation of billion-dollar companies will come from markets that traditional VCs ignore, and they want to be the first institutional check in those companies.
**Portfolio Highlights:** Twilio (seed), Credit Karma (seed), Canva (early), GitLab, Grab, Bukalapak, Talkdesk, Udemy, Calm, Hims, Intercom (early)
**Sector Preferences:** Broadly sector-agnostic but particularly strong in fintech, e-commerce/marketplaces, SaaS, and consumer internet — especially where these categories intersect with emerging market dynamics. They look for business models that work in specific geographies and have potential to expand. They have deep conviction in mobile-first models and companies serving underbanked or underserved populations.

### Behavioral Model
**Meeting Style:** Fast-paced, efficient, and no-nonsense. 500 Global partners evaluate a massive volume of founders, so meetings tend to be shorter and more structured than at smaller funds. Expect 20-30 minute slots. They are friendly and direct — no performative intellectualism. The vibe is more "startup operator who's seen 10,000 pitches" than "finance person evaluating a deal." They evaluate founders on hustle and market opportunity, not pedigree. If you went to a no-name school but have traction in Lagos or Jakarta, that's more interesting to them than a Stanford MBA with a slide deck.
**Signature Questions:**
- "What's your traction in your home market?" (they want to see local product-market fit before global ambitions)
- "How does this translate globally?" (they invest for international scale — show them the expansion path)
- "How fast can you get to 1,000 users?" (velocity matters — they want to see urgency and a clear acquisition playbook)
- "What's your unfair advantage in this geography?" (local knowledge, regulatory moats, distribution partnerships — what do you have that a Silicon Valley clone doesn't?)
- "How capital-efficient can you be?" (at $50K-$500K check sizes, they need founders who can do a lot with a little)
**What Gets Them Excited:** Founders with deep local market knowledge building for underserved markets. Traction in geographies that other VCs don't understand. Capital-efficient businesses that are already generating revenue or have clear paths to revenue on small budgets. Repeat founders from emerging markets. Companies that have cracked distribution in their home market and have a believable story for regional expansion. Founders who are building for markets of hundreds of millions of people that Western VCs simply don't cover.
**Red Flags For Them:** US-only thinking — founders who can't articulate why their product matters outside Silicon Valley. Founders who need too much hand-holding or expect white-glove VC service from a high-volume fund. Companies that only work in one geography with no expansion path. Burn rates that are too high for a seed-stage company. Founders who are chasing trends without understanding their local market dynamics. Over-reliance on a single channel or customer for growth.
**Decision Process:** Very fast for accelerator batches — acceptance decisions come quickly during application windows, and the process is standardized. For direct investments outside the accelerator, decisions typically take 1-2 weeks. The partnership evaluates deals efficiently given the volume. They use standardized investment instruments (SAFEs) to minimize legal friction. They don't spend weeks on diligence at seed — the check size doesn't justify it. They'd rather invest fast, take the risk, and let the portfolio play out at scale.
**Unique Quirks:** 500 Global runs accelerator programs globally — similar to YC but far more internationally distributed. Their alumni network of 2,700+ companies across 80+ countries is one of the largest in venture capital. They have a strong operator network in Southeast Asia, Latin America, and MENA that few other funds can match. They've been through significant leadership transitions (Dave McClure's departure) and have emerged as a more institutionalized, globally-focused fund under Christine Tsai's leadership. They also run thematic funds (e.g., 500 Durians for Southeast Asia, 500 Luchadores for Latin America) with dedicated regional partners.

### For the /pitch Simulator
**Opening Style:** Straightforward and time-conscious. "Thanks for coming in — we see a lot of companies, so let's make the most of our time. Give me the 2-minute version: what do you do, who's it for, and what's working?" They won't spend 10 minutes on pleasantries. If you're pitching from an emerging market, they'll perk up immediately — that's their sweet spot. They might ask where you're based before you even start your pitch, because geography is a key signal for them.
**Push-back Patterns:** They push hard on scalability and geographic expansion. "This works in Nigeria — how do you know it'll work in Kenya?" or "Your unit economics depend on labor costs that will change as you scale — how do you handle that?" They'll also challenge capital efficiency: "You're raising $500K but your burn plan looks like a Series A company — can you do this with half?" They question founders who seem overly focused on the US market: "Why are you pitching us? You sound like you should be talking to a US-only fund." Their pushback is practical and operational, not theoretical.
**Closing Signals:**
- *Positive:* "Let me connect you with our regional partner in [your geography]." "Have you applied to our accelerator batch? I think you'd be a strong fit." "We'd like to move forward — let me send you our SAFE terms." Quick follow-up email (within 48 hours) with specific next steps or intro to another partner. Asking about your cap table and existing investors.
- *Negative:* "This is interesting but I think you need more local traction before we can commit." "Come back when you've proven this in your home market." "We're not sure this translates beyond [single geography]." Silence for more than a week after the meeting. Suggesting you apply to a future accelerator batch instead of taking a direct investment now.
**Internal Discussion:** *Bull case:* "This founder has 5,000 users in Indonesia with essentially zero marketing spend. The product is localized perfectly — competitors from the US can't touch this. The check is $150K, the market is 270 million people, and this founder clearly understands distribution in a way that no Silicon Valley team ever will. This is exactly why we exist. Let's fund them and connect them with our SEA network." *Bear case:* "Interesting product but this founder seems fixated on the US market and is only talking to us because they got passed on by Tier 1 US funds. The traction is thin — 200 users in 6 months with no clear acquisition channel. And the burn rate assumes $500K which is too much for what they've shown. Let's pass and revisit if they can show real local traction."
