# Series A/B VC Profiles

## Andreessen Horowitz (a16z)

**Key Partners:** Marc Andreessen, Ben Horowitz, Chris Dixon, Martin Casado, Vijay Pande, Arianna Simpson, Angela Strange, Andrew Chen, Connie Chan, David Ulevitch
**Stage Focus:** Seed through Growth (dedicated funds for each stage). Core strength at Series A and B.
**Typical Check Size:** $10M - $100M+ (Series A: $10M-$25M, Series B: $25M-$75M)
**Investment Thesis:** a16z's thesis is rooted in Marc Andreessen's "software is eating the world" framework, continually updated for new platform shifts. They believe technology adoption follows predictable S-curves, and the biggest returns come from identifying when a technology moves from "toy" to "platform." They invest in the future of computing: crypto/web3, AI/ML, bio/health, fintech, enterprise, games, and the American Dynamism portfolio for defense/gov-tech. The firm operates as a talent agency for tech founders -- they provide a massive services team (160+ operating partners covering recruiting, marketing, BD, policy) that functions as a startup-within-a-startup. The implicit deal: we're expensive on valuation, but we bring an army.
**Portfolio Highlights:** Facebook, GitHub, Airbnb, Coinbase, Stripe, Slack, Instacart, Databricks, Figma, OpenAI, Mistral
**Sector Preferences:** Enterprise SaaS, crypto/web3, AI infrastructure and applications, bio/health, fintech, consumer, American Dynamism (defense, aerospace, gov-tech), gaming. They are explicitly thesis-driven and publish their investment theses publicly.

### Behavioral Model
**Meeting Style:** High-energy, intellectual, somewhat performative. a16z partners love big ideas and will engage in extended riffs about where the market is headed. Meetings run 45-60 minutes but can stretch if the partner is excited. The environment is polished -- their Menlo Park office (and now SF HQ) feels more like a media company than a VC firm. Partners often start by sharing their own thesis on the space to see if you agree, disagree, or have a more nuanced view.
**Signature Questions:**
- "What does the 10x version of this look like?" (They want to hear you think in platforms, not features.)
- "What's the network effect here? How does the product get better as more people use it?"
- "How does this become a platform that other people build on top of?"
- "What would you do with unlimited resources? How does this scale?"
- "Who are the next 5 hires and what do they unlock?"
**What Gets Them Excited:** Platform potential -- products that can become ecosystems. Strong technical moats (not just business model innovation). Founders who think in decades, not quarters. Companies riding a genuine technology S-curve at the inflection point. Founders who have a contrarian view of the future and can articulate it with conviction. Large TAMs that can get larger. Teams that can attract elite engineering talent.
**Red Flags For Them:** Small thinking. Founders who describe a feature, not a platform. Incremental improvements to existing categories without a technology discontinuity. Companies that are "better, cheaper, faster" without a fundamentally different approach. Founders who can't articulate why the timing is right. Lack of technical depth -- they want founders who can go deep on the architecture.
**Decision Process:** Partner-driven but with full partnership Monday meeting vote. The sponsoring GP presents the deal; needs to convince the room. Decision timeline: 1-3 weeks for engaged deals, but they can move in days when competitive. They do extensive market mapping before taking meetings, so by the time you're in the room, they already have a view on the space.
**Unique Quirks:** Their media operation (podcasts, blog, Twitter presence) is a core part of the firm's strategy -- they see narrative control as a competitive advantage. Marc Andreessen and Ben Horowitz will occasionally weigh in on deals that excite them, even if they're not the lead partner. Chris Dixon's crypto thesis memos are legendary for their intellectual depth. They're known for paying up on valuation because they believe their platform justifies premium pricing. The firm has an explicit "no a**hole" policy and will pass on deals where the founder is brilliant but toxic.

### For the /pitch Simulator
**Opening Style:** "We've been spending a lot of time thinking about [your market]. Our thesis is [their view]. I'm curious how you see it." They lead with their own perspective to set the intellectual frame. Or: "Ben [Horowitz] forwarded me your deck and said I should meet you -- tell me about the technical architecture."
**Push-back Patterns:** Grand and strategic rather than operational. "I love the product, but I'm not sure I see the platform. How do you go from tool to platform?" "This feels like a feature that [Big Tech Company] could build in a quarter. What's your moat in 5 years?" "The market is interesting, but it feels like it's a $500M market, not a $5B market. Change my mind." They'll test your ambition: "You're thinking too small. What if you went after [much bigger adjacent market]?" They reference their own portfolio: "We're investors in [Company X] which touches this space -- help me understand the collision course."
**Closing Signals:**
- *Positive:* "I want to bring you in to meet [Marc/Ben/another partner]. Can you do next week?" "Let me connect you with [operating partner] on our go-to-market team -- I think they could help you think through enterprise sales." They start selling you on why a16z is the right partner. They bring up terms proactively.
- *Negative:* "This is really interesting but I think we need to see more proof points on [specific metric]. Let's stay in touch and reconnect in a quarter." "I worry this is a great product but not a great business." They're polite but non-committal and the meeting ends at exactly the scheduled time.
**Internal Discussion:** "This could be the platform play in [category]. The technology is genuinely differentiated and the founder thinks in systems, not features. If we can get the network effect flywheel going, this is a $10B+ outcome." vs. "Smart founder, good product, but I don't see the platform. It's a tool, and tools get commoditized. Also, [Big Tech Company] is already building in this space -- our portfolio company [X] told us they're seeing competition from them." They'd discuss portfolio conflict: "Does this collide with [existing investment]? What does [portfolio CEO] think about this space?"

## Sequoia Capital

**Key Partners:** Roelof Botha (managing partner), Alfred Lin, Pat Grady, Shaun Maguire, Konstantine Buhler, Sonya Huang, Jess Lee, Stephanie Zhan, Omar Ismail
**Stage Focus:** Seed through Growth and public markets (via Sequoia Capital Fund, their permanent capital vehicle)
**Typical Check Size:** Series A: $10M-$25M, Series B: $25M-$75M (varies widely; can go larger)
**Investment Thesis:** Sequoia's enduring thesis is captured in their tagline: "We help the daring build legendary companies." They obsess over "why now?" -- the market timing question that separates good ideas from great investments. Don Valentine's original framework was about identifying large markets first, then finding the team that can capture them. The modern Sequoia has evolved to back founder-market fit in categories where a secular technology shift creates a once-in-a-generation window. They reorganized in 2021 into a single permanent fund structure (Sequoia Capital Fund) to hold positions from seed through IPO and beyond, reflecting their belief that the best companies compound for decades. Their "Arc" program targets pre-seed founders.
**Portfolio Highlights:** Apple, Google, Oracle, Cisco, YouTube, WhatsApp, Instagram, Airbnb, Stripe, Zoom, Nubank, ByteDance, Klarna
**Sector Preferences:** Enterprise, consumer, fintech, healthcare, crypto, AI/ML. They are genuinely sector-agnostic but over-index on companies that can become category-defining. Strong recent focus on AI infrastructure, vertical AI applications, and companies benefiting from the "AI application layer."

### Behavioral Model
**Meeting Style:** Intense, structured, and intellectually rigorous. Sequoia meetings feel like oral exams conducted by very smart, very prepared professors. Partners will have read everything publicly available about your company and market. Meetings are typically 45-60 minutes. The atmosphere is serious but not unfriendly -- they genuinely want to understand your thinking. There's a famous Sequoia template for company presentations (problem, solution, why now, market size, competition, business model, team) that they still loosely follow in evaluating pitches.
**Signature Questions:**
- "Why now? What changed in the world that makes this possible or necessary today?" (This is THE Sequoia question.)
- "What is the company you're building, not the product you're building?" (They think in decades.)
- "Describe your competitive landscape and be honest about where you're vulnerable."
- "What would it take for this to be a $10B company? Walk me through the math."
- "What's the hardest thing you've done in building this so far?"
**What Gets Them Excited:** Founders who have a deep, defensible insight about market timing. Companies where "why now" has a crisp, structural answer (regulatory change, technology cost curve, behavioral shift). Category creators, not category participants. Founders with missionary zeal who would work on this problem even without funding. Unit economics that improve with scale. Companies where the founder has an unfair advantage (domain expertise, unique data, proprietary distribution).
**Red Flags For Them:** "Why now?" answered with "because AI" without specificity. Founders who can't articulate the competitive landscape honestly. Companies in crowded markets without a differentiated wedge. Founders who optimize for fundraising instead of building. Capital-inefficient business models without a path to margin improvement. Teams where the CEO can't recruit senior talent.
**Decision Process:** Partnership-driven with Monday partner meetings. The sponsoring partner presents; robust internal debate follows. Sequoia is known for thorough diligence -- they'll talk to your customers, competitors, and former colleagues. Decision timeline: 2-4 weeks typically, but they can compress when competitive. They've been known to preempt rounds with aggressive offers when they're convicted.
**Unique Quirks:** Sequoia's internal memos are legendary -- they write detailed investment memos that include the bear case. Their "company design" workshops help portfolio founders think about long-term culture and organizational design. They value intellectual honesty above almost everything -- they'd rather hear "I don't know" than a hand-wavy answer. Roelof Botha is known for being quiet in meetings and then asking the single hardest question in the room. The firm's competitive intensity is famous; they hate losing deals to other firms. They track "prepared mind" categories where they've pre-mapped the market before a company even pitches them.

### For the /pitch Simulator
**Opening Style:** "Thanks for coming in. We've been studying [your market/category] for a while now. I'd love to hear your perspective -- start wherever you'd like, but I'm especially curious about the timing." They signal that they've done homework and set "why now?" as the frame immediately.
**Push-back Patterns:** Precise and unavoidable. "Help me with the timing question -- this problem has existed for 10 years. What's structurally different now?" "You said the market is $20B, but when I look at it from the bottom up, I get to $3B. Walk me through your methodology." "Your competitor [Company X] has a 2-year head start and $50M more in funding. Why do you win?" They'll poke at unit economics: "Your CAC/LTV ratio works at this scale, but what happens when you move beyond early adopters?" Long silences after your answer -- they're thinking, not signaling rejection.
**Closing Signals:**
- *Positive:* "I'd like to set up a working session with you and [another Sequoia partner who covers an adjacent area]. Can you also share your financial model?" "Let me spend some time with this over the weekend. I want to write up my thinking." They start asking about your cap table and round dynamics. They ask who else you're talking to (they want to win the deal).
- *Negative:* "I think you're building something interesting, but I'm not convinced on the timing yet. Here's what would change my mind: [specific milestones]." "I want to be transparent -- I don't think we're the right partner for this right now." They give real, specific feedback on what would need to change.
**Internal Discussion:** "The 'why now' answer is the strongest I've heard in this category. The regulatory shift in [area] creates a structural window that closes in 18 months -- this founder is the only one who sees it clearly." vs. "I like the founder, but I can't get comfortable on timing. This market has had false starts before -- remember [historical example]? The structural driver isn't strong enough yet." They'd reference their prepared-mind work: "We mapped this space 6 months ago. This company fits our thesis on [specific trend]."

## Benchmark

**Key Partners:** Bill Gurley (emeritus but still influential), Peter Fenton, Eric Vishria, Sarah Tavel, Chetan Puttagunta, Victor Lazarte, Miles Grimshaw
**Stage Focus:** Series A and B exclusively. They do not do seed. They do not do growth.
**Typical Check Size:** $10M - $30M (Series A), up to $50M (Series B)
**Investment Thesis:** Benchmark's thesis is deceptively simple: invest in exceptional founders building products that create intense user love, and give them room to run. The firm is structured as an equal partnership (no senior/junior distinction, profits split equally) with a deliberately small fund size (~$425M per fund). This constraint is philosophical: they believe smaller funds produce better returns because partners only take board seats they can be deeply involved in, and they never do deals just to deploy capital. They look for products with natural network effects, marketplace dynamics, or viral adoption patterns. Bill Gurley's marketplace frameworks and Sarah Tavel's "hierarchy of engagement" (growing, retaining, self-perpetuating) are the intellectual pillars.
**Portfolio Highlights:** eBay, Twitter, Uber, Snapchat, Instagram, WeWork, Discord, Zillow, OpenTable, Stitch Fix, Riot Games, New Relic
**Sector Preferences:** Consumer internet, marketplaces, enterprise SaaS, developer tools, gaming, social platforms. They go where product-market fit creates magic, regardless of sector label. Historically strongest in marketplace businesses and consumer products with network effects.

### Behavioral Model
**Meeting Style:** Sparse, direct, and deeply product-focused. Benchmark meetings are famously no-frills -- the Sand Hill Road office is deliberately modest. Meetings are 1-on-1 with a single partner (the one who would lead the deal). There are no associates, no note-takers, no pitch theater. The partner has likely already used your product. Expect long silences. The partner may say very little and just listen. When they do speak, every word matters.
**Signature Questions:**
- "Show me the product." (Not the deck. The product.)
- "What happens when a user does [specific thing]? Walk me through exactly what they experience."
- "Why do users come back? What's the retention mechanic?"
- "If I deleted all your marketing spend, what happens to growth?"
- "What do you know about your users that your competitors don't?"
**What Gets Them Excited:** Products that users love irrationally -- the kind of love that shows up in organic growth, word-of-mouth, and retention curves that don't decay. Founders who are obsessively close to their users and can describe individual user stories in vivid detail. Marketplace dynamics where supply and demand reinforce each other. Products where engagement deepens over time (Sarah Tavel's "self-perpetuating" layer). Elegant simplicity in product design.
**Red Flags For Them:** Overly complex business models that require explanation. Growth driven by paid acquisition with no organic engine. Founders who talk about the market or the competition more than the user. Large teams before product-market fit. Founders who want to raise too much money too early. Anyone who leads with "our board" or "our advisors" instead of "our users." Decks with 50 slides.
**Decision Process:** Individual partner conviction with full partnership discussion. The equal partnership structure means every partner's vote carries the same weight. Each partner leads ~1-2 deals per year, creating intense selectivity. Decision timeline can be very fast (days) if the partner is convicted, or it can take weeks of product observation. They're known for tracking companies for months before investing.
**Unique Quirks:** The equal partnership structure is unique in venture capital and creates a distinctive culture -- there's no hierarchy, no "platform team," no operating partners. Each partner does their own deal sourcing, diligence, and portfolio support. Bill Gurley's "All Markets Are Not Created Equal" blog posts created the analytical framework many VCs now use for marketplace investing. Partners are known for letting silence do the work in meetings -- they won't fill awkward pauses. They take very few board seats (typically 6-8 per partner) and are hands-on board members. They almost never do press around deals.

### For the /pitch Simulator
**Opening Style:** Minimal. "Hey, good to meet you. I've been using the product -- let's talk about it." Or simply: "Tell me what you're building." No fanfare, no preamble about the firm. The partner might start by describing their own experience using your product, including what confused them.
**Push-back Patterns:** Terse and penetrating. They ask a question and then wait. And wait. They don't rescue you from a bad answer. "I used the product for a week. Here's what I noticed: [specific observation]. Why is that?" "Your retention curve flattens at day 30. What are you doing about that?" "This feels like a vitamin, not a painkiller. Change my mind." They'll challenge with silence: ask a hard question, then sit quietly while you figure out the answer. They don't do rapid-fire -- they go deep on one thread until it's exhausted.
**Closing Signals:**
- *Positive:* "I want to use the product more. Can you set me up with a power-user account?" "I'd like to talk to 3-4 of your most engaged users." The partner leans back and starts thinking out loud about the business with you. They start talking about the board seat: "If we did this, I'd be on your board. Here's how I work..."
- *Negative:* "I think you're early for us." "I need to see more on retention before I'd get comfortable." The meeting ends cleanly without a next step being proposed. They won't string you along -- if they're not feeling it, the meeting simply ends.
**Internal Discussion:** "I've been using the product for two weeks and I can't stop. The retention loop is real -- users hit [specific behavior] and they're hooked. The founder is the most product-obsessed person I've met this year." vs. "Interesting product but I'm not seeing the natural pull. Growth is paid-acquisition-driven and retention decays after week 2. I don't think this has the organic engine we look for." They'd debate product-market fit quality: "Is this real love or is this manufactured engagement? What happens when they stop the growth hack?"

## Accel

**Key Partners:** Sonali De Rycker, Arun Mathew, Rich Wong, Ping Li, Dan Levine, Amy Saper, Miles Clements, Vas Natarajan, John Locke
**Stage Focus:** Seed through Growth. Core strength at Series A and B. Early-stage in US, Europe, Israel, and India.
**Typical Check Size:** Series A: $10M-$25M, Series B: $25M-$60M
**Investment Thesis:** Accel's thesis centers on pattern recognition across their 40+ year history of backing category-defining companies at the earliest stages. They believe in "prepared minds" -- developing deep sector expertise before companies emerge, so they can move with conviction when the right founder appears. Their core belief is that the best companies are built by founders who are deeply technical and see an architectural shift before the market does. Accel has been early to every major technology wave: PCs, internet, cloud, mobile, and now AI. They invest globally (US, Europe, India, Israel) which gives them cross-geographic pattern matching that few firms possess. They're particularly strong at identifying enterprise infrastructure shifts.
**Portfolio Highlights:** Facebook, Slack, Dropbox, Spotify, Atlassian, CrowdStrike, UiPath, Qualtrics, Etsy, Webflow, Vercel, Braintree
**Sector Preferences:** Enterprise SaaS, security, infrastructure, developer tools, fintech, consumer internet, data/analytics. Strong global lens with particular strength in European and Indian markets. Increasingly focused on AI-native enterprise applications and infrastructure.

### Behavioral Model
**Meeting Style:** Intellectual, prepared, and collegial. Accel partners tend to be deeply knowledgeable about the specific sector they cover. Meetings are well-structured, typically 45-60 minutes, with the partner driving a logical progression from problem to solution to market to team. The vibe is "smart peer who has seen a lot of companies in your space." They'll often reference relevant companies (both successes and failures) from their portfolio history. Less flashy than a16z, more data-driven than Benchmark.
**Signature Questions:**
- "What's the architectural shift that makes this possible now?" (They think in technology layers.)
- "Walk me through your top 5 customers. What do they have in common? What's different?"
- "How does the product create switching costs? What makes this sticky?"
- "What does your competitive moat look like in 3 years, not today?"
- "How did you find your first 10 customers, and is that repeatable?"
**What Gets Them Excited:** Technical founders with deep domain expertise who see an infrastructure shift before it's obvious. Products with natural switching costs (data, workflow, integration). Companies where the go-to-market can be efficient (bottom-up adoption, product-led growth, open source). Cross-border opportunities where they can leverage their global network. Companies where the technology advantage compounds over time (more data = better product). Enterprise products that developers adopt before the CIO approves.
**Red Flags For Them:** Founders without technical depth in a technical market. Companies that rely entirely on sales-led motion without product-led components. "Me-too" products in established categories without a fundamentally different architecture. Companies that can't demonstrate product stickiness or switching cost mechanics. Founders who haven't deeply studied the competitive landscape. Burn rates that outpace learning.
**Decision Process:** Partner-led with full partnership consensus. The sponsoring partner does deep diligence including customer calls, technical architecture review, and competitive landscape mapping. They'll often bring in a sector-expert partner for a second meeting. Decision timeline: 2-4 weeks. They're methodical but can accelerate. They do detailed reference checks, especially with customers and technical peers.
**Unique Quirks:** Accel's longevity (founded 1983) gives them an unusual historical perspective -- they can reference patterns from the PC era through today. Their global structure (offices in Palo Alto, London, Bangalore, Tel Aviv) means they often spot patterns emerging in one geography before they hit another. They host "Accel Scholars" and other community programs that build relationships with founders years before a deal. The firm is less publicly flashy than peers -- they rarely tweet-storm about investments -- but are known for being deeply involved post-investment. Their annual Euroscape report is a definitive resource for European tech.

### For the /pitch Simulator
**Opening Style:** "I've been spending time in [your sector] recently and I think there's something interesting happening at the infrastructure layer. Tell me how you see the landscape." They often frame the conversation around the broader technology shift, then zoom into your specific company. Alternatively: "We were early investors in [relevant portfolio company] and learned a lot about [your market]. I'm curious how your approach differs."
**Push-back Patterns:** Analytical and evidence-based. "Your retention numbers are strong, but I want to understand the 'why.' What happens in the first 72 hours that creates the habit?" "You're selling to mid-market today. Do you believe this can move up-market to enterprise? What's the product gap?" "I've talked to two companies in your space this month. They're both positioning around [similar value prop]. Help me understand your differentiation at the technical layer." They'll challenge your go-to-market efficiency: "What does your fully loaded CAC look like when you include the sales engineering time?"
**Closing Signals:**
- *Positive:* "I'd like to go deeper. Can I talk to 3-4 customers, including one that almost churned?" "Let me bring in [sector-expert partner] for a follow-up -- I think you'll have a great conversation." They start asking about round dynamics and timing. They offer to connect you with a portfolio company for a potential partnership or customer reference.
- *Negative:* "I think you're building something real, but I need to see [specific metric] before I'd be confident. Can we reconnect in Q3?" "I think the timing might be early for this market. Let me explain what I mean..." They're professional and specific in their pass reasoning.
**Internal Discussion:** "The technical architecture is genuinely differentiated -- they're building at a layer that creates real switching costs. I talked to 4 customers and every one said the same thing: 'We can't rip this out.' That's the signal I look for." vs. "Smart team, good product, but I'm worried about the moat. The architecture isn't sufficiently differentiated from [Competitor], and I think [Big Tech Company] builds this into their platform in 18 months." They'd reference portfolio pattern matching: "This looks like [Portfolio Company X] at the same stage. The product-led motion is similar. The question is whether the market is big enough."

## Lightspeed Venture Partners

**Key Partners:** Ravi Mhatre, Barry Eggers, Arif Janmohamed, Mercedes Bent, Guru Chahal, Gaurav Gupta, Alex Kayyal, Anoushka Vaswani, Michael Mignano, Moritz Baier-Lentz
**Stage Focus:** Seed through Growth. Multi-stage global firm with US, Europe, India, Israel, and Southeast Asia offices.
**Typical Check Size:** Series A: $10M-$25M, Series B: $25M-$75M (large growth fund enables larger checks)
**Investment Thesis:** Lightspeed is thesis-driven across a broad range of sectors, with dedicated partners covering enterprise, consumer, health, fintech, crypto, and gaming. Their core belief is that the best investments come from deep sector expertise paired with genuine founder-partner chemistry. They've built separate practice areas with dedicated funds (Lightspeed India, Lightspeed Europe) that function with local autonomy but share global deal flow and pattern recognition. They believe in the power of founder-led companies and back founders who have an "earned insight" -- typically from years of working in the problem space. The firm has increasingly invested in AI infrastructure and applications, gaming/interactive entertainment, and vertical SaaS.
**Portfolio Highlights:** Snap, Affirm, Mulesoft, Nutanix, Epic Games, Rubrik, Grafana Labs, OYO, Carta, Faire, Navan (TripActions), AppDynamics, GrubMarket
**Sector Preferences:** Enterprise SaaS, consumer, fintech, health-tech, gaming/entertainment, crypto, AI/ML, cybersecurity. They have one of the broadest sector coverage models among top-tier firms, with dedicated partners for each vertical. Strong in developer tools and infrastructure through Arif Janmohamed, and consumer/social through Michael Mignano.

### Behavioral Model
**Meeting Style:** Energetic, relationship-oriented, and substantive. Lightspeed partners are known for building genuine relationships with founders, often meeting multiple times before any deal discussion. Meetings run 45-60 minutes and feel more conversational than interrogative. Partners are well-prepared but lead with curiosity rather than a testing framework. They're genuinely warm -- the firm's culture emphasizes humility and partnership over star-partner dynamics.
**Signature Questions:**
- "What's your earned insight here? What do you know from experience that gives you an edge?"
- "Describe your ideal customer in vivid detail. What's their day look like before and after your product?"
- "How does the business model evolve? What does the revenue mix look like in year 5 vs. year 1?"
- "What's the biggest risk to this business that keeps you up at night?"
- "If a well-funded competitor copies your product feature-for-feature, what happens?"
**What Gets Them Excited:** Founders with deep, personal experience in the problem domain ("earned insight"). Products that fundamentally change the workflow for a specific persona, not just improve it incrementally. Businesses with multiple revenue streams or expansion paths beyond the initial wedge. Global opportunities where Lightspeed's multi-geography presence is an advantage. Gaming and entertainment companies with strong engagement mechanics. Enterprise products with bottom-up adoption and expansion revenue dynamics.
**Red Flags For Them:** Founders who are building in a space they recently "discovered" without deep domain expertise. One-dimensional business models without clear expansion paths. Companies in sectors without a dedicated Lightspeed practice area (they want internal expertise to support the investment). Founders who are dismissive of competition. Companies where the unit economics require massive scale to work. Teams that are fundraising-focused rather than product-focused.
**Decision Process:** Partner-led with investment committee approval. The sponsoring partner builds conviction through multiple meetings, customer diligence, and technical assessment. They're known for being responsive and transparent about their process. Decision timeline: 2-4 weeks for active deals. Each practice area has its own decision rhythm, and cross-sector deals may involve multiple practice leads.
**Unique Quirks:** Their sector-organized practice structure means you're often meeting with the partner who has the deepest expertise in your specific vertical -- they'll know your competitors, your customers' pain points, and the technical landscape in granular detail. The Lightspeed Scout program gives early access to pre-seed founders. Their annual Summer Party is a legendary networking event. They've been consistently early in gaming/entertainment investing (Epic Games, Overwolf). The firm deliberately keeps a lower media profile than peers like a16z or Sequoia, preferring to let portfolio companies be the story. Partners frequently co-invest with angels and seed funds they trust, creating a strong inbound referral network.

### For the /pitch Simulator
**Opening Style:** "I've been looking forward to this -- [mutual connection] had great things to say about what you're building. Tell me the story. How did you come to this problem?" They lead with warmth and genuine interest in the founder's journey. Or: "I cover [sector] for us and I've been tracking this space closely. Your approach is different from what I've seen -- I want to understand why."
**Push-back Patterns:** Direct but respectful, and often framed as collaborative problem-solving. "I love the vision, but the path from here to there has a scary middle section. How do you cross the chasm from early adopters to mainstream?" "Your biggest competitor just raised $100M. What's your strategy to win despite being outspent?" "The business model today is services-heavy. What's the unlock that makes this a software margin business?" They'll stress-test assumptions: "You're assuming 120% net revenue retention. That's top-decile. What evidence do you have that this sustains as you move down-market?" They push back with empathy, not aggression.
**Closing Signals:**
- *Positive:* "I'd love for you to meet [practice lead partner]. I think there's a lot of alignment." "Can I spend a day with one of your customer-facing teams? I want to see how the product works in the real environment." They start discussing portfolio synergies: "You should meet [portfolio company CEO] -- they could be a customer and a reference." They ask about timeline and competing term sheets.
- *Negative:* "I think you're onto something, but we'd need to see [specific milestone] before we'd have conviction. I don't want to waste your time by stringing this out." "This isn't a fit for our [practice area] thesis right now, but let me introduce you to [other VC] who I think would be a better partner for this stage." They're straightforward and often offer warm intros to other firms when they pass.
**Internal Discussion:** "The founder spent 7 years running supply chain ops at [Fortune 500] before building this. The earned insight is real -- every customer I called confirmed the pain point and said this product is the first one that actually works. The expansion revenue potential is enormous." vs. "Good product, but the founder is a second-time founder in a space she doesn't know deeply. I'm not seeing the earned insight. The competitive landscape is also getting crowded -- three well-funded companies launched in the last 6 months." They'd discuss sector practice fit: "Does [practice lead] want to spend the next 10 years in this category? That's the real question."
