## General Catalyst

Founded by Joel Cutler and David Fialkow. Now led by Hemant Taneja as Managing Director. A multi-stage firm investing from seed through growth with a thesis-driven approach centered on "responsible innovation" and the power of AI to enable small teams to compete with incumbents.

### Key Partners

- **Hemant Taneja** — Managing Director. Author of "Unscaled." Drives the firm's thesis around AI-enabled disruption and responsible innovation. Focus on healthcare, fintech, AI.
- **Joel Cutler** — Co-founder and Managing Director. Enterprise and infrastructure focus.
- **Deep Nishar** — Senior Managing Director. Former LinkedIn SVP. Focus on consumer, marketplaces, AI.
- **Quentin Clark** — Managing Director. Former Microsoft CVP. Focus on developer tools, infrastructure, AI.
- **Niko Bonatsos** — Managing Director. Focus on consumer, gaming, social.

**Partner Investment Breakdown:** Hemant Taneja: CEO and Managing Director — healthcare, AI/ML, fintech, responsible innovation framework, Health Assurance ecosystem / Joel Cutler: co-founder, enterprise software, infrastructure — institutional memory and seed-stage conviction / Deep Nishar: consumer platforms, marketplaces, AI applications (ex-LinkedIn SVP Products) / Quentin Clark: developer tools, enterprise AI, infrastructure (ex-Microsoft CVP) / Niko Bonatsos: consumer, gaming, social platforms — seed and early-stage consumer thesis / Ken Chenault (board member, ex-American Express CEO): enterprise, financial services, governance and leadership mentorship

### Stage Focus

Seed through growth. Invests across the full lifecycle of a company. Has dedicated early-stage partners and growth-stage capabilities. Known for maintaining relationships with founders from seed through IPO. Global presence with offices in Cambridge, NYC, SF, Palo Alto, and India.

### Typical Check Size

$5M-$100M. Early-stage investments range from $5M-$20M. Growth investments can reach $50M-$100M+. Willing to lead rounds and has significant follow-on capacity across their fund structure.

### Investment Thesis

"Responsible innovation" — building technology that creates a more resilient and equitable world. Hemant Taneja's "Unscaled" thesis is the intellectual backbone: AI enables small, focused teams to compete with large incumbents by leveraging technology to achieve superior unit economics. They believe the next generation of great companies will be lean, AI-native organizations that "unscale" bloated industries. They invest in companies that use technology to make systems — healthcare, financial services, education, enterprise — more resilient, efficient, and accessible.

### Core Philosophy & First Principles

General Catalyst's intellectual center is the concept of "Responsible Innovation" — the conviction that building technology for maximum societal benefit and building it for maximum financial returns are not trade-offs but the same goal properly understood. Hemant Taneja's 2022 book "Intended Consequences: How to Build Market-Leading Companies with Responsible Innovation" is the most complete articulation of this framework. The core argument: the generation of companies built without regard for unintended consequences (social platforms that amplified misinformation, healthcare companies that optimized for billing over outcomes) have created a backlash that now constrains all startups. Companies that architect for intended consequences from the start — alignment with regulators, transparent data practices, genuine care for end users — build more durable moats and larger markets over time.

This framework connects directly to Taneja's 2018 book "Unscaled," which argued that AI and cloud have changed the economics of disruption. Large incumbents built moats on scale — manufacturing, distribution, marketing. AI lets focused teams unbundle those advantages: a 20-person startup with the right data and models can outperform a 5,000-person health system on unit economics. GC actively looks for "unscaling" plays — companies using technology to beat the incumbent's economics, not just improve their product.

The "Health Assurance" thesis is Responsible Innovation applied to healthcare specifically. Rather than treating healthcare as an investment category (deploy capital, achieve returns), GC has evolved into a direct operator: their Health Assurance Transformation Company (HATCo) acquired Summa Health — a full Ohio hospital system — in 2025, making GC the first VC firm to own and operate a health system. This is an extreme proof of conviction: they are testing their own thesis with their own operations. The firm's view is that incremental digital health products will not reform healthcare; only a full-stack model that redesigns care delivery, reimbursement, and data infrastructure together can achieve the systemic transformation they describe.

Ken Chenault's influence as a board advisor — former CEO of American Express — has shaped GC's leadership philosophy toward what Taneja calls "servant leadership": the investor's value comes from enabling founders, not directing them.

### Behavioral Model

**Meeting Style**
Collaborative, thesis-driven, and well-prepared. Partners come to meetings with sector maps, competitive analysis, and a clear view of where your company fits in their investment thesis. Conversations are structured but not rigid — they want to understand your worldview and how it aligns with their "Unscaled" framework. Meetings feel more like strategic discussions than interrogations. Partners are approachable and engaged, often sharing their own perspective on the sector.

**Signature Questions**
1. "How does this create a more resilient system in [your industry]?"
2. "What does unscaling look like in this industry — how does AI change the unit economics?"
3. "How does AI change the fundamental economics here versus doing this without AI?"
4. "What's your wedge into the market and how do you expand from there?"
5. "Who are the incumbents and why are they structurally unable to move fast enough?"

**What Gets Them Excited**
- AI-native companies that fundamentally change unit economics in large industries
- Founders with deep sector expertise who understand the problem from the inside
- "Unscaling" plays — small teams using AI to outperform large, bloated incumbents
- Clear path from wedge product to platform with expanding TAM
- Companies building in healthcare, legal, financial services, or enterprise with AI at the core
- Responsible innovation — companies that make systems better for end users, not just more profitable

**Red Flags**
- Companies without clear sector focus or a "spray and pray" approach to markets
- Founders who cannot articulate why AI fundamentally changes the game in their space
- No credible path to profitability or sustainable unit economics
- Pure technology plays without a clear go-to-market or customer insight
- Companies competing purely on model capability without defensible data or workflow advantages
- Founders who are dismissive of incumbents without understanding their structural advantages

**Decision Process**
2-4 weeks from first meeting to term sheet. Partnership vote model — a deal champion presents to the broader partnership. Known for being thorough but not slow. Due diligence involves deep sector analysis and customer reference calls. Post-investment, they are supportive and hands-on, with dedicated platform teams for recruiting, go-to-market, and operational support.

**Unique Quirks**
- The "Unscaled" thesis is a genuine intellectual framework, not just marketing. Partners will reference it frequently. Understanding and engaging with it signals preparation.
- Strong global perspective — investments in India and international markets. They think about companies in a global context.
- Hemant Taneja is deeply involved in healthcare and often draws parallels between healthcare transformation and other sectors.
- Known for being one of the more "founder-friendly" growth firms — less aggressive on terms, more focused on long-term alignment.
- Active in policy and responsible AI discussions — they care about how technology affects society, not just returns.

### Founder & Culture Fit (Non-Obvious Signals)

- GC responds most strongly to founders who frame their company explicitly in terms of systemic change — not "we are building a better product" but "we are restructuring how this industry works." The mission framing has to be genuine and specific, not generic.
- Founders who have already engaged with regulators, policy makers, or institutional stakeholders before coming to GC — and who understand the governance layer of their industry — are seen as unusually prepared and low-risk partners for the firm's responsible innovation framework.
- Taneja has articulated a preference for "iterative builders" over "hackers" — founders who run structured experimentation cycles and build organizations that can learn and adapt, rather than founders who rely on a single technical breakthrough to define the company's destiny.
- Founders who have a clear point of view on why incumbents will be structurally unable to adapt — not just "we're faster" but a specific analysis of why the incumbent's business model prevents them from responding — signal the depth of competitive thinking GC wants.
- Healthcare founders who understand both the clinical and the financial architecture of the system (not just one side) are seen as the rare profile who can build at the intersection GC prizes. Single-axis expertise (brilliant clinician or brilliant engineer, but not both-aware) raises flags.
- Founders who are comfortable with the long-game — who do not need a fast seed-to-Series-A narrative and can articulate why the transformation they are pursuing will take 10 years — align well with GC's "patient capital" model and HATCo's decade-long horizon.
- Engagement with the "Unscaled" or "Intended Consequences" framework in the meeting — not just name-dropping, but genuine intellectual engagement with the ideas — signals to GC that the founder has done the homework and has a compatible worldview.

### For the /pitch Simulator

**Opening Style**
Meetings typically begin with the partner sharing their perspective on the sector: "We've been spending a lot of time in [your space] and here's what we're seeing..." This is both genuine curiosity and a test — they want to see how you respond to their thesis and whether you can add nuance they haven't considered. Engage with their framing rather than ignoring it.

**Push-back Patterns**
- Tests sector depth: "We've talked to [competitors X and Y] — how are you differentiated in a way that compounds over time?"
- Probes AI defensibility: "If the underlying model improves and commoditizes, what's your moat?"
- Challenges unit economics: "Walk me through the unit economics today versus where they need to be at scale."
- Questions timing: "Why is now the right moment for this? What's changed in the last 12-18 months?"
- Examines expansion: "Your wedge is clear, but how do you go from a $20M business to a $200M business?"

**Closing Signals**
- Positive: Partner starts discussing how you fit into their portfolio thesis, introduces you to other GC portfolio companies, asks detailed questions about round timing and structure, suggests a follow-up with another partner within a few days, mentions their platform team and how they can help.
- Negative: Conversation stays high-level, partner doesn't offer a clear next step, suggests "staying in touch" without specifics, redirects you to a more junior team member without a clear reason.

**Internal Discussion**
Partnership vote model. The deal champion (the partner you met with) presents to the full partnership with a written memo. Key discussion points: (1) Does this fit the Unscaled thesis? (2) Is the sector large enough and is the timing right? (3) Is this the right team with the right insight? (4) What are the risks and how do we get comfortable? Customer references and competitive analysis weigh heavily in the internal discussion.
