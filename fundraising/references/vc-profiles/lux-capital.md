## Lux Capital

**Stage Focus:** Seed and Series A. They invest earliest in deep tech and frontier science companies, often at the formation stage when there is barely a prototype. They will occasionally follow on into Series B for existing portfolio companies.
**Typical Check Size:** $1M - $5M (seed), $5M - $20M (Series A)
**Investment Thesis:** Lux Capital practices what Josh Wolfe calls "directed serendipity" -- they invest in founders working at the frontier of science and technology, building things that feel like science fiction today but will be obvious in 10 years. Josh's core framework is "narratives + numbers": a founder needs both a compelling story about the future and rigorous quantitative evidence that the science works. Their mantra is to "find what's in the adjacent possible" -- they look for breakthroughs in physics, biology, materials science, or computation that unlock entirely new categories of product. They are not interested in incremental improvements or pure software plays. They want hard tech moats rooted in genuine scientific innovation: things that are difficult to replicate because the underlying science is hard. They believe the best venture returns come from investing in founders who see the future before anyone else and have the technical depth to build it.
**Portfolio Highlights:** Anduril, Hugging Face, Eikon Therapeutics, Variant Bio, Saildrone, Shapeways, Kurion, Desktop Metal, Evolv Technology, Kallyope, Latchbio
**Recent Investments (2022-2025):** Evolutionary Scale (spinout from Meta's biology AI team; protein language models for drug discovery, 2023); Osmo (spinout from Alphabet's olfaction team; AI-driven aroma molecule design, 2023); Sakana AI (Japan-based nature-inspired foundation models using evolutionary/swarm intelligence principles, 2023); Cognition (AI coding agent, makers of Devin the first AI software engineer, 2023); Applied Intuition (autonomous vehicle simulation, $250M raise at $6B+ valuation, 2024); Lumafield (industrial CT scanning for defect detection and maintenance); SkySelect (AI-driven aircraft parts/MRO logistics); Varda (commercial pharmaceutical manufacturing in space, 2023 Series B). Fund VIII closed at $1.15B in 2023; total AUM over $5B.
**Key Partners:** Josh Wolfe (co-founder, managing partner), Peter Hébert (co-founder, managing partner), Bilal Zuberi, Deena Shakir, Grace Isford, Brandon Reeves, Shahin Farshchi
**Partner Investment Breakdown:** Josh Wolfe: deep tech / defense / entropy-thesis companies; primary public face, deal sourcing through scientific community network — Peter Hébert: business model viability, Series A leads; complementary analytical lens to Wolfe — Bilal Zuberi: industrial tech, advanced manufacturing, climate and energy — Deena Shakir: health and biotech, digital health, reproductive health — Grace Isford: consumer tech, applied AI, developer tools — Shahin Farshchi: robotics, autonomous systems, semiconductor and deep hardware — Brandon Reeves: defense tech, aerospace, national security applications
**Sector Preferences:** Deep tech and frontier technology across: AI/ML and AI infrastructure, robotics, space and aerospace, defense technology, biotech and computational biology, quantum computing, advanced materials, energy, and climate tech. They go where the science is hardest and the moats are deepest.

### Core Philosophy & First Principles

Josh Wolfe's worldview begins with the Second Law of Thermodynamics: entropy is the default state of all systems. Things fall apart unless energy is continuously invested to maintain order. This is not a metaphor — it is Wolfe's literal operating framework. In markets, relationships, institutions, and companies, disorder is always the path of least resistance. The implication for investing is that durable ventures must build genuine physical or scientific moats; software alone cannot hold off entropy indefinitely.

From this foundation flows the "Entropic Apex" concept, which Wolfe articulated around 2022 as markets peaked: the point at which maximum instability makes systems most susceptible to collapse. He distinguished between FOMO-driven investing (fear of missing out) and the discipline he calls SOBS (shame of being suckered) — the more productive emotion for deep tech investors who must resist crowded narratives.

The counterpart thesis to entropic decay is the "Fixware" framework (articulated in 2024): as capital becomes expensive and the era of zero-rate FOMO ends, value accrues to technologies that extend asset life, predict failure before it happens, and resist the entropic decline in physical infrastructure. This includes predictive maintenance, autonomous inspection, advanced sensors, and ML models that anticipate breakdowns — a direct inversion of the speculative software bets that dominated the 2010s.

Wolfe's framework for sourcing is what he calls "directed serendipity": a disciplined process of staying at the edges of scientific communities — labs, papers, government research programs, defense agencies — to identify the precise moment when a previously impossible technology crosses into the commercially feasible. His organizing phrase for this is "the adjacent possible": the set of things that can exist now that couldn't exist before, given recent scientific developments. The fund's 100-0-100 rule captures the philosophy: 100% confidence Lux will fund the most important frontier companies, 0% certainty which ones those will be, and 100% commitment to finding them through deep network immersion.

### Behavioral Model
**Meeting Style:** Intellectual and intense. Meetings with Lux -- especially with Josh Wolfe -- feel like a graduate seminar, not a pitch meeting. Josh is deeply read across physics, biology, history, and philosophy, and expects founders to match that depth. He will have read your papers, tested your models, and researched your scientific claims before you walk in. Expect probing technical questions from minute one. There is no warm-up small talk about the market or the TAM slide. They go straight to the science. Peter Hébert brings a complementary lens focused on business model viability and defensibility. The atmosphere is one of genuine intellectual curiosity -- they are not trying to trip you up, they are trying to understand whether the science is real and the founder truly understands it at the deepest level.
**Signature Questions:**
- "What's the physics or science breakthrough that makes this possible now?"
- "Who else in the world could build this?"
- "What does the world look like if you succeed?"
- "What's the adjacent possible here?"
- "What breaks first when you scale?"
**What Gets Them Excited:** Founders with deep scientific expertise who can explain complex phenomena with clarity and conviction. Science breakthroughs that unlock entirely new product categories -- things that were physically impossible five years ago. Hard tech moats that competitors cannot replicate with software alone. Founders who have spent years in a lab or research environment and have a unique insight into what just became possible. Companies where the technology risk is real but the reward is a new market category. Intersections of frontier science and practical applications (defense AI, computational drug discovery, autonomous systems). Founders who think in decades, not quarters.
**Red Flags For Them:** Pure software plays without a hard tech moat. Founders who cannot explain the underlying science clearly or who hand-wave through technical questions. Incremental improvements over existing technology rather than category-creating breakthroughs. Founders who lead with market size and business model before demonstrating that the technology actually works. Overly polished pitch decks that substitute storytelling for substance. Companies where the "AI" is really just a wrapper around an API with no proprietary model or data advantage.
**Decision Process:** Thorough and conviction-driven, typically 3-6 weeks. Lux conducts heavy technical diligence -- they will read your papers, talk to domain experts, and test your technology. The partnership is small and high-conviction: they do not do deals by committee or consensus. A lead partner must develop deep personal conviction. They often involve external scientific advisors in diligence. Once convinced, they move decisively and are known for leading rounds and setting terms. They are comfortable with long timelines to commercialization, which is unusual in venture.
**Unique Quirks:** Josh Wolfe is one of the most prolific and quotable investors on Twitter/X, known for aphorisms like "the best ideas look like bad ideas at first" and his "narratives + numbers" framework. He maintains a public persona as an intellectual provocateur and science evangelist, which means he often finds companies through the scientific community rather than traditional deal flow. Lux runs an annual "Lux Summit" that brings together scientists, founders, and defense leaders. They have a deep network in the defense and intelligence community through investments like Anduril. They were early believers in the Hugging Face open-source AI ecosystem when most VCs dismissed open-source AI as a business model.

### Founder & Culture Fit (Non-Obvious Signals)

- **Adversity history as signal.** Wolfe actively seeks founders who have overcome significant personal hardship — poverty, learning disabilities, speech impediments, broken families, or a formative rejection that left something to prove. He has said he looks for "productive resentment" and a "burning fire inside them." Smooth backgrounds with easy paths are a mild negative signal.

- **Rebel-intellectual archetype.** The ideal Lux founder is a lifelong learner who connects disparate disciplines — someone who has read Feynman AND read Taleb, who sees physics in markets and biology in organizations. Wolfe himself embodies this archetype and gravitates toward founders who mirror it.

- **Scientific credibility without academic fragility.** Founders must have genuine technical depth — but not the kind of academic expert who can't speak to outsiders. Wolfe values founders who can explain hard science simply. If you can only explain your work to other experts in your subfield, that's a red flag.

- **Chip-on-shoulder energy combined with intellectual humility.** Wolfe's "chips on shoulders put chips in pockets" principle is about motivational fuel, not arrogance. The best founders in his portfolio are fiercely competitive AND open to being wrong on secondary questions. They fight for the mission, not for ego.

- **Comfort with long timelines and no external validation.** Lux invests in things that often won't be proven right for a decade. Founders who need quarterly affirmation or are emotionally dependent on external praise are poorly suited to this kind of partnership.

- **Narrative + numbers duality.** Wolfe has articulated that he needs founders who can do both: tell a compelling story about why the future will look radically different AND back it with rigorous quantitative evidence. Founders who are purely poetic visionaries (no data) or purely technical (can't tell a story) both get filtered out.

### For the /pitch Simulator
**Opening Style:** Josh might open with a reference to a paper you published or a tweet you wrote six months ago. "I read your paper on [topic] -- the part about [specific finding] was interesting. Tell me what you've figured out since then." Or: "I've been following the work coming out of [your lab/institution]. What are you seeing that nobody else is talking about?" The opening is intellectually engaged from the start -- they've done their homework and they want you to know it. Peter may start more directly: "Walk me through the core technology. What's the breakthrough?"
**Push-back Patterns:** Deep and technical. Josh will go several layers into the science: "You say this works because of [mechanism]. Walk me through why that mechanism scales. What are the failure modes?" "I talked to [domain expert] who thinks [alternative approach] is more promising. Why are they wrong?" "The physics works in the lab. What changes at manufacturing scale?" "Hugging Face made open-source AI work as a business. What makes your moat defensible if someone open-sources the core science?" They push on the difference between lab results and commercial viability. They want to see that you've thought about the hard engineering problems, not just the science.
**Closing Signals:**
- *Positive:* Josh starts riffing on the future with you: "Okay, so if this works, then you could also do [adjacent application]..." He brings up other portfolio companies and potential synergies. "I want to bring you in to meet [specific Lux advisor or portfolio founder]." He starts talking about the round structure and who else should be in the syndicate. Peter asks about capital requirements and milestones for the next 18 months.
- *Negative:* "This is interesting science but I'm not sure it's a venture-scale business yet." "I think you're a year or two early for us -- come back when you have [specific technical milestone]." The meeting stays at a high level and they don't go deep into diligence questions. Josh doesn't bring up other people he wants you to meet. The conversation drifts to general industry discussion rather than your specific company.
**Internal Discussion:** "I've been tracking this area for two years and this is the first team I've seen that actually has the science working. The founder spent eight years in [lab] and has a unique insight into [mechanism]. Nobody else can build this -- the talent moat is real. This is classic adjacent possible: [breakthrough] just made this feasible for the first time." vs. "The science is cool but I'm not convinced it translates to a product. The founder is brilliant but can't articulate a path to commercialization. And the moat worries me -- if [big tech company] decides to go after this, they have more compute and more data. I'd want to see [specific milestone] before getting comfortable."
