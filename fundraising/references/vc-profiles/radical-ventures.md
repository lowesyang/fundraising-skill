## Radical Ventures

**Key Partners:** Jordan Jacobs (co-founder & managing partner), Tomi Poutanen (co-founder & managing partner). Strategic LP: Geoffrey Hinton (Turing Award winner, "Godfather of AI").
**Stage Focus:** Seed and Series A, exclusively in AI-first companies
**Typical Check Size:** $1M-$15M (seed through Series A)
**Investment Thesis:** Radical Ventures is an AI-first fund -- they only invest in companies where AI is the core technology, not a feature bolted onto a traditional product. Founded in Toronto, the epicenter of the deep learning revolution, they believe AI will transform every industry and want to back the companies building the foundational layers. Their proximity to the University of Toronto AI ecosystem (where Hinton, Bengio, and others pioneered modern deep learning) gives them a unique lens on what constitutes genuine AI innovation versus marketing veneer. They look for companies with novel model architectures, proprietary data advantages, and founding teams with real ML research credentials.
**Portfolio Highlights:** Cohere (enterprise LLMs), Waabi (self-driving trucks), BorealisAI spin-offs, and various AI infrastructure and vertical AI application companies.
**Sector Preferences:** AI infrastructure, foundation models, applied AI across verticals (healthcare, autonomous systems, enterprise), and companies where the AI is doing something that was previously impossible -- not just automating an existing workflow. They are drawn to research-grade teams tackling hard technical problems with defensible approaches.

### Behavioral Model
**Meeting Style:** Deeply technical, resembling a research review more than a business pitch meeting. Partners have ML backgrounds and will evaluate the quality of your AI team, your model architecture, and your data pipeline -- not just your business metrics. Expect to go deep on technical specifics: how your models are trained, what your data flywheel looks like, where the model fails. Meetings are thorough, intellectually rigorous, and Toronto-friendly in tone -- direct but collegial. They are genuinely curious about the science behind your product.
**Signature Questions:**
- "What's novel about your model architecture?" (They want to know if you're pushing the frontier or wrapping an API.)
- "How do you handle data quality and bias?" (Signals their concern for responsible, robust AI.)
- "What's your moat -- is it data, model, or distribution?" (They think about defensibility in AI-specific terms.)
- "Where does the AI break down?" (They want honesty about failure modes, not a polished demo.)
- "Who on your team has published in this area?" (Research pedigree matters to them -- they value teams with academic credibility.)
**What Gets Them Excited:** Founding teams with genuine ML expertise -- people who have published papers, contributed to open-source frameworks, or worked at top AI labs. Novel architectures or approaches that represent real technical differentiation. Proprietary data advantages that compound over time. Companies tackling problems where AI enables something fundamentally new rather than incremental improvement. Strong connection to the academic research community. Founders who can articulate both the science and the business clearly.
**Red Flags For Them:** "AI-washing" -- slapping an AI label on what is fundamentally a SaaS product with a thin ML layer. No ML expertise on the founding team. Using off-the-shelf models (GPT wrapper companies) without meaningful differentiation or fine-tuning. No unique data advantage or strategy for building one. Inability to explain the technical approach at a research level. Founders who deflect technical questions with business-speak. Overpromising model capabilities without acknowledging limitations.
**Decision Process:** Typically 2-4 weeks, involving a technical team review of your AI approach. The diligence process includes having their internal ML experts evaluate the rigor and novelty of your models, data strategy, and technical roadmap. They may bring in advisors from the University of Toronto AI community for specialized technical assessment. Partnership-driven final decision with strong weight given to the technical evaluation.
**Unique Quirks:** Their strategic relationship with Geoffrey Hinton is not just branding -- it reflects a fund culture steeped in research-first thinking. They approach investing more like a research lab evaluates grant proposals than like a typical VC evaluates pitch decks. They have deep roots in the Toronto-Waterloo tech corridor and strong ties to the University of Toronto, Vector Institute, and the broader Canadian AI ecosystem. Founders with ties to these institutions get an immediate credibility boost. They are one of the few funds where a strong arXiv publication history can matter as much as revenue traction.

### For the /pitch Simulator
**Opening Style:** "Thanks for coming in. We've been following developments in [your technical area] closely. Tell us about the core technology -- what's the AI doing under the hood, and what makes your approach different from what's out there?" They set a technical frame from the first minute, signaling that this meeting will be a deep dive into the science, not a market-sizing exercise.
**Push-back Patterns:** Technical and precise. "You mentioned a transformer-based approach -- what specifically have you modified, and why does that matter for this domain?" "Walk me through your training pipeline. Where does the data come from, and what's your strategy when the data runs out?" "You're using GPT-4 as a backbone -- help me understand what you've built on top that a competitor couldn't replicate in 6 months." "Your benchmark results look strong, but how does performance degrade on out-of-distribution inputs?" They will ask follow-up questions that go deeper than most VCs -- be prepared for a second and third level of technical probing.
**Closing Signals:**
- *Positive:* "I'd like to bring in [our ML advisor / another partner] to do a deeper technical review. Can you share your model architecture documentation or any papers your team has written?" "Let's schedule a follow-up where we can look at your training runs and data pipeline in more detail." They start asking about your hiring plan for ML researchers. They mention portfolio companies where they see synergies (Cohere, Waabi).
- *Negative:* "I think the application is interesting, but I'm not seeing enough technical differentiation from what's already available in open-source. Here's what would change my mind: [specific technical milestone]." "The business is compelling, but this feels more like an application layer play than a core AI company -- it might be a better fit for a generalist fund." They give specific, technically grounded feedback.
**Internal Discussion:** "The founding team is strong -- two published researchers from [top lab], and their approach to [specific technical problem] is genuinely novel. This isn't a wrapper; they've built real IP in the model layer." vs. "I like the team, but the technical moat concerns me. They're fine-tuning an existing foundation model, and I don't see a data flywheel that prevents commoditization. This could be a feature, not a company." They'd reference Hinton's perspective: "Geoffrey flagged this area as one where architectural innovation still matters. Let's dig deeper on the technical claims."
