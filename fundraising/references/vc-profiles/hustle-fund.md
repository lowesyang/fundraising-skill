## Hustle Fund

**Key Partners:** Elizabeth Yin (co-founder, GP), Eric Bahn (co-founder, GP)
**Stage Focus:** Pre-seed — they are one of the most active pre-seed micro funds in the world. They write the earliest checks, often before product-market fit, before revenue, sometimes before product.
**Typical Check Size:** $25K - $200K (small, fast checks via SAFEs — they are not leading rounds, they are catalyzing them)
**Investment Thesis:** Hustle Fund invests in hustle, not pedigree. Elizabeth and Eric believe the best predictor of startup success is not the founder's resume, not the TAM slide, and not the pitch deck — it's speed of execution. How fast can you ship? How fast can you learn from what you shipped? How fast can you iterate? They define "hustle" as learning velocity — the rate at which a founder runs experiments, absorbs signal, and changes course. This is not about grinding 100-hour weeks; it's about being ruthlessly efficient with time and capital. They intentionally back founders that traditional VCs overlook: first-time founders, non-Stanford founders, founders outside Silicon Valley, founders who don't have warm intros to Sand Hill Road.
**Portfolio Highlights:** Snackpass, Autobrains, Jasper (early check), Stacker, ShipBob (early), Thinkific, Retool (early), Pocus, Firstbase, Prequel, Wolffia Biotech, FairMarkit
**Recent Investments (2022-2025):** Gamma (AI-powered presentation platform that became a Hustle Fund unicorn, now valued at $1B+); Webflow (design-to-code website builder, also a unicorn in the Hustle Fund portfolio); Boom Supersonic (supersonic commercial aviation — one of three Hustle Fund unicorns); HoneyBook (client management software for creative entrepreneurs); Nova Credit (cross-border credit bureau enabling immigrants to use foreign credit history); NerdWallet (personal finance platform, now public on NASDAQ); Uniblock (blockchain API infrastructure for developers); Amari AI (AI-powered enterprise workflow automation).
**Sector Preferences:** Sector-agnostic. They care far more about the founder's execution speed than the market they're in. That said, they see a lot of SaaS, marketplaces, developer tools, fintech, and consumer. They follow the founder, not the thesis.

### Core Philosophy & First Principles

Hustle Fund's investing framework is the most operationally explicit of any pre-seed fund: Elizabeth Yin publishes the firm's actual scoring rubrics, partner-level disaggregations, and investment data on her blog, treating radical transparency as a competitive advantage for attracting founders who have done their homework. The published data shows the firm scores deals across four dimensions — Team (2.98 average), Market (2.79), Execution (2.76), and Product (2.32) — in that order of importance. Product is last. This is not an accident.

The core thesis is that **learning velocity** is the single best proxy for startup success available at the pre-seed stage. Not the TAM. Not the product. Not the pitch. How fast does the founder learn from each experiment and change course? This is operationalized through a specific question that functions as Hustle Fund's canonical filter: "What did you ship in the last two weeks?" The answer reveals more than a year's worth of deck review because it simultaneously tests execution speed, ability to prioritize, and willingness to launch before things are perfect.

Hustle Fund operates on a **champion model** — any individual partner can write a check without consensus, even when other partners disagree. Elizabeth Yin has noted that "outliers tend to be the most contentious," meaning the companies that become the biggest wins are often the ones the partnership argued about most. This model bets on high-conviction individual judgment over committee risk management.

The firm's anti-credentialism is structural, not rhetorical. They intentionally invest in founders from backgrounds that traditional seed funds filter out: non-Stanford founders, founders outside Silicon Valley, first-time founders, founders who don't have warm intros. This isn't a diversity program layered onto a normal investment process — it's the thesis. The belief is that underestimated founders, if they execute fast enough, compound advantages that pedigreed founders get for free. The chip-on-shoulder dynamic is something Hustle Fund actively looks for.

### Behavioral Model
**Meeting Style:** Fast, informal, zero pretense. Elizabeth and Eric don't do the "tell me about your journey" warmup — they jump straight into what you've built and what's working. Meetings are short (often 20-30 minutes). They treat the pitch itself as a data point: did you prepare efficiently? Can you communicate clearly under time pressure? There's no theater. No mahogany conference table energy. It feels like talking to a fellow founder at a coffee shop who happens to have a checkbook.
**Signature Questions:**
- "What did you ship in the last two weeks?" (the canonical Hustle Fund question — they want to see velocity, not plans)
- "How many customers did you talk to this week and what did you learn?" (signal over slides)
- "What's your unfair advantage — and be honest if you don't have one yet?" (they respect self-awareness over bluster)
- "If we gave you $100K today, what would you do in the next 30 days?" (testing for decisiveness and prioritization)
- "Why are you the person to build this, even if your resume doesn't say so?" (they actively fight credentialism)
**What Gets Them Excited:** Founders who have already shipped something — anything — and can show what they learned. A founder who launched three experiments in two weeks beats a founder who spent three months perfecting a pitch deck. Scrappiness. Revenue on a shoestring. Founders who bootstrapped to first customers before raising. People who treat fundraising as fuel, not validation. Founders from underestimated backgrounds who have a chip on their shoulder and something to prove.
**Red Flags For Them:** Slow movers. Founders who have been "working on the idea" for six months with nothing live. Over-planners who want everything perfect before launching. Trend-chasers who pivoted to AI/crypto/whatever is hot without genuine conviction. Founders who name-drop instead of showing metrics. People who confuse activity with progress. Anyone who treats pre-seed fundraising like a Series B process.
**Decision Process:** Extremely fast — they can and do invest on the same day as the first meeting. They use standardized SAFE notes (no negotiation on terms, no legal back-and-forth). Elizabeth and Eric make decisions together but don't require a full partnership vote or multi-week diligence process. Their bar is: does this founder move fast, and do we believe they'll keep moving fast? If yes, wire the money and get out of the way.
**Unique Quirks:** They run Angel Squad, a paid program teaching aspiring angel investors how to evaluate and invest in startups — which means they've built an entire ecosystem around demystifying early-stage investing. Elizabeth has one of the largest VC followings on Twitter/X and writes extensively about fundraising mechanics, which means many founders come to Hustle Fund already educated on their philosophy. They are radically transparent about how VC works — publishing their fund metrics, rejection reasons, and decision frameworks publicly. Their brand is anti-gatekeeping. They also have a Scout program and fund-of-funds strategy, making them a node in a massive pre-seed network.

### Founder & Culture Fit (Non-Obvious Signals)
- **Bias toward action that's already visible**: Hustle Fund doesn't wait for polished products — they're looking for founders who have already taken action before asking for money. Someone who ran three customer interviews last week beats someone who has been refining a business plan for three months.
- **Self-aware about their own knowledge gaps**: Elizabeth Yin specifically values founders who answer "What's your unfair advantage?" with honesty if they don't yet have one. Founders who bluster through weakness questions signal low self-awareness — which predicts slow learning.
- **Emotional durability under rejection**: Hustle Fund backs founders who are "undeterred" — their internal language for founders who hear "no" and continue building rather than stalling. This isn't about grinding; it's about whether rejection functions as data (iterate) or damage (spiral).
- **Communication efficiency under time pressure**: The meeting format itself is a test. If a founder can't communicate clearly in 25 minutes, Hustle Fund reads that as a signal about sales efficiency, hiring conversations, and every future pitch. Founders who bring polished decks to a 20-minute conversation have already miscalibrated.
- **Chip on shoulder without chip on shoulder energy**: Hustle Fund actively backs founders from underestimated backgrounds, but the specific cultural fit signal is founders who are motivated by the mission, not by proving critics wrong. The drive should be constructive, not reactive.
- **Bootstrapped or scrappy past**: Founders who built anything — even a freelance business, a side project, a community, a small service business — before building a venture-scale startup demonstrate risk tolerance and resourcefulness that Hustle Fund treats as durable evidence of execution DNA.
- **Speed of response to Hustle Fund itself**: Elizabeth Yin has noted publicly that how quickly founders respond to their emails and follow up is itself a signal. Founders who take three days to reply to a term sheet question are failing the velocity test in real time.

### For the /pitch Simulator
**Opening Style:** No fluff. Elizabeth or Eric will say something like "Hey — we've got 25 minutes, let's dive in. What are you building and what's happening with it right now?" They might skip the deck entirely and just ask questions. If you try to start with a long market-sizing slide, they'll politely fast-forward you. They want to hear about what exists today, not what might exist in five years.
**Push-back Patterns:** They push on execution speed, not market theory. "You've been working on this for four months — why don't you have users yet?" or "You said you're going to launch in Q3 — why not next week?" They'll also challenge founders who seem to be fundraising as a substitute for building: "Have you considered just getting three paying customers instead of raising a round?" Their pushback is direct but never cruel — it's the tone of a founder who's been there giving you real talk.
**Closing Signals:**
- *Positive:* "Let me tell you how our SAFEs work" (they're already in deal mode). "Can you send me your bank details?" (yes, they move that fast). "I want to introduce you to someone in our portfolio who's solving a similar distribution problem." Follow-up email within 24 hours with specific next steps. Elizabeth sharing your product on Twitter.
- *Negative:* "This is interesting — let's check back in a few months when you have more traction." (polite pass). "I think you need to ship more before we can evaluate this." If they don't follow up within 48 hours, it's a no — they don't ghost, but their speed cuts both ways. "Have you considered applying to an accelerator first?"
**Internal Discussion:** *Bull case:* "This founder shipped a working product in two weeks on a $500 budget. They already have 30 users giving feedback. They don't have the 'right' background but they clearly don't need permission to build. This is exactly who we back — fast, scrappy, undeterred. Let's wire the SAFE today." *Bear case:* "Smart founder, good idea, but they've been talking about this for months and all they have is a Figma prototype and a waitlist. No experiments. No user conversations. They're optimizing for fundraising when they should be optimizing for learning. Let's pass and tell them to come back when they've shipped something and talked to 50 customers."
