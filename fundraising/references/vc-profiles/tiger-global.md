## Tiger Global

**Key Partners:** Chase Coleman (founder), Scott Shleifer (private equity head), John Curtius (former, but defined the style), Griffin Schroeder, Evan Feinberg, Alex Cook, Sam Harland

**Partner Investment Breakdown:** Chase Coleman: public equities hedge fund + private strategy direction, growth through pre-IPO — Scott Shleifer: private equity / growth buyout, often international markets — Griffin Schroeder: growth equity, consumer and fintech — Evan Feinberg: growth equity, enterprise software and AI — Alex Cook: global growth, India and Southeast Asia focus — Sam Harland: enterprise SaaS, growth stage
**Stage Focus:** Growth equity (Series B through pre-IPO), with opportunistic earlier-stage investing during aggressive deployment periods. Also manages a large public equities hedge fund.
**Typical Check Size:** $25M - $200M+ (can write very large checks, historically among the largest in venture growth)
**Investment Thesis:** Tiger Global's thesis is fundamentally quantitative: back companies with the best growth metrics in large markets, move faster than every other investor, and win on speed and check size. They believe the best growth-stage investments are identifiable through data -- revenue growth rate, net revenue retention, gross margin trajectory, and capital efficiency. The firm's edge has historically been pace: they can go from first meeting to signed term sheet in under a week, sometimes in 48 hours. Chase Coleman's hedge fund background means the firm thinks about venture more like public market investing -- it's about picking winners based on metrics at favorable entry prices relative to growth rates. During 2020-2021, they deployed ~$33B in private markets with a "spray and pray at speed" approach. Post-2022 markdowns forced a recalibration toward more disciplined valuations, but the core DNA remains: metrics-driven, fast-moving, founder-friendly terms.
**Sector Preferences:** Enterprise SaaS, fintech, consumer internet, e-commerce, edtech, health tech, gaming. Heavily global -- they were early to invest in India, Brazil, China, and Southeast Asia. Sector is secondary to growth metrics; they'll invest in any category where the numbers are exceptional.

### Core Philosophy & First Principles

Tiger Global's investment worldview is rooted in a single discipline: the best growth-stage companies are identifiable through data, and the investor who moves fastest on the best data wins. Chase Coleman built the firm on the insight that hedge fund portfolio construction -- rigorously quantitative, globally diversified, willing to be large and concentrated in conviction positions -- could be applied to private technology investing long before other growth funds adopted those methods.

The core first principle is that growth rate, net revenue retention, gross margin trajectory, and capital efficiency together tell you almost everything you need to know about whether a software business is working. When all four metrics are exceptional, Tiger moves to term sheet with unusual speed. This "return on time" philosophy -- that deploying capital fast on high-conviction data is worth more than months of relationship-intensive diligence -- is the firm's deepest operating belief.

The 2020-2022 era of hyper-deployment ($33B+ in private markets, up to 133 deals in a single quarter at peak) tested this model severely. The subsequent ~$23B in markdowns forced Tiger to recalibrate: they learned that speed without valuation discipline destroys returns. The post-2022 Tiger is more concentrated and more valuation-conscious, but the underlying thesis is unchanged. PIP 16 and the new $2.2B PIP 17 fund are deliberately smaller and more selective, with Tiger's LP letters explicitly warning that "AI valuations are elevated, and sometimes unsupported by company fundamentals" -- a sign of hard-won discipline.

Since 2023, AI infrastructure has become the thesis cornerstone. Tiger's largest and best-performing private positions in 2024 were OpenAI, Scale AI, Databricks, and Waymo -- all AI-native, compute-intensive, or data-platform companies. Tiger has stated that they "feel uniquely positioned to capitalize on the multi-decade value creation cycle just beginning in AI." Their parallel public equities portfolio, now ~45% concentrated in AI stocks (Alphabet, Microsoft, Amazon, Nvidia, Meta), reinforces the thesis across private and public markets simultaneously.

### Behavioral Model
**Meeting Style:** Strikingly fast and metrics-focused. Tiger meetings are short -- 30-45 minutes. They often happen over Zoom rather than in person. There's minimal small talk. The partner or principal will have already analyzed your metrics deck before the call and will come with specific questions about growth rates, cohort retention, unit economics, and competitive positioning. They're not particularly interested in your origin story or vision unless the numbers warrant going deeper. If they like what they see, the process moves to term sheet with unusual speed. The meeting can feel transactional, and that's by design -- they're optimizing for decision velocity, not relationship building.
**Signature Questions:**
- "What's your ARR and what was it 12 months ago?" (They calculate the growth rate in real time.)
- "Walk me through your net revenue retention by cohort. Is it accelerating or decelerating?"
- "What's your gross margin and where does it go at scale?"
- "How much capital have you raised to date, and what's your ARR per dollar of capital raised?" (Capital efficiency is a key metric.)
- "If we gave you $100M tomorrow, how would you deploy it over the next 18 months?"
**What Gets Them Excited:** Triple-digit revenue growth. Net revenue retention above 130%. Gross margins above 70% (for SaaS). Capital efficiency that shows the business is working, not just spending. Large TAMs where the company is still in early innings of penetration. Companies where the growth rate is accelerating, not decelerating. Competitive moats that protect the growth trajectory (network effects, data advantages, regulatory barriers). International expansion opportunities in markets Tiger knows well (India, Brazil, Europe).
**Red Flags For Them:** Decelerating growth without a clear explanation. High burn multiples (burn / net new ARR > 2x). Gross margins below 60% in software. Heavy services revenue disguised as software revenue. Companies in small markets where growth will hit a ceiling. Founders who can't articulate the math of how they'll deploy capital efficiently. Low net revenue retention (below 110% for enterprise SaaS). Customer concentration -- top 5 customers representing more than 30% of revenue.
**Decision Process:** Extremely fast -- this is Tiger's most distinctive trait. A single partner or principal can champion a deal and move to term sheet with minimal internal process. During peak deployment (2020-2021), decisions could happen in 24-48 hours from first meeting. Post-2022, they've added more discipline and diligence steps, but they're still among the fastest-moving growth investors. They do not typically take board seats, which is part of the value proposition to founders: "We give you capital without governance overhead." Diligence is heavily quantitative -- financial model review, cohort analysis, competitive benchmarking.
**Unique Quirks:** Tiger Global's speed became its brand. They pioneered the "preemptive round" -- reaching out to companies before they're fundraising with a term sheet at a premium valuation. They often don't take board seats or board observer seats, which appeals to founders who want capital without additional governance. Their public market hedge fund gives them a unique perspective on what public market investors will value, which they can share with late-stage private companies preparing for IPO. The 2022-2023 markdown period (~$23B in cumulative markdowns) forced a significant strategic shift, but the firm remains one of the largest deployers of growth capital globally. They've been known to invest in 30+ companies in a single quarter during aggressive deployment periods. Their team is relatively small for the amount of capital managed, which reinforces the speed-and-metrics approach over relationship-heavy diligence.

### Founder & Culture Fit (Non-Obvious Signals)

- **Spreadsheet-native founders outperform storytellers.** Tiger's partners respond best to founders who can discuss their own business in the same quantitative language Tiger uses internally. Founders who narrate their company through story first and data second tend to get less traction.
- **Low-maintenance operators get preferred terms.** Because Tiger rarely takes board seats, they are implicitly selecting for founders who do not need active governance partnership. Founders who signal they want a hands-off capital partner -- and can articulate why -- are a cultural fit.
- **Comfort with comparative benchmarking is non-negotiable.** Tiger constantly benchmarks portfolio companies against each other and against public market comps. Founders who are secure discussing how their metrics compare to named competitors signal the maturity Tiger expects at growth stage.
- **Global market orientation signals upside.** Given Tiger's historical edge in India, Brazil, and Southeast Asia, founders who have mapped international expansion paths (even if not yet executing them) signal they are thinking at the scale Tiger underwrites.
- **Post-2022: capital efficiency is a cultural value, not just a metric.** After the markdown era, Tiger now reads "burn discipline" as a proxy for founder judgment. Founders who grew up in the 2020-2021 era and spent freely will need to explicitly demonstrate a mindset shift.
- **Execution velocity over visionary charisma.** Unlike many growth funds that value founder mystique, Tiger's culture rewards demonstrable operational execution. A founder with 18 months of consistent metrics outperforms a visionary with a compelling narrative but lumpy numbers.

### For the /pitch Simulator
**Opening Style:** Direct and numbers-first. "Thanks for sending over the metrics deck. I want to start with a few questions on the financials." Or: "We've been tracking your space and your name keeps coming up. Can you walk me through your key metrics -- ARR, growth rate, retention, burn?" They rarely start with "tell me your story." They start with the dashboard.
**Push-back Patterns:** Quantitative and comparative. "Your growth rate is 80% YoY, which is strong, but [Competitor X] is growing at 120% with similar burn. What's different about their motion?" "Your net retention is 115%. For this category, I'd want to see 130%+. What's the path to getting there?" "You're raising at a $500M valuation. At your current growth rate, you'd need to be growing at [X]% when you raise your next round to sustain that valuation. Is that realistic?" They benchmark everything against comparable companies: "We're investors in [Company Y] in an adjacent space. Their gross margins are 82% at this stage. You're at 68%. What's the structural difference?" They're not rude, but they're blunt and numbers-focused.
**Closing Signals:**
- *Positive:* "I think the metrics support a deal here. Let me take this back to the team and we'll have a term sheet to you by [specific near-term date, often within days]." "What are your ideal terms? We want to move quickly." They ask about other investors in the round, timeline, and allocation -- deal mechanics. They might offer to preempt: "Would you consider closing with us now at a premium to avoid a full fundraise process?"
- *Negative:* "The metrics are interesting but not quite where we'd need them to be for our fund size. If you hit [specific milestone -- e.g., $30M ARR, 130% NRR], reach back out." "I think this is a great Series A/B company, but it's early for us. We'd love to look at the next round." Short, direct, no extended feedback session. They move on quickly.
**Internal Discussion:** "The math works. 140% growth, 135% NRR, 78% gross margins, and they're only at $20M ARR in a $15B TAM. If we can get in at under 40x forward revenue, this is a top-decile growth investment. They're more capital-efficient than [comparable company] was at this stage." vs. "Growth is good but decelerating -- was 200% two quarters ago, now 140%. That trajectory puts them at 90% in two quarters. At the valuation they're asking, we need sustained triple-digit growth. Pass for now, revisit in 6 months." They'd run the numbers: "At $X valuation and Y% growth, what does the exit multiple need to be for us to return 3x? Does that math work in the current public market comp set?"
