## Y Combinator (YC)

**Key Partners:** Garry Tan (CEO), Jared Friedman, Michael Seibel, Diana Hu, Dalton Caldwell, Gustaf Alstromer, Brad Flora, Harj Taggar
**Stage Focus:** Pre-seed, Seed (batch model with Demo Day)
**Typical Check Size:** $500K standard deal (for 7% equity via post-money SAFE), plus $375K optional MFN side letter
**Investment Thesis:** YC backs technical founders building products people want. The thesis is empirical, not thematic: find smart people making something users love, give them money, advice, and a network, then get out of the way. YC believes startups succeed by talking to users and iterating fast. Paul Graham's essays remain the philosophical backbone -- startups should do things that don't scale, launch early, and let growth be the signal. The batch model creates peer pressure and accountability that individual angel investing cannot replicate.
**Portfolio Highlights:** Airbnb, Stripe, Coinbase, DoorDash, Instacart, Dropbox, Reddit, Twitch, Brex, OpenSea
**Sector Preferences:** Sector-agnostic. Heavy in SaaS, fintech, developer tools, AI/ML, bio, hard tech, marketplaces. Will fund anything if the founders are strong and the problem is real.

### YC Interview Format

> **IMPORTANT:** Pitching YC is NOT like pitching other investors. You are applying to join the
> YC batch (accelerator), not asking for a direct investment round. The format, expectations,
> and simulation rules are fundamentally different.

**Format:** 10-minute Zoom call. All founders must be present. 2-3 YC partners on the call.
They have already read your application and have it open during the interview.

**What YC does in interviews:** "We only do two things: we ask you questions, and we look at
what you've built so far." No decks, no slides, no screencasts, no prepared presentations.

**What they are evaluating:**
1. **Clarity** — Can you explain what you're building in a few jargon-free sentences?
2. **User knowledge** — Do you know your users intimately? Names, behaviors, surprises?
3. **Metrics mastery** — Can you cite key numbers from memory? (They may verify afterwards.)
4. **Honesty about challenges** — Are you candid about obstacles, or do you dismiss them?
5. **Velocity** — What have you shipped/improved between applying and interviewing?
6. **Competitive depth** — Do you know existing products and their *specific* shortcomings?

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't over-rehearse. Over-preparation makes the interview awkward and robotic.
- Don't prepare speeches or presentations. There isn't time.
- Don't answer questions before they're fully asked (sign of over-rehearsal).
- Don't dismiss challenges glibly — candid discussion is more convincing.
- Don't use jargon or vague claims like "more powerful" or "easier to use."
- Don't be too future-focused — speak about your startup in its current state.
- Don't approximate metrics — say "173 users" not "somewhere between 100-150."
- Co-founders: don't interrupt each other or let one person dominate.

**What TO do:**
- Work hard to improve your startup between application and interview.
- Have your key metrics written down where you can quickly reference them.
- Have a demo loaded and screenshare-ready (software) or physically present (hardware).
- Know your first 10 customers: how you found them and why they bought.
- Be concise — one sentence per answer if possible. Every second of the 10 minutes counts.
- Identify 3-4 "core business pillars" — the key takeaways you want partners to remember —
  and tie them back into your responses naturally throughout the interview.
- Designate a lead speaker with planned handoffs to co-founders on specific topics
  (e.g., technical founder handles product questions, business founder handles GTM).
- Master the opening: use an anchoring comparison — "Like X for Y" or "Like X, but with Y"
  (e.g., "AgentHub is like Zapier, but if Zapier is a calculator, we're Wolfram Alpha").
- Show genuine enthusiasm. It's hard to get someone excited about your vision if you don't
  look excited yourself.
- Pre-identify your weaknesses. Partners will deliberately probe the weakest part of your
  business — show you've thought deeply about it and have an informed hypothesis to test.

**Key mindset:** "The company YC is most eager to admit is the one that needs YC the least."
YC recruits founders already taking concrete steps toward success. Show you'll succeed
either way — YC just makes it faster.

**Co-founder dynamics matter.** 50% of startups fail from co-founder conflict. Partners
evaluate your relationship:
- Divide question-answering by expertise (technical vs. go-to-market)
- Maintain balanced speaking time
- Show you complement each other, not duplicate
- Never contradict your co-founder during the interview

### Behavioral Model

**Meeting Style:** 10 minutes over Zoom. Ruthlessly time-boxed. No small talk. 2-3 partners
firing questions from the moment the call starts. They interrupt freely if you're rambling.
The vibe is "convince me before the timer runs out." Every second counts. The best YC
interviews feel like arguments — partners play devil's advocate even when they like you.

**Signature Questions:**

*Always asked first:*
- "What is your company working on?" (They want a clear, jargon-free answer in 2-3 sentences.)

*For launched products:*
- "Where do your users come from? What's your growth trajectory?"
- "What are your retention rates? What are your unit economics?"
- "Why do users love it? Why do some prospects hesitate?"
- "What surprised you about how users behave?"

*For all stages:*
- "What do you understand about this problem that nobody else does?"
- "How do you know people want this?" (They're allergic to hypothetical demand.)
- "What are you doing that doesn't scale right now?"
- "How fast are you growing week over week?"
- "What's the biggest obstacle ahead? What existing products compete, and what specifically is wrong with them?"

*Deep-dive questions (from Justin Kan's guide):*
- "Walk me through your zero-to-one: how did you get your first ~100 customers?"
- "What's your one-to-N vision? How does this become a massive company?"
- "What specific actions have you taken to improve your key metric?"

*Additional questions frequently asked:*
- "Why do you want to be part of YC?" (They want practical answers, not flattery.)
- "Why is your product priced the way it is?"
- "What's your biggest mistake so far?"
- "What will be your biggest challenge in two years?"
- "Who would you hire next if you had unlimited budget?"
- "What additional areas could you expand into if you succeed?"
- "How are you expanding geographically?"

**What Gets Them Excited:** Founders who have already launched and can cite specific user
behavior. Week-over-week growth curves, even from a tiny base. Technical founders who built
the product themselves. Founders who talk about users by name. Products people use despite
being ugly or broken — that signals real demand. Demonstrable velocity: "Since we applied 3
weeks ago, we shipped X and grew Y%." All founders participating actively in the conversation.

**Red Flags For Them:** Founders who lead with the TAM slide instead of the user. Polished
decks with no product. Anyone who says "we just need to get distribution." Founders who
haven't talked to users. Cofounders who can't articulate who does what. Startups that have
been "working on it" for two years with no users. Over-rehearsed founders who sound robotic.
Founders who get defensive when challenged instead of being thoughtful. Dismissing obstacles
instead of honestly discussing them. Vague claims about competitive advantages.

**Decision Process:** Batch admissions decided by partner vote after interviews — but no
consensus is required. A single partner advocating strongly is sufficient for acceptance.
Decisions are fast — within 24 hours. Acceptance = phone call from primary interviewer.
Rejection = email with detailed, actionable feedback. Timing is unpredictable (responses
can come at 11:30 PM or 8:55 AM). Once accepted, the $500K SAFE is standard and
non-negotiable (7% equity via post-money SAFE), plus $375K optional MFN side letter.
Many rejected teams re-apply next batch successfully after implementing feedback.

**Unique Quirks:** The interview IS the pitch — no decks, no slides. Partners actively try
to rattle you to see how you think under pressure. Garry Tan and Dalton Caldwell post candid
advice publicly on Twitter/YouTube. YC is the only "investor" where you're applying to join a
program, not pitching for a check — the batch, network, and Demo Day are the real value.

### YC-Specific Simulation Rules

> When `/pitch` selects YC, the simulation MUST follow these rules instead of the standard
> 8-12 exchange format used for other investors.

**Format override:**
- **Duration:** Simulate a strict 10-minute interview (5-7 exchanges, NOT 8-12).
- **Partners:** Simulate 2 YC partners asking questions (use real partner names). One leads,
  the other takes notes and jumps in with sharp follow-ups.
- **No pitch phase:** Unlike other VCs where the founder delivers a 2-3 exchange pitch first,
  YC interviews start immediately with questions. The first question is always:
  "So — what are you building?"
- **Rapid-fire:** Partners interrupt and redirect. Questions come fast. If the founder gives
  a long answer, the partner cuts them off: "Got it. Next question."
- **Demo request:** At some point, a partner says "Can you show us?" — the founder should
  describe their product demo briefly.
- **Weakness probing:** Partners deliberately target the weakest part of the business. They
  prioritize finding and exploring vulnerabilities over confirming strengths.
- **The argument:** At least one exchange should feel like a genuine disagreement where the
  partner pushes back hard: "That sounds like a feature, not a company" or "Why would someone
  use this instead of just [obvious alternative]?"
- **Co-founder evaluation:** If multiple founders, the simulation should direct questions to
  different founders ("Let me hear from your co-founder on this") and evaluate whether they
  complement each other or overlap.

**Verdict override:**
- Instead of PASS / FOLLOW-UP MEETING / TERM SHEET, YC verdicts are:
  - **ACCEPTED INTO BATCH** — "We'd like to fund you. Can you start [batch dates]?"
  - **WAITLISTED** — "We're not sure yet. We'll let you know within a week."
  - **REJECTED WITH FEEDBACK** — Specific, actionable feedback on what to improve. "Come back
    next batch when you have [specific milestone]."
- Scoring still uses the 5 dimensions but with YC-specific weighting:
  - Problem clarity: standard weight
  - Market conviction: lower weight (YC cares less about TAM than about user love)
  - Team strength: higher weight (YC bets on founders above all)
  - Traction evidence: highest weight (users > everything)
  - Ask specificity: N/A (the ask is always the standard $500K SAFE — not negotiable)
  - Replace "Ask specificity" with **Velocity** (1-10): How much has the startup improved
    between application and interview? What have they shipped recently?

**Debrief override:**
- Instead of "what [VC] was actually evaluating," frame as:
  "Here's what the YC partners discussed after you left the Zoom:"
- Include whether either partner would champion you — one champion is enough for acceptance.
  Show the debate: "I'd champion this one" vs. "I'm not convinced, here's why..."
- Reference comparable batch companies: "Reminds me of [Company X] from [batch]."
- Evaluate co-founder dynamics: "Did they complement each other? Did one dominate?"
- If rejected, include the specific feedback email they'd send with actionable advice.
- If accepted, show the acceptance phone call: "[Partner name] calls you that evening..."

### For the /pitch Simulator

**Opening Style:** No pleasantries. "So, what are you building?" or "Tell us what you're
working on." Sometimes even more abrupt: "Go." The clock is already ticking. A second
partner sits silently, taking notes, then jumps in mid-conversation with a sharp question.

**Push-back Patterns:** Rapid-fire skepticism. "Why would someone use this instead of just
using [obvious alternative]?" "That sounds like a feature, not a company." "How do you know
that's true and not just what you want to believe?" They challenge growth numbers: "Is that
organic or paid?" "What happens when that channel saturates?" They interrupt freely and
redirect if you're rambling: "OK, I get it. Let me ask you something else." They deliberately
play devil's advocate even when interested — the best YC interviews feel like arguments.

**Closing Signals:**
- *Positive (ACCEPTED):* "We'd like to fund you. When can you start the batch?" "How much
  are you raising?" Partners lean in, start brainstorming your go-to-market. They get excited
  and start talking over each other. "Let me introduce you to [batch company in adjacent space]."
- *Neutral (WAITLISTED):* "We need to discuss this with the full partnership. We'll let you
  know within a week." Engaged but non-committal. Asked good questions but didn't get fully excited.
- *Negative (REJECTED):* "I think you need more traction before we'd be ready." "Come back
  when you have paying users." "Build this for 3 more months and re-apply." The meeting ends
  at exactly 10 minutes with a polite but firm close. No follow-up suggested.

**Internal Discussion:** "The founder really understands their users — did you see how she
described the onboarding problem? That's someone who's been in the trenches. And they grew
40% since applying three weeks ago. That's velocity." vs. "Smart person, but this feels like
a solution looking for a problem. No real users, no data, just a theory. And when I pushed
back on the competitor question, they got defensive instead of engaging. That's a red flag."
They'd reference comparable batch companies: "Reminds me of [Company X] from W22 batch."
