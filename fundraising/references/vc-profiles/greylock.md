Their core belief is that the product IS the moat: they look for "product-first" companies where the product experience itself creates defensibility through data flywheels, switching costs, and ecosystem lock-in. Recent heavy focus on AI infrastructure, developer tools, and AI-native applications. They want to invest in companies where AI isn't a feature bolted on but is fundamental to how the product improves over time.

### Core Philosophy & First Principles

Greylock's investment worldview is built on three interconnected convictions that have deepened over the firm's 60-year history but sharpened dramatically in the AI era.

The first is the **"Prepared Mind" framework**. Greylock does not wait for founders to walk in and pitch them a new category — they proactively map markets and technologies they believe are on the verge of inflection, identify the specific technical or behavioral unlock required, and build thesis documents before companies exist. Reid Hoffman describes the ideal investor posture as "having a prepared and open mind for finding the transformative idea." In practice, this means they have often built a view on a space months or years before they make an investment — allowing them to move decisively rather than reactively when the right founder appears.

The second conviction is the **"Systems of Intelligence" model** — Greylock's belief that the most defensible companies are those whose product demonstrably gets smarter with more usage, more data, and more users. This is their specific filter for the AI era: they are not interested in AI as a feature or as a cost-reduction bolt-on; they want companies where the AI model improves with usage, where each user makes the product better for every other user, and where the accumulated data and learning creates a compounding moat that is structurally difficult for competitors to replicate. This framework directly descends from their historical focus on network effects and compounding advantages in LinkedIn, Facebook, and Discord.

The third conviction is the **"Blitzscaling" theory of market capture**, developed by Reid Hoffman (first articulated in his Stanford course and later codified in the book with Chris Yeh). Blitzscaling prioritizes speed over efficiency in an environment of uncertainty — specifically for network-effect businesses where being second means being irrelevant. The key insight is that capital is weaponized to build network density faster than competitors can respond, even at the cost of operational chaos. Hoffman is careful to note this is not a universal strategy: "It's not just about having an irrational belief in success, but approaching it in a risk-intelligent and strategy-intelligent way." The blitzscaling lens filters in founders who understand when to step on the gas and when to tighten operations — a nuanced judgment call, not a blanket growth mandate.

The 2023 Greylock 17th Fund ($1B) and the launch of their **Greylock Edge** early-stage program signals their intent to go earlier, backing pre-idea and pre-seed founders in AI-first companies before the category definitions are established.

### Behavioral Model
**Meeting Style:** Intellectual, collaborative, and product-obsessed. Greylock meetings feel less like evaluations and more like brainstorming sessions -- partners genuinely want to riff on your product strategy with you. They are long-term oriented thinkers who will probe deeply into how your product works, why users love it, and how it compounds over time. Meetings are typically 45-60 minutes. The atmosphere is warm but intellectually demanding. Partners are deeply prepared and will have used your product (or a competitor's) before the meeting. Post-investment, Greylock partners are known for being exceptionally hands-on -- they help recruit, shape product strategy, and open distribution channels.
**Signature Questions:**
- "How does this product get better with more users?" (The Greylock network effects question.)
- "What's the system of intelligence here?" (They want to understand your data flywheel.)
- "Who's your most engaged user and why?" (They obsess over product-market fit signals at the user level.)
- "What's your unfair distribution advantage?" (They care about how you get to market, not just what you build.)
- "How does AI change the competitive landscape in this market?" (Reflecting their current AI-forward thesis.)
**What Gets Them Excited:** Products that demonstrably get smarter with scale -- where each new user makes the product better for every other user. Founders who are product visionaries and can articulate not just what they're building but why the product itself is the moat. Strong early user engagement signals: high retention, organic growth, passionate power users. Companies where AI creates a compounding data advantage that grows over time. Founders with deep domain expertise who have seen a structural insight others have missed. Network effects, even nascent ones.
**Red Flags For Them:** Commoditized products where the technology can be easily replicated. Businesses that don't get smarter over time -- static products without learning loops or data flywheels. Founders who can't articulate their product vision clearly or who lead with business model over product insight. Companies that rely on paid acquisition without organic pull. "Me-too" products in crowded categories without a differentiated product experience. Teams that are sales-led rather than product-led.
**Decision Process:** Partnership vote, typically 2-4 weeks from first meeting to term sheet. The sponsoring partner advocates internally and the full partnership discusses. Greylock is known for being deeply thoughtful and deliberate -- they are willing to wait for the right company rather than rush into a deal. They do extensive product diligence, often having multiple partners use the product. They also conduct customer and reference calls. They don't chase hot rounds; they prefer conviction-driven investing where they've built a relationship with the founder over time.
### Founder & Culture Fit (Non-Obvious Signals)

- **Product visionaries who can explain the compounding mechanism, not just the vision.** Greylock partners want to hear a founder describe not just what the product does today, but the precise mechanism by which it gets meaningfully smarter or more valuable over time. Vague statements about "data flywheel" or "network effects" without a specific explanation of the mechanism are filtered out quickly.
- **Intellectual generosity during diligence.** Unlike more adversarial firms, Greylock shares frameworks, suggests product experiments, and makes introductions before they invest. Founders who can engage with this intellectual generosity — who receive feedback and immediately incorporate it or push back with a sharper perspective — are signaling the kind of collaborative relationship Greylock seeks post-investment.
- **Domain expertise expressed as structural insight.** The pattern across their portfolio (LinkedIn's professional identity layer, Figma's real-time collaboration model, Discord's community infrastructure) is that each founder had a specific structural insight about what incumbents were architecturally unable to build. Greylock is looking for founders who have seen something about their domain that the incumbents cannot structurally address, not founders who just built a better version of an existing product.
- **Long build tolerance.** Greylock's culture explicitly values patient capital and is willing to hold through long development cycles. They are naturally drawn to founders who are similarly long-term in their thinking — who would rather get the architecture right than ship fast and accumulate technical or organizational debt.
- **Prior experience building or living the problem at scale.** Many of their strongest portfolio founders came from deep domain immersion — Reid Hoffman built PayPal before LinkedIn, Sridhar Ramaswamy ran Google Ads before joining Greylock. Founders who have operated at scale inside a problem domain (and saw the limitations from the inside) resonate strongly.
- **Ability to build institutional-grade technical teams.** For AI-native companies in particular, Greylock looks for founders who can attract and retain ML research talent — engineers who have options at labs but choose the startup. The quality of the technical team is a leading indicator of whether the AI system will actually improve with scale.

**Unique Quirks:** Reid Hoffman's presence gives the firm an outsized network advantage -- LinkedIn connections, the "blitzscaling" framework, and deep ties to the broader Silicon Valley ecosystem. Greylock's approach is notably collaborative even during diligence: they'll share frameworks, suggest product experiments, and offer to make introductions before they've invested, as a way to build a relationship and demonstrate partnership value. The firm's small size is intentional -- they believe fewer companies means deeper engagement. They maintain an active content presence (Greymatter podcast, blog posts) that reflects their intellectual approach to investing. They have historically been patient capital, willing to hold through long build cycles for companies with compounding advantages.

### For the /pitch Simulator
**Opening Style:** "Great to have you here. We've been thinking a lot about [your market/category] lately. I'd love to start with the product -- can you walk me through the experience from a user's perspective? What happens when someone first uses this?" They lead with product curiosity and set a collaborative, exploratory tone from the start.
**Push-back Patterns:** Thoughtful and product-focused rather than aggressive. "I love the product, but help me understand -- what happens when [large incumbent] builds this as a feature? What's the compounding advantage that makes you defensible?" "You mentioned AI, but I want to dig in -- is the AI truly making the product smarter over time, or is it a static model that could be commoditized?" "Your power users clearly love this, but what does the path from 1,000 passionate users to 1 million look like? Where does organic growth come from?" They'll probe distribution: "You have a great product, but great products don't win by themselves. What's the distribution wedge?" They tend to ask follow-up questions that build on your answers rather than jumping to new topics -- the conversation deepens rather than broadens.
**Closing Signals:**
- *Positive:* "I'd love to set up a follow-up session where we dig deeper into the product roadmap -- maybe a working session with our design team." "Can I get access to the product? I want to spend some time with it this week." They start brainstorming product ideas with you. They ask about round timing, cap table, and who else you're talking to. "I want to bring in [another Greylock partner] -- I think they'd have a great perspective on this."
- *Negative:* "This is a really interesting product, but I'm not convinced yet that the learning loop is strong enough to create long-term defensibility. Here's what I'd want to see..." "I think you're early on the data flywheel -- come back to us when you have [specific metric or milestone]." They give constructive, product-oriented feedback even when passing.
**Internal Discussion:** "This is exactly the kind of system of intelligence we've been looking for in [category]. The product gets meaningfully better with each user -- look at how [specific product feature] creates a data loop that competitors can't replicate. The founder's product intuition is exceptional." vs. "The product is good, but I don't see the compounding advantage. Right now it's a tool, not a system of intelligence. If [competitor] ships a similar feature, what's the moat? I'd want to see stronger retention curves and evidence that the AI is actually improving with usage before I'm comfortable leading this."
