## Intel Capital

**Key Partners/Team:** Mark Rostick (VP, Intel Capital -- leads investment strategy and portfolio management), Anthony Lin (Senior Managing Director -- focuses on AI, cloud, and data infrastructure investments), Nick Washburn (Managing Director -- enterprise software and AI applications). The team operates as sector specialists embedded across Intel's technology divisions, with deep domain expertise in semiconductors, AI hardware, networking, and autonomous systems. Investment decisions involve both the venture team and Intel's relevant technology groups.
**Stage Focus:** Seed through growth stage. One of the oldest and largest corporate venture capital arms in the world -- $16B+ invested in 1,800+ companies over 30+ years. The sweet spot is Series A and Series B companies building compute-intensive technology where Intel hardware and silicon play a meaningful role. They invest earlier than most CVCs (including seed), especially in semiconductor ecosystem companies and AI infrastructure. At growth stage, they participate in larger rounds for companies with proven Intel hardware integration and scaling compute needs.
**Typical Check Size:** $2M - $50M. Seed investments are typically $2M-$5M, Series A investments $5M-$15M, and growth rounds up to $50M. They co-invest alongside financial VCs and are comfortable as either lead or co-investor depending on strategic alignment.
**Investment Thesis:** Invest in companies that grow the demand for Intel silicon and computing infrastructure. This is the foundational logic -- if your company succeeds, Intel sells more chips. The thesis spans AI (inference, edge AI, hardware optimization), autonomous vehicles, cloud and data center infrastructure, 5G/networking, and the broader semiconductor ecosystem. With Intel competing aggressively against NVIDIA in AI chips (Gaudi accelerators, Xeon processors with AI acceleration, FPGAs), Intel Capital heavily backs companies that validate Intel's AI hardware story. Every investment is evaluated through the lens: does this company expand Intel's addressable market, strengthen Intel's competitive position, or create new demand for Intel silicon? The portfolio functions as a strategic map of where Intel believes computing is heading and where it needs ecosystem partners to win.
**Portfolio Highlights:** MongoDB (database), DocuSign (e-signatures), Cloudera (big data), ClearML (MLOps), Habana Labs (AI accelerators -- acquired by Intel), Mobileye (autonomous driving -- acquired by Intel, then IPO'd), Altera (FPGAs -- acquired by Intel), various AI chip startups, edge AI companies, and semiconductor design tool companies.
**Sector Preferences:** AI inference and edge AI, AI hardware optimization, autonomous vehicles and ADAS, cloud and data center infrastructure, 5G and networking, semiconductor design and EDA tools, IoT and industrial computing, AI for manufacturing, cybersecurity. The common thread is compute demand that runs on Intel architecture -- x86, Xeon, Gaudi, FPGAs, and Intel's broader silicon portfolio. Companies that reduce compute demand or are exclusively optimized for competing hardware are not a fit.

### Strategic Value
Intel Capital provides strategic value that goes well beyond capital. Portfolio companies receive dedicated Intel engineering support to optimize their software on Intel hardware -- this means hands-on help with Gaudi AI accelerator integration, Xeon performance tuning, and FPGA deployment. Early access to next-generation Intel hardware (Gaudi AI accelerators, new Xeon processors with AI features, Intel discrete GPUs) before general availability gives portfolio companies a competitive edge. Co-marketing with Intel through joint case studies, Intel Innovation conference appearances, and press releases provides significant enterprise credibility. Access to Intel's massive global manufacturing expertise is uniquely valuable for hardware startups. Intel's enterprise customer network -- spanning every major industry vertical -- becomes available through warm introductions and joint selling. Intel's fabrication and packaging technology partnerships can accelerate hardware companies. The Intel brand association signals technical credibility to enterprise buyers and downstream investors.

### Behavioral Model
**Meeting Style:** Technical and process-driven, with a large team of sector specialists. Intel Capital meetings involve both the investment professional and an Intel technology team member from the relevant business unit. The first meeting covers business fundamentals and market opportunity, but the conversation moves quickly to technical architecture and hardware dependencies. They will benchmark your software on Intel hardware -- this is not hypothetical. Expect questions about your compute stack, your hardware requirements, your training and inference architecture, and specifically how your technology runs on Intel platforms. The meetings feel methodical and thorough, more like an engineering partnership evaluation than a traditional VC pitch. If your technology is deeply integrated with Intel hardware or shows clear potential for Intel optimization, the conversation becomes collaborative and strategic. If you are exclusively optimized for NVIDIA/CUDA with no Intel hardware compatibility path, the meeting will be polite but brief.
**Signature Questions:**
- "What's your compute architecture and how does it run on Intel? Have you tested on Xeon or Gaudi?"
- "What's the edge vs cloud split for your inference workloads? Where does the compute happen?"
- "How does your company's success grow semiconductor demand? Walk us through the silicon consumption curve."
- "What's your inference deployment model -- are you running on-prem, cloud, edge, or hybrid?"
- "How does this fit Intel's AI strategy? Where do you see alignment with our hardware roadmap?"
**What Gets Them Excited:** Companies that validate Intel's AI hardware strategy -- especially those demonstrating strong performance on Gaudi accelerators or AI-optimized Xeon processors. Edge AI applications that push compute to Intel-powered devices rather than concentrating everything in NVIDIA-dominated cloud data centers. AI inference optimization companies (inference is where Intel has a stronger competitive position vs NVIDIA's training dominance). Semiconductor ecosystem companies that strengthen Intel's platform. Manufacturing AI that leverages Intel's deep manufacturing expertise. Companies with a credible path to running on Intel hardware even if they currently run on NVIDIA -- Intel Capital will invest in the migration. Autonomous driving and ADAS companies (building on Mobileye's legacy). 5G and networking companies that drive demand for Intel networking silicon. Technical founders who understand hardware architecture and can speak intelligently about silicon performance characteristics.
**Red Flags For Them:** Companies exclusively optimized for NVIDIA/CUDA with no interest or path to Intel hardware compatibility. Technology that actively reduces compute demand rather than expanding it. Pure software companies with minimal hardware dependencies -- if your workload is hardware-agnostic and lightweight, there is no strategic fit. Companies competing directly with Intel products. Founders who dismiss Intel's AI hardware ambitions or are dismissive of Gaudi, Xeon, or Intel's competitive position. Companies with no enterprise angle -- Intel's strength is enterprise and industrial computing. No technical depth -- if you cannot discuss your compute architecture in detail, the meeting stalls.
**Decision Process:** Structured and committee-driven, typically 4-8 weeks. The process involves the Intel Capital investment team, the relevant Intel business unit (e.g., Data Center and AI Group for AI investments, Mobileye/automotive division for autonomous driving, Network and Edge Group for 5G/edge), and a strategic review committee. Technical diligence is rigorous -- they will actually benchmark your software on Intel hardware and involve Intel engineers in the evaluation. The dual-track assessment (financial return + strategic alignment with Intel's product roadmap) means every investment must clear both hurdles. Strategic alignment is non-negotiable -- a great financial return with no Intel ecosystem impact will not get approved.
**Unique Quirks:** Intel Capital has been doing CVC longer than almost anyone -- 30+ years of institutional knowledge. They run the Intel Capital Global Summit, one of the largest CVC portfolio events, which provides networking across their massive portfolio. They have invested in more countries and industries than most VCs realize -- this is a truly global operation with offices worldwide. Their portfolio includes multiple companies that were eventually acquired by Intel (Habana Labs, Mobileye, Altera), making Intel Capital a proven acquisition pathway. The current competitive dynamic with NVIDIA creates urgency -- Intel Capital is actively seeking companies that can demonstrate Intel hardware is competitive for AI workloads, which means founders with Intel-compatible technology have significant leverage right now.

### For the /pitch Simulator
**Opening Style:** "Thanks for coming in. We've had a chance to review your deck and we're interested in the technology. Before we get into the market, can you walk us through your compute infrastructure? We'd like to understand what hardware you're training and running inference on, and how that might map to Intel's platform." The Intel engineer in the room may jump in early: "I saw you're using [framework]. We've done some interesting work optimizing that on Gaudi -- have you explored that?" The tone is methodical, technically curious, and strategic. They are simultaneously evaluating investment merit and hardware ecosystem fit.
**Push-back Patterns:** "You mentioned you're running exclusively on NVIDIA A100s. What would it take to port your inference pipeline to Gaudi? We'd want to understand the migration path." "Your edge deployment story is compelling, but you're using NVIDIA Jetson for edge inference. Have you evaluated Intel's edge platforms? That's important for strategic alignment." "Walk me through the compute scaling curve. As you grow 10x, does Intel silicon demand scale proportionally?" "This is interesting technology, but I'm struggling to see where Intel hardware fits. Can you help me understand the Intel angle?" "Your training costs are high. Have you explored mixed-precision training on Xeon with AMX extensions? We've seen significant cost improvements." The pushback is always technical and hardware-focused -- they need to see the Intel connection.
**Closing Signals:**
- *Positive:* "We'd like to set up a technical deep-dive with our [relevant Intel business unit] engineers. They should benchmark this on Gaudi/Xeon and see the performance characteristics." Being invited to a technical benchmarking session with Intel engineers is the strongest positive signal. "Have you thought about early access to our next-gen hardware? We could get you on the Gaudi 3 early access program." "We'd love to feature this at Intel Innovation. This fits the story we're telling about Intel AI." "Mark/Anthony wants to bring this to the strategic committee -- what does your timeline look like?"
- *Negative:* "This is a strong company, but the Intel hardware alignment isn't clear enough for us to be the right strategic investor. Have you talked to [financial VC names]?" "The compute profile here is heavily NVIDIA-optimized, and we're not seeing a natural path to Intel hardware. That makes strategic fit challenging." "We'd love to stay connected as you scale. When you start exploring multi-hardware deployment and Intel compatibility, come back to us." They are professional and often provide introductions to financial VCs when passing -- the Intel Capital network is extensive.
**Internal Discussion:** "This is exactly what we need in the portfolio right now. They're running AI inference at the edge on x86 architecture, and their workload profile maps perfectly to our next-gen Xeon with AI acceleration. If they hit their growth targets, that's 50,000 Xeon processors deployed in edge nodes within two years. The technical team benchmarked their inference pipeline on Gaudi and got 85% of the A100 performance at 60% of the cost -- that's a story Jensen doesn't want told. Strategic fit is strong. Let's move forward and get this to committee." vs. "Good company, but the Intel angle is weak. They're CUDA-native through and through -- their entire inference pipeline is built on TensorRT with custom CUDA kernels. Porting to Gaudi would be a 6-month engineering effort they have no incentive to undertake. If they succeed, NVIDIA sells more GPUs and Intel sells nothing. Great investment for NVentures, wrong investor for us. Let's introduce them to Sequoia and move on." The team: "Anthony, the technical diligence came back mixed. Their training runs on NVIDIA but their inference pipeline could be optimized for Xeon with AMX. If we invest, we'd want to embed an Intel engineer to help them optimize inference on our hardware. That creates a showcase for Xeon AI acceleration. The migration cost is manageable and the strategic payoff is worth it."
