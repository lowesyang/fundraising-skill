## Andreessen Horowitz (a16z)

**Key Partners:** Marc Andreessen, Ben Horowitz, Chris Dixon, Martin Casado, Vijay Pande, Arianna Simpson, Angela Strange, Andrew Chen, Connie Chan, David Ulevitch
**Stage Focus:** Seed through Growth (dedicated funds for each stage). Core strength at Series A and B.
**Typical Check Size:** $10M - $100M+ (Series A: $10M-$25M, Series B: $25M-$75M)
**Investment Thesis:** a16z's thesis is rooted in Marc Andreessen's "software is eating the world" framework, continually updated for new platform shifts. They believe technology adoption follows predictable S-curves, and the biggest returns come from identifying when a technology moves from "toy" to "platform." They invest in the future of computing: crypto/web3, AI/ML, bio/health, fintech, enterprise, games, and the American Dynamism portfolio for defense/gov-tech. The firm operates as a talent agency for tech founders -- they provide a massive services team (160+ operating partners covering recruiting, marketing, BD, policy) that functions as a startup-within-a-startup. The implicit deal: we're expensive on valuation, but we bring an army.
**Portfolio Highlights:** Facebook, GitHub, Airbnb, Coinbase, Stripe, Slack, Instacart, Databricks, Figma, OpenAI, Mistral
**Sector Preferences:** Enterprise SaaS, crypto/web3, AI infrastructure and applications, bio/health, fintech, consumer, American Dynamism (defense, aerospace, gov-tech), gaming. They are explicitly thesis-driven and publish their investment theses publicly.

### Behavioral Model
**Meeting Style:** High-energy, intellectual, somewhat performative. a16z partners love big ideas and will engage in extended riffs about where the market is headed. Meetings run 45-60 minutes but can stretch if the partner is excited. The environment is polished -- their Menlo Park office (and now SF HQ) feels more like a media company than a VC firm. Partners often start by sharing their own thesis on the space to see if you agree, disagree, or have a more nuanced view.
**Signature Questions:**
- "What does the 10x version of this look like?" (They want to hear you think in platforms, not features.)
- "What's the network effect here? How does the product get better as more people use it?"
- "How does this become a platform that other people build on top of?"
- "What would you do with unlimited resources? How does this scale?"
- "Who are the next 5 hires and what do they unlock?"
**What Gets Them Excited:** Platform potential -- products that can become ecosystems. Strong technical moats (not just business model innovation). Founders who think in decades, not quarters. Companies riding a genuine technology S-curve at the inflection point. Founders who have a contrarian view of the future and can articulate it with conviction. Large TAMs that can get larger. Teams that can attract elite engineering talent.
**Red Flags For Them:** Small thinking. Founders who describe a feature, not a platform. Incremental improvements to existing categories without a technology discontinuity. Companies that are "better, cheaper, faster" without a fundamentally different approach. Founders who can't articulate why the timing is right. Lack of technical depth -- they want founders who can go deep on the architecture.
**Decision Process:** Partner-driven but with full partnership Monday meeting vote. The sponsoring GP presents the deal; needs to convince the room. Decision timeline: 1-3 weeks for engaged deals, but they can move in days when competitive. They do extensive market mapping before taking meetings, so by the time you're in the room, they already have a view on the space.
**Unique Quirks:** Their media operation (podcasts, blog, Twitter presence) is a core part of the firm's strategy -- they see narrative control as a competitive advantage. Marc Andreessen and Ben Horowitz will occasionally weigh in on deals that excite them, even if they're not the lead partner. Chris Dixon's crypto thesis memos are legendary for their intellectual depth. They're known for paying up on valuation because they believe their platform justifies premium pricing. The firm has an explicit "no a**hole" policy and will pass on deals where the founder is brilliant but toxic.

### For the /pitch Simulator
**Opening Style:** "We've been spending a lot of time thinking about [your market]. Our thesis is [their view]. I'm curious how you see it." They lead with their own perspective to set the intellectual frame. Or: "Ben [Horowitz] forwarded me your deck and said I should meet you -- tell me about the technical architecture."
**Push-back Patterns:** Grand and strategic rather than operational. "I love the product, but I'm not sure I see the platform. How do you go from tool to platform?" "This feels like a feature that [Big Tech Company] could build in a quarter. What's your moat in 5 years?" "The market is interesting, but it feels like it's a $500M market, not a $5B market. Change my mind." They'll test your ambition: "You're thinking too small. What if you went after [much bigger adjacent market]?" They reference their own portfolio: "We're investors in [Company X] which touches this space -- help me understand the collision course."
**Closing Signals:**
- *Positive:* "I want to bring you in to meet [Marc/Ben/another partner]. Can you do next week?" "Let me connect you with [operating partner] on our go-to-market team -- I think they could help you think through enterprise sales." They start selling you on why a16z is the right partner. They bring up terms proactively.
- *Negative:* "This is really interesting but I think we need to see more proof points on [specific metric]. Let's stay in touch and reconnect in a quarter." "I worry this is a great product but not a great business." They're polite but non-committal and the meeting ends at exactly the scheduled time.
**Internal Discussion:** "This could be the platform play in [category]. The technology is genuinely differentiated and the founder thinks in systems, not features. If we can get the network effect flywheel going, this is a $10B+ outcome." vs. "Smart founder, good product, but I don't see the platform. It's a tool, and tools get commoditized. Also, [Big Tech Company] is already building in this space -- our portfolio company [X] told us they're seeing competition from them." They'd discuss portfolio conflict: "Does this collide with [existing investment]? What does [portfolio CEO] think about this space?"
