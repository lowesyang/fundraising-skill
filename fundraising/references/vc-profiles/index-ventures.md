## Index Ventures

**Key Partners:** Mike Volpi, Danny Rimer, Martin Mignot, Nina Achadjian, Mark Goldberg
**Partner Investment Breakdown:** Danny Rimer: consumer internet, design-led products, marketplaces — transatlantic consumer thesis (Figma, Discord, Revolut, Glossier) / Mike Volpi: enterprise infrastructure, AI, open-source companies — US and Israel (Elastic, Confluent, Cohere) / Martin Mignot: early-stage enterprise, healthcare AI, fintech (Wiz, Scale AI) / Nina Achadjian: AI, robotics, vertical SaaS, software-for-hardware (seed and early-stage) / Mark Goldberg: enterprise SaaS, developer tools, growth stage
**Stage Focus:** Seed through Growth. Strong presence across all stages with particular strength at Series A and B. Dedicated early-stage and growth funds.
**Typical Check Size:** $5M - $100M (Series A: $5M-$20M, Series B: $20M-$60M, Growth: $50M-$100M)
**Investment Thesis:** Index backs companies that define their category. The firm has a strong transatlantic perspective -- born in Geneva and London, now equally rooted in San Francisco -- and they understand both US and European markets at a deep level. They believe in "product velocity" -- the speed at which a company ships and iterates -- as the strongest leading indicator of long-term success. The core conviction is that the best founders build fast, learn fast, and create categories rather than competing in existing ones. They look for companies with the potential to become the defining player in a market, not the second or third entrant. Their European roots give them an edge in spotting global opportunities that US-only firms miss.
**Portfolio Highlights:** Figma, Notion, Roblox, Discord, Datadog, Revolut, Deliveroo, Adyen, Stripe (early European VC), Cohere
**Recent Investments (2022-2025):** Wiz (cloud security — Index was early investor, $32B acquisition by Google represents one of venture's largest payouts, 2022 Series D participation); Mistral AI (European open-source LLM, 2023); Cohere (enterprise LLM platform — ongoing support); Linear (engineering workflow tool, growth round); Anthropic (AI safety/research, participation); Physical Intelligence (robotics AI, 2024); Temporal (workflow orchestration, growth round); ClickHouse (analytics database, 2022); LiveKit (real-time communications infrastructure, 2023); Fireworks AI (AI inference infrastructure, 2024)
**Sector Preferences:** Enterprise SaaS, AI infrastructure and applications (strong portfolio including Cohere), fintech, consumer platforms, gaming, European tech expanding globally. Particularly strong in companies with transatlantic potential. Active in AI: backing Cohere (competitor to OpenAI) and various AI application-layer companies and European AI infrastructure plays.

### Core Philosophy & First Principles

Index Ventures describes itself as "scaled artisans" — a phrase that captures the firm's core tension and core identity. They have the scale of a top-tier global firm ($2.3B deployed in their 2024 fund cycle alone) but operate with the workshop intimacy of a craft partnership. The founding thesis from Geneva and London was that truly transformative companies need investors who understand both their home market and the global market they will eventually need to win; the transatlantic DNA that Index built over three decades gives them pattern recognition that US-only or Europe-only firms cannot match.

Danny Rimer, the firm's most prominent consumer investor, has articulated their selection philosophy most clearly: Index is looking for founders who "have been placed on this Earth to build their business" and would be doing it regardless of funding. The standard is intrinsic purpose over opportunity-seeking. This framing is more than marketing — it shapes how Index evaluates founders in the first meeting. Partners are listening for the presence or absence of existential commitment to the problem, and founders who pitch with a portfolio-company mindset rather than a mission-driven one rarely advance.

Mike Volpi, the firm's enterprise and infrastructure anchor, adds a technical dimension: he believes the best infrastructure companies are built by founders who have lived the problem as users and have strong opinions about what the right architecture looks like. His investments in companies like Elasticsearch (Elastic), Confluent, and Cohere reflect a pattern of backing open-source communities that have achieved developer love before adding commercial layers.

The product velocity thesis — the conviction that shipping speed is the strongest leading indicator of long-term success — runs throughout the firm. Index actively measures deploy frequency, feature cadence, and time-to-ship in diligence, and uses these metrics to rank teams in competitive investment processes. The "Relationships That Reshape Industries" framing of their 2024 fund launch signals a deliberate emphasis on long-term founder-investor relationships over transactional deal execution.

### Behavioral Model
**Meeting Style:** Polished, data-driven, globally minded. Partners are well-connected across both the US and Europe, and meetings reflect this sophistication. Conversations are structured and efficient -- they respect your time and expect you to respect theirs. Partners come prepared with research on your market and will reference European and global comparables that US-only firms would miss. The atmosphere is intellectually rigorous but not combative. They are genuinely curious about how you think about your category and will probe your understanding of the global competitive landscape.
**Signature Questions:**
- "What's your category and how do you define it?" (They want founders who create categories, not compete in existing ones.)
- "How fast do you ship?" (Product velocity is a core evaluation metric for them.)
- "What does the European opportunity look like?" (They want to understand if you think globally.)
- "What's your product velocity metric?" (They want specific data on iteration speed -- deploy frequency, feature cadence, time-to-ship.)
- "Who's the budget holder and what are they replacing?" (Practical go-to-market clarity, especially for enterprise.)
**What Gets Them Excited:** Category-defining companies with global ambition. Founders who ship at extraordinary speed and can quantify their product velocity. Companies that can bridge US and European markets. Strong technical teams with a clear product vision. Founders who understand their competitive landscape globally, not just in Silicon Valley. Products with natural network effects or strong retention metrics. European founders building for a global market from day one.
**Red Flags For Them:** Slow shippers -- if your release cadence is quarterly, they lose interest fast. US-only thinking for markets that are inherently global. Founders who don't understand their competitive landscape beyond their home market. Companies entering crowded categories without a differentiated thesis on why they win. Lack of clarity on the customer's buying process and budget allocation. Founders who can't articulate what category they're creating.
**Decision Process:** 2-4 weeks typical, involving multiple partners in the evaluation. Known for moving fast when genuinely excited -- can compress to under a week for high-conviction opportunities. The sponsoring partner presents to the broader partnership. They do thorough market mapping and will often have already spoken to potential customers before the decision meeting.
**Unique Quirks:** Their transatlantic DNA is genuine -- they have deep networks in both Europe and the US and actively help companies expand across the Atlantic in both directions. They publish the "Index Ventures Portfolio" talent network, connecting portfolio companies for recruiting. Danny Rimer's consumer investing instinct and Mike Volpi's enterprise depth give the firm unusual range. They are one of the few top-tier firms equally credible in London, Geneva, and San Francisco. Their European heritage means they often see opportunities in markets like fintech and logistics before US-only firms do.

### Founder & Culture Fit (Non-Obvious Signals)

- Index responds to founders who treat their company as a calling rather than a career move. Danny Rimer's "placed on this Earth" standard filters for people who would be building the same thing regardless of funding conditions or market timing.
- Intellectual curiosity about culture and design — not just technology — is a recurring trait in Index's successful founders. Danny Rimer's interest in art, design, and human behavior shapes which consumer founders he champions; founders who can discuss the human experience they are building for tend to resonate far more than those who lead with market size.
- The firm is drawn to founders who have already built something — even if small — before asking for capital. The proof that they ship is more compelling than the claim that they will.
- A global frame of reference matters even at the earliest stages: founders who have lived in or deeply studied multiple markets are seen as more likely to build durable category-defining companies rather than regional niches.
- Mike Volpi in particular is drawn to founders with strong architectural opinions — people who can explain not just what they are building but why the current architectural consensus in their space is wrong and what the correct one is.
- Founders who actively engage with the competitive landscape in other geographies — citing European or Asian comparables rather than only US competitors — signal the kind of global awareness Index prizes.
- Low need for validation and high comfort with ambiguity: Index backs many companies at stages where the market narrative hasn't formed yet, and founders who need strong external validation signals struggle to align with that posture.

### For the /pitch Simulator
**Opening Style:** "We've been tracking [your space] for a while, and we're particularly interested in how this plays out globally. Walk me through the product and how fast you're iterating." Or: "Martin [Mignot] flagged your company after seeing your growth in Europe -- tell me about your product velocity." They tend to open with a global or cross-market angle and quickly steer toward shipping speed and category definition.
**Push-back Patterns:** Measured and data-driven rather than combative. "I see the US traction, but what's the European play? If this is truly a category, it should be global." "Your shipping cadence concerns me -- how do you get to weekly deploys?" "This feels like you're competing in [existing category] rather than creating a new one. How do you reframe this?" "Revolut started in Europe and went global -- do you have that same DNA, or is this a US-centric play?" They'll probe global ambition: "If you only win the US, is this still a venture outcome?" They reference their own portfolio to test your knowledge: "How do you think about Notion's approach to this adjacent space?"
**Closing Signals:**
- *Positive:* "I'd like you to meet [Danny/Mike/another partner] -- can we schedule something this week?" "Let me connect you with some of our European portfolio founders who've done this expansion successfully." They start discussing how they'd help you scale transatlantically. They ask about your timeline and other investors in the process.
- *Negative:* "This is promising but I think we need to see another quarter of product velocity data. Let's reconnect in Q[X]." "I'm not sure I see the category creation here yet -- it feels like a strong product in an existing market." The meeting wraps on time with a polite but vague "let's stay in touch."
**Internal Discussion:** "This founder ships faster than almost anyone we've seen -- their deploy frequency is in the top 5% of our portfolio. The category thesis is compelling and there's a clear European expansion path. If they can define this category globally, this is a $5B+ outcome." vs. "Good product, good team, but they're competing in [existing category], not creating a new one. The shipping speed is okay but not exceptional. And the founder seems US-focused -- I'm not sure they see the global opportunity the way we'd want." They'd discuss transatlantic fit: "Can we help them expand to Europe? Does this fit our cross-border thesis?"
