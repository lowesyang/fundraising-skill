## Felicis Ventures

**Stage Focus:** Seed and Series A — Felicis invests primarily at seed and Series A, though they have expanded to growth-stage follow-ons for their best portfolio companies. Aydin built his reputation as one of the most prolific seed investors in Silicon Valley history, and the firm retains that early-stage DNA even as fund sizes have grown.
**Typical Check Size:** $500K - $10M (seed checks on the lower end, Series A leads on the higher end; they can also do larger follow-on checks in later rounds for breakout portfolio companies)
**Investment Thesis:** Felicis invests in "ridiculously ambitious founders" building category-defining companies. Aydin Senkut, Google employee #80, brings deep pattern recognition for platform companies and brings an operator's lens to investing. The firm's thesis centers on founders who combine technical depth with market insight — people who see something others don't and have the skills to build it. They believe in backing founders early, before consensus forms, and supporting them through multiple stages of growth. They have a strong conviction that the best companies start with a narrow wedge and expand into massive platforms. In recent years, they have become very active in AI applications and infrastructure, believing AI is a once-in-a-generation platform shift.
**Portfolio Highlights:** Shopify (seed), Fitbit (seed), Adyen (early), Credit Karma, Notion, Plaid, FlexPort, Meraki (acquired by Cisco for $1.2B), Twitch (early), Ayasdi, Bonobos, Dollar Shave Club
**Recent Investments (2022-2025):** Runway (AI video generation, led $50M Series C 2022); Supabase (open-source Firebase alternative, led $80M Series B 2022); Weights & Biases (MLOps and model monitoring platform); Poolside (foundation model for software engineering); Skild AI (general-purpose robot learning / physical AI); Mercor (AI-powered technical recruiting marketplace); Wherobots (cloud geospatial platform, led $21.5M Series A 2024); Vannevar Labs (defense tech AI). Fund IX: $825M (2023); Fund X: $900M (2025) — total AUM over $3.8B, 45+ unicorns.
**Key Partners:** Aydin Senkut (founder, Managing Director), Victoria Treyger (GP), Niki Pezeshki (GP), Sundeep Peechu (GP), Viviana Faga (GP)
**Partner Investment Breakdown:** Aydin Senkut: generalist across all sectors; primary seed conviction engine; platform/developer ecosystem plays from Google operator lens — Sundeep Peechu: AI/ML infrastructure and foundation models (Runway, Poolside); engineer background, joined 2010 — Victoria Treyger: fintech, security, health tech, vertical SaaS — Niki Pezeshki: healthcare, fintech, enterprise software (departed 2024 per public reports) — Viviana Faga: go-to-market expertise, portfolio support, community building; focus on AI applications and growth-stage companies
**Sector Preferences:** Historically broad — fintech, commerce/marketplaces, enterprise SaaS, developer tools, consumer internet, health tech. In the current era, heavily focused on AI applications and AI infrastructure. They follow conviction, not sector mandates, but Aydin's Google background gives them particular strength in evaluating platform and developer-ecosystem plays. They like companies that sit at the intersection of technology and large, inefficient markets.

### Core Philosophy & First Principles

Aydin Senkut's foundational investment philosophy stems from a single personal conviction formed at Google: the most transformative companies start as wedge products that look small or niche, and the best founders understand the full platform they're building before the market does. His experience as Google employee #80 — watching a search tool become the organizing layer of the internet — shaped a pattern-recognition for platform shifts that pervades all of Felicis's investment logic.

The firm has articulated two investment archetypes: "reinvention" plays (taking a large, inefficient existing market and rebuilding it with 10x better technology) and "frontier" plays (enabling entirely new markets that didn't exist before). Senkut applies a "10x or nothing" filter — a company must have a credible path to being ten times better than the incumbent, not merely incrementally better. This filter eliminates much of what looks like a good business without a venture-scale moat.

Senkut's generalist philosophy is itself a deliberate competitive strategy, not a limitation. He has argued publicly that generalism creates flexibility across market cycles — when AI is hot, Felicis can rotate heavily into AI without portfolio constraints; when something else emerges, the firm isn't locked in by mandate. Currently, more than 70% of Felicis's active portfolio companies are AI-native, reflecting the conviction that AI is "a once-in-a-generation platform shift" comparable to the internet itself. Partner Sundeep Peechu has articulated this as expecting AI to become infrastructure rather than a category: "In five years, I'd be shocked if people say 'I'm investing in AI.'"

The firm also structures founder relationships unconventionally: Felicis treats founders as its primary customer (not LPs), has publicly committed to always voting with founders in board disputes, and maintains a "1% Founder Development Pledge" that provides non-dilutive capital for founder coaching and personal development — a cultural statement about who the fund serves.

### Behavioral Model
**Meeting Style:** Warm, conversational, and genuinely curious. Aydin is known for being one of the most approachable and founder-friendly investors in the Valley. Meetings feel less like interrogations and more like collaborative brainstorming sessions. He leans in, asks follow-up questions that show he's actually listening, and often shares relevant portfolio examples or industry patterns. The vibe is low-ego — he's not trying to prove he's smarter than you. He's trying to understand if you're onto something real. Other partners (Victoria, Niki, Sundeep) maintain this culture — Felicis meetings consistently feel like conversations with smart, supportive people who want to help you think through your business.
**Signature Questions:**
- "What's the insight that makes you the right person to build this?" (they want founder-market fit rooted in genuine insight, not resume credentials)
- "How does this become a $10B company?" (they invest for category-defining outcomes — show them the path from wedge to platform)
- "What's the wedge and how do you expand?" (classic Felicis question — they love narrow entry points that open into massive markets)
- "Who are your first 10 customers?" (specificity over abstraction — they want to hear real names and real conversations, not TAM slides)
- "What does the competitive landscape look like in 3 years?" (they think about durability — not just who you're beating today, but who might come after you tomorrow)
**What Gets Them Excited:** Founders with deep, personal insight into the problem they're solving — the kind of insight that only comes from living in the problem space. Technical founders who also understand go-to-market. Companies with a narrow wedge that clearly opens into a much larger platform opportunity. Early signs of organic pull — users who love the product and tell others about it without being asked. AI-native companies that are rethinking entire workflows, not just adding a chatbot. Founders who can articulate their expansion path with conviction and specificity.
**Red Flags For Them:** Founders without conviction — people who seem to be building what's trendy rather than what they believe in. Markets that are too small to support a venture-scale outcome, even with perfect execution. Companies that can't articulate their expansion path beyond the initial wedge. Founders who are dismissive of competition or haven't thought seriously about competitive dynamics. Over-reliance on a single customer or channel. Pitch decks that are all vision and no substance — Felicis wants to see that you've done the work, not just dreamed the dream.
**Decision Process:** Aydin can move extremely fast — days, not weeks — for high-conviction seed bets. He has a legendary reputation for making quick decisions when he sees a founder he believes in. For larger Series A investments, the process is more structured: 2-3 weeks with multiple partner meetings, reference calls, and deeper diligence. But even at Series A, they move faster than most institutional VCs. The firm is collegial — partners discuss deals together — but Aydin's conviction carries significant weight, especially for seed investments.
**Unique Quirks:** Aydin Senkut is one of the most prolific angel/seed investors in Silicon Valley history, with a personal portfolio that spans hundreds of companies. His track record of backing founders others passed on is remarkable — he invested in Shopify when it was a tiny Canadian e-commerce tool, and Fitbit when wearables were considered a niche market. He maintains an enormous network from his Google days and his decades of angel investing, which makes Felicis introductions exceptionally valuable. The firm has grown significantly but still retains a boutique, founder-first culture. They are known for providing meaningful help with hiring, strategic intros, and go-to-market — not just capital. Aydin's breadth of portfolio experience means he can pattern-match across an unusually wide range of business models and markets.

### Founder & Culture Fit (Non-Obvious Signals)

- **Outsider-with-conviction archetype.** Senkut himself was the unconventional VC: no engineering degree, Turkish immigrant background, not from the finance or Stanford pipeline. He instinctively trusts founders who don't fit the conventional profile but have an unusual angle on a problem — and tends to be skeptical of founders who look perfect on paper but lack genuine obsession.

- **International background as unfair advantage.** Senkut's own experience navigating multiple cultures, regulatory environments, and market structures informs a preference for founders who are "geo-agnostic" — able to build for markets beyond Silicon Valley's defaults. His track record includes Shopify (Canadian), Adyen (Dutch), and Canva (Australian) long before those companies were obvious global winners.

- **Anti-fragility as personality trait.** Felicis explicitly prioritizes founders with an "anti-fragility" orientation — people who get stronger under pressure rather than more cautious. Senkut has described this in terms of how investors and founders behave when markets turn. Founders who get creative and double down in adversity, rather than retreating to safety, signal cultural alignment.

- **Storytelling + substance duality.** Felicis will not invest in anything that takes 10 PowerPoint slides to explain, but also demands real substance behind the story. The ideal founder can explain their company clearly in one sentence AND has done the specific customer development, technical work, or market mapping to back it up. Pure vision without homework is as problematic as pure execution without narrative.

- **Founder-as-customer orientation.** Felicis has publicly committed to treating founders as its primary customer, including voting with founders on board matters and providing non-dilutive personal development funding. Founders who have been burned by adversarial VC behavior or who deeply value investor alignment over financial terms alone tend to resonate with this posture.

- **Velocity as personality.** Senkut has a reputation for making decisions in days when he's convinced. Founders who can match that tempo — who respond quickly, iterate fast, and have a bias toward action — are more likely to generate the positive spiral that leads to a Felicis investment than founders who operate slowly and deliberatively, even if thoughtfully.

### For the /pitch Simulator
**Opening Style:** Warm and disarming. Aydin might start with "Tell me the story — how did you get obsessed with this problem?" or "I saw [something specific about your product] and I was really intrigued — walk me through how it works." He does his homework before meetings and often references something specific about your company, which sets a collaborative tone from the start. Other Felicis partners similarly open with genuine curiosity rather than a formal "give me your pitch" frame. You'll feel like you're having a conversation, not delivering a presentation.
**Push-back Patterns:** Felicis pushback is constructive, not adversarial. Aydin might say "I love the wedge, but I'm struggling to see how you get from $10M to $100M in revenue — help me understand the expansion." Or "The AI space is crowded right now — what makes your moat real in 18 months when the foundation models improve?" Victoria might probe on go-to-market: "You've got 10 design partners but none are paying — what's the conversion path?" They challenge expansion narratives and competitive durability, but they do it as thought partners, not interrogators. Their pushback often sounds like "Help me get there" rather than "Convince me."
**Closing Signals:**
- *Positive:* "I want to bring you in to meet [another Felicis partner]." "Let me introduce you to [portfolio founder in adjacent space]." "What does your timeline look like? We'd like to move quickly on this." Aydin personally following up within 24-48 hours with specific questions or asks for additional data — a sign he's actively building his investment thesis internally. Requests for customer references (they're doing diligence, which means they're serious).
- *Negative:* "This is really interesting — let's stay in touch as you hit your next milestones." "I think the market needs to develop a bit more before we'd be comfortable." "We're going to pass for now but I'd love to see how things evolve." Felicis tends to be direct and respectful in their passes — they won't ghost you. If they go quiet for more than a week after an engaged meeting, it's likely a pass.
**Internal Discussion:** *Bull case:* "This founder was employee #5 at [relevant company] and saw exactly where the system broke. The wedge is narrow — just [specific use case] — but the expansion into [broader market] is completely natural. They already have 8 design partners, 3 converting to paid, and the product has organic word-of-mouth. The AI angle is real — they're not wrapping an API, they've built proprietary models on proprietary data. This could be Plaid-scale if the expansion works. Aydin wants to lead the seed at $3M, and I think we should move this week before [competitor VC] gets in." *Bear case:* "Impressive founder, good product intuition, but I'm worried about the expansion story. Right now this is a point solution for a niche market — maybe $500M TAM at best. The founder says they'll expand into [adjacent market] but they haven't validated any demand there. And the AI moat is thinner than it looks — if [large tech company] ships this feature natively, the wedge disappears. Let's pass and revisit if they can show the expansion is real."
