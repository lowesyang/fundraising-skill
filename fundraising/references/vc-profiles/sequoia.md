## Sequoia Capital

**Key Partners:** Roelof Botha (managing partner), Alfred Lin, Pat Grady, Shaun Maguire, Konstantine Buhler, Sonya Huang, Jess Lee, Stephanie Zhan, Omar Ismail
**Stage Focus:** Seed through Growth and public markets (via Sequoia Capital Fund, their permanent capital vehicle)
**Typical Check Size:** Series A: $10M-$25M, Series B: $25M-$75M (varies widely; can go larger)
**Investment Thesis:** Sequoia's enduring thesis is captured in their tagline: "We help the daring build legendary companies." They obsess over "why now?" -- the market timing question that separates good ideas from great investments. Don Valentine's original framework was about identifying large markets first, then finding the team that can capture them. The modern Sequoia has evolved to back founder-market fit in categories where a secular technology shift creates a once-in-a-generation window. They reorganized in 2021 into a single permanent fund structure (Sequoia Capital Fund) to hold positions from seed through IPO and beyond, reflecting their belief that the best companies compound for decades. Their "Arc" program targets pre-seed founders.
**Portfolio Highlights:** Apple, Google, Oracle, Cisco, YouTube, WhatsApp, Instagram, Airbnb, Stripe, Zoom, Nubank, ByteDance, Klarna
**Sector Preferences:** Enterprise, consumer, fintech, healthcare, crypto, AI/ML. They are genuinely sector-agnostic but over-index on companies that can become category-defining. Strong recent focus on AI infrastructure, vertical AI applications, and companies benefiting from the "AI application layer."

### Behavioral Model
**Meeting Style:** Intense, structured, and intellectually rigorous. Sequoia meetings feel like oral exams conducted by very smart, very prepared professors. Partners will have read everything publicly available about your company and market. Meetings are typically 45-60 minutes. The atmosphere is serious but not unfriendly -- they genuinely want to understand your thinking. There's a famous Sequoia template for company presentations (problem, solution, why now, market size, competition, business model, team) that they still loosely follow in evaluating pitches.
**Signature Questions:**
- "Why now? What changed in the world that makes this possible or necessary today?" (This is THE Sequoia question.)
- "What is the company you're building, not the product you're building?" (They think in decades.)
- "Describe your competitive landscape and be honest about where you're vulnerable."
- "What would it take for this to be a $10B company? Walk me through the math."
- "What's the hardest thing you've done in building this so far?"
**What Gets Them Excited:** Founders who have a deep, defensible insight about market timing. Companies where "why now" has a crisp, structural answer (regulatory change, technology cost curve, behavioral shift). Category creators, not category participants. Founders with missionary zeal who would work on this problem even without funding. Unit economics that improve with scale. Companies where the founder has an unfair advantage (domain expertise, unique data, proprietary distribution).
**Red Flags For Them:** "Why now?" answered with "because AI" without specificity. Founders who can't articulate the competitive landscape honestly. Companies in crowded markets without a differentiated wedge. Founders who optimize for fundraising instead of building. Capital-inefficient business models without a path to margin improvement. Teams where the CEO can't recruit senior talent.
**Decision Process:** Partnership-driven with Monday partner meetings. The sponsoring partner presents; robust internal debate follows. Sequoia is known for thorough diligence -- they'll talk to your customers, competitors, and former colleagues. Decision timeline: 2-4 weeks typically, but they can compress when competitive. They've been known to preempt rounds with aggressive offers when they're convicted.
**Unique Quirks:** Sequoia's internal memos are legendary -- they write detailed investment memos that include the bear case. Their "company design" workshops help portfolio founders think about long-term culture and organizational design. They value intellectual honesty above almost everything -- they'd rather hear "I don't know" than a hand-wavy answer. Roelof Botha is known for being quiet in meetings and then asking the single hardest question in the room. The firm's competitive intensity is famous; they hate losing deals to other firms. They track "prepared mind" categories where they've pre-mapped the market before a company even pitches them.

### For the /pitch Simulator
**Opening Style:** "Thanks for coming in. We've been studying [your market/category] for a while now. I'd love to hear your perspective -- start wherever you'd like, but I'm especially curious about the timing." They signal that they've done homework and set "why now?" as the frame immediately.
**Push-back Patterns:** Precise and unavoidable. "Help me with the timing question -- this problem has existed for 10 years. What's structurally different now?" "You said the market is $20B, but when I look at it from the bottom up, I get to $3B. Walk me through your methodology." "Your competitor [Company X] has a 2-year head start and $50M more in funding. Why do you win?" They'll poke at unit economics: "Your CAC/LTV ratio works at this scale, but what happens when you move beyond early adopters?" Long silences after your answer -- they're thinking, not signaling rejection.
**Closing Signals:**
- *Positive:* "I'd like to set up a working session with you and [another Sequoia partner who covers an adjacent area]. Can you also share your financial model?" "Let me spend some time with this over the weekend. I want to write up my thinking." They start asking about your cap table and round dynamics. They ask who else you're talking to (they want to win the deal).
- *Negative:* "I think you're building something interesting, but I'm not convinced on the timing yet. Here's what would change my mind: [specific milestones]." "I want to be transparent -- I don't think we're the right partner for this right now." They give real, specific feedback on what would need to change.
**Internal Discussion:** "The 'why now' answer is the strongest I've heard in this category. The regulatory shift in [area] creates a structural window that closes in 18 months -- this founder is the only one who sees it clearly." vs. "I like the founder, but I can't get comfortable on timing. This market has had false starts before -- remember [historical example]? The structural driver isn't strong enough yet." They'd reference their prepared-mind work: "We mapped this space 6 months ago. This company fits our thesis on [specific trend]."
