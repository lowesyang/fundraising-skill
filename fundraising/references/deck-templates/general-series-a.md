# General Series A Deck Template

> Used by the `/pitch-deck` command. 15-20 slides for a 30-45 minute meeting. Comprehensive, data-rich, narrative-driven.

---

## Format Overview

- **Total slides:** 15-20 (core 15 + up to 5 appendix)
- **Meeting length:** 30-45 minutes (first meeting), followed by Q&A
- **Style:** Clean, professional, consistent design system. One key takeaway per slide.
- **Audience:** GP or partner at a venture firm, often with an associate who has done pre-screening.
- **Narrative arc:** Problem → Solution → Proof → Scale → Team → Ask

---

## Slide-by-Slide Template

### Slide 1: Title Slide
**Title:** [Company Name]
**Key message format:** One-liner that captures the company's essence.
**What to include:**
- Company name and logo
- One-line description (under 10 words)
- Presenter name(s) and title(s)
- Date and "Confidential" label

**What to exclude:**
- Funding history
- Contact details (save for final slide)

**Design notes:** Use company brand colors. The logo should be prominent. White space is your friend.

---

### Slide 2: Mission / Vision
**Title:** Our Mission
**Key message format:** "We exist to [transformative outcome] for [who]."
**What to include:**
- A bold, aspirational mission statement (1-2 sentences)
- Optional: a compelling quote from a customer or industry expert that frames the problem

**What to exclude:**
- Metrics (too early in the narrative)
- Jargon or buzzwords

**Data points needed:** None — this is a narrative slide.

**Design notes:** Large text, centered. This sets the emotional tone for the entire deck.

---

### Slide 3: Problem
**Title:** The Problem
**Key message format:** "[Who] struggles with [specific pain] because [structural reason]. This costs them [quantified impact]."
**What to include:**
- The problem stated from the customer's perspective, not the company's
- Quantified pain: dollars lost, hours wasted, opportunities missed
- Why the problem is getting worse (regulatory changes, market shifts, increasing complexity)
- 1-2 real customer quotes that validate the pain

**What to exclude:**
- Your solution (not yet)
- Multiple unrelated problems
- Problems that sound theoretical rather than lived

**Data points needed:** Market research data on the cost/prevalence of the problem. Customer interview quotes.

**Design notes:** Use a visual or infographic to make the pain tangible. Before/after comparisons work well.

---

### Slide 4: Solution
**Title:** Our Solution
**Key message format:** "[Product name] enables [who] to [do what] by [how], resulting in [measurable outcome]."
**What to include:**
- Clear, jargon-free description of what the product does
- The core insight or innovation that makes this approach better
- 1-2 product screenshots or a short workflow diagram
- A specific customer outcome (e.g., "reduces claim processing from 3 days to 3 minutes")

**What to exclude:**
- Technical architecture
- Feature lists or roadmap
- How it works under the hood (save for a later slide or appendix)

**Data points needed:** Customer case study with before/after metrics.

**Design notes:** Show the product. Real screenshots > mockups > descriptions. If possible, embed a 30-second demo GIF.

---

### Slide 5: Product Deep Dive
**Title:** How It Works
**Key message format:** Walk through the user journey in 3-4 steps.
**What to include:**
- 3-4 step workflow showing how a user goes from problem to solution
- Screenshots for each step
- Key differentiating features highlighted
- Any proprietary technology called out (AI model, algorithm, data pipeline)

**What to exclude:**
- Backend architecture diagrams
- Database schemas
- Edge cases or admin features

**Data points needed:** Product screenshots, user flow data, feature usage metrics.

**Design notes:** Use a numbered flow (Step 1 → Step 2 → Step 3). Keep it to 3-4 steps maximum. Each step should have a screenshot.

---

### Slide 6: Market Size
**Title:** Market Opportunity
**Key message format:** "The [market] is $[X]B today, growing [Y]% annually, driven by [secular trend]."
**What to include:**
- TAM/SAM/SOM with bottom-up calculations
- Current market size and projected growth rate
- The secular trends driving market expansion
- How you define your addressable market (be specific)

**What to exclude:**
- Top-down only TAM ("the global X market")
- Markets you cannot credibly serve in 5 years
- Overlapping market segments

**Data points needed:** Bottom-up TAM calculation (# of potential customers x ACV), third-party market research citations, growth rate data.

**Design notes:** Use concentric circles (TAM → SAM → SOM) or a funnel diagram. Show the math behind each layer. Cite your sources.

---

### Slide 7: Traction — Revenue & Growth
**Title:** Traction
**Key message format:** "$[X] ARR, growing [Y]% MoM, from [Z] customers."
**What to include:**
- ARR/MRR chart showing growth trajectory (at least 6-12 months)
- Month-over-month or quarter-over-quarter growth rate
- Number of customers and notable logos
- Key milestone callouts on the chart (product launch, pricing change, channel launch)

**What to exclude:**
- Revenue projections (they belong on the financials slide)
- Vanity metrics that do not connect to revenue
- Inconsistent time periods

**Data points needed:** Monthly ARR/MRR data for at least 6 months, customer count over time, logo permissions.

**Design notes:** The revenue chart is the single most scrutinized visual in the deck. Use a clean line or bar chart. Annotate inflection points. Make the current ARR number the largest text on the slide.

---

### Slide 8: Traction — Key Metrics
**Title:** Unit Economics & Engagement
**Key message format:** Present 4-6 metrics that prove the business works at a unit level.
**What to include:**
- LTV/CAC ratio
- Net dollar retention (NDR)
- Gross margin
- CAC payback period
- Churn rate (logo and revenue)
- Any domain-specific engagement metric

**What to exclude:**
- Metrics that look bad without context (address weaknesses proactively in narrative)
- More than 6 metrics (it overwhelms)
- Metrics without trend data

**Data points needed:** Trailing 12-month cohort data, LTV calculation breakdown, CAC by channel, gross margin breakdown.

**Design notes:** Use a grid layout — 2 rows of 3 metrics. Each metric should show the current value, the trend (arrow or sparkline), and a brief label. Use green/yellow/red color coding sparingly and only where you are green.

---

### Slide 9: Customer Case Study
**Title:** Customer Spotlight: [Customer Name]
**Key message format:** "[Customer] achieved [specific outcome] using [product] in [timeframe]."
**What to include:**
- Named customer (with permission) and their logo
- The specific problem they faced
- How they use the product
- Quantified results (ROI, time saved, revenue gained)
- Direct customer quote

**What to exclude:**
- Anonymized case studies (they carry less weight)
- Generic outcomes without numbers
- Multiple case studies (pick the strongest one; put others in appendix)

**Data points needed:** Customer-approved metrics, quote with attribution, timeline of deployment to results.

**Design notes:** Use a quote callout prominently. Before/after metrics in a side-by-side comparison. Include the customer's photo if possible.

---

### Slide 10: Business Model
**Title:** Business Model
**Key message format:** "We charge [who] [pricing model] with an average ACV of $[X]."
**What to include:**
- Pricing model clearly explained (subscription, usage-based, hybrid, marketplace take rate)
- Average contract value (ACV) or ARPU
- Expansion revenue mechanics (how customers spend more over time)
- Path to negative churn / NDR > 100%

**What to exclude:**
- Complex pricing tables with multiple tiers
- Future pricing plans that are not yet validated
- Competitor pricing comparisons

**Data points needed:** ACV distribution, pricing tier breakdown, expansion revenue data, NDR over time.

**Design notes:** Keep it simple. One clear diagram showing the flow of value and money. If usage-based, show a typical customer ramp curve.

---

### Slide 11: Go-to-Market Strategy
**Title:** Go-to-Market
**Key message format:** "We acquire customers through [primary channel] at $[X] CAC, with [organic/inbound] driving [Y]% of pipeline."
**What to include:**
- Primary and secondary acquisition channels
- CAC by channel
- Sales cycle length
- Pipeline conversion rates (lead → opportunity → closed)
- Current team structure (AEs, SDRs, marketing)

**What to exclude:**
- Channels you plan to use but have not tested
- Partnerships that are not signed
- "We'll go viral" without evidence

**Data points needed:** Channel attribution data, conversion funnel metrics, sales team productivity (quota attainment, ramp time).

**Design notes:** Use a funnel visualization showing conversion at each stage. Include actual numbers, not just percentages.

---

### Slide 12: Competitive Landscape
**Title:** Why We Win
**Key message format:** Position on two axes that matter most to customers, with your company in the top-right quadrant.
**What to include:**
- 2x2 matrix with carefully chosen axes that favor your positioning
- 3-5 competitors positioned honestly
- Your key differentiators in 2-3 bullet points
- Why alternatives fail (not just why you are better)

**What to exclude:**
- Exhaustive feature comparison tables
- Dismissing well-funded competitors
- "We have no competitors" (investors will stop listening)
- The word "only" as in "the only platform that..."

**Data points needed:** Competitor product capabilities, pricing, funding, market share. Customer switching stories.

**Design notes:** The 2x2 matrix is the standard format. Choose axes that are genuinely important to buyers, not gerrymandered to flatter your product. If investors Google your competitors, the positioning should hold up.

---

### Slide 13: Technology / Moat
**Title:** Our Unfair Advantage
**Key message format:** "Our [moat type] creates [compounding advantage] that becomes harder to replicate over time."
**What to include:**
- The primary defensibility mechanism: network effects, data moat, switching costs, proprietary technology, regulatory advantage, or brand
- How the moat compounds over time (flywheel diagram)
- Evidence that the moat is already working (metrics that show compounding)

**What to exclude:**
- "First mover advantage" (not a real moat)
- IP/patents as primary moat (investors are skeptical unless in biotech/hardware)
- Multiple unrelated moats (pick the strongest)

**Data points needed:** Moat-specific metrics (e.g., data volume growth, network density, switching cost evidence, proprietary model performance vs alternatives).

**Design notes:** A flywheel diagram is the most effective visual for showing compounding advantages. Label each node with a metric.

---

### Slide 14: Team
**Title:** Team
**Key message format:** "[Founder names] — [combined years] of experience in [relevant domain]. Previously built [relevant thing]."
**What to include:**
- Co-founder names, photos, and titles
- One line per founder: most impressive credential or accomplishment
- Domain expertise and why this team is uniquely positioned
- Key hires and functional coverage (CTO, VP Sales, etc.)
- Notable advisors or board members (only if genuinely impressive)

**What to exclude:**
- Full resumes or work histories
- More than 6 people
- Advisors who are not actively engaged
- Team members without clear relevance

**Data points needed:** Founder backgrounds, team size and composition, key hire pipeline.

**Design notes:** Professional headshots, consistent style. Arrange founders prominently, key hires below. Link to LinkedIn profiles in the sent version.

---

### Slide 15: Financials & Projections
**Title:** Financial Plan
**Key message format:** "At current growth rates, we project $[X]M ARR by [date] with [Y] months of runway."
**What to include:**
- 3-year revenue projection with assumptions stated
- Current burn rate and runway
- Path to breakeven or next fundraise milestones
- Key assumptions driving the model (growth rate, churn, ACV, headcount)

**What to exclude:**
- 5-year projections (low credibility at Series A)
- Detailed P&L statements (share in data room)
- Optimistic/base/pessimistic scenarios on a single slide

**Data points needed:** Monthly P&L for past 12 months, forward model with assumptions, cash balance and runway.

**Design notes:** Use a simple chart showing revenue projection and key milestones. Annotate when you expect to hit profitability or raise next. Investors know projections are aspirational — what they evaluate is whether the assumptions are reasonable.

---

### Slide 16: The Ask
**Title:** Our Series A
**Key message format:** "We are raising $[X]M to [achieve specific milestones] over the next [timeframe]."
**What to include:**
- Raise amount
- Key milestones the capital will fund (3-4 specific goals)
- High-level use of funds (% to engineering, sales, marketing)
- Target timeline for deploying capital
- Contact information

**What to exclude:**
- Valuation or terms (negotiate later)
- Detailed budget breakdowns
- "We are also open to [alternative deal structures]"

**Data points needed:** Headcount plan, milestone definitions with target dates, capital allocation breakdown.

**Design notes:** End strong. Restate the one-liner from slide 1. Include the company URL and founder email. The last visual impression should be confident and clear.

---

## Appendix Slides (Optional, 3-5 slides)

### A1: Detailed Product Roadmap
- Q-by-Q roadmap for next 12 months
- Longer-term vision (2-3 year horizon)
- Only share when asked; do not present proactively

### A2: Additional Case Studies
- 2-3 more customer stories with metrics
- Useful for addressing "is this just one customer?" objections

### A3: Detailed Financials
- Monthly P&L for trailing 12 months
- Cohort analysis charts
- Detailed unit economics breakdown

### A4: Market Landscape Detail
- Full competitor feature matrix
- Market map showing all players by segment
- Analyst quotes or market research citations

### A5: Technical Architecture
- System architecture diagram (for technical founders pitching technical VCs)
- Model performance benchmarks (for AI companies)
- Security and compliance certifications

## Tailoring to Different VC Firms

### a16z (Andreessen Horowitz)
- **Emphasis:** Market size, category-defining potential, technical differentiation
- **Style preferences:** Bold claims backed by data. a16z invests in "big ideas" — frame the company as creating or defining a category, not competing in one.
- **What resonates:** Network effects, platform potential, developer ecosystem plays, AI/crypto theses. Reference their published investment theses.
- **What to avoid:** Modest market sizing. a16z wants to see $10B+ TAM with a credible path.
- **Meeting prep:** Read the relevant GP's blog posts and podcast appearances. Reference their frameworks (e.g., "software is eating the world" adjacencies).

### Sequoia Capital
- **Emphasis:** Founder-market fit, speed of learning, capital efficiency
- **Style preferences:** Sequoia values concise storytelling. They popularized the "company purpose" framework — why does this matter now?
- **What resonates:** Compelling origin stories, earned secrets (insights from experience that others don't have), evidence of velocity (shipping speed, learning speed, growth speed).
- **What to avoid:** Bloated decks. Sequoia partners famously prefer fewer slides with more substance. Cut anything that does not earn its place.
- **Meeting prep:** Study their "Elements of Enduring Companies" framework. Be prepared to discuss your company through the lens of clarity of purpose, unfair advantage, and business model durability.

### Benchmark
- **Emphasis:** Product quality, founder obsession, network effects
- **Style preferences:** Benchmark is a small, equal-partnership firm. They value depth over breadth. Expect a deeply technical product conversation.
- **What resonates:** Beautiful product craft, organic growth, products that users love (not just use). Consumer behavior insights. Benchmark backed eBay, Twitter, Snapchat, Uber — they look for addiction-level engagement.
- **What to avoid:** Heavy sales-led GTM narratives. Benchmark historically favors product-led companies. Do not over-index on financial engineering.
- **Meeting prep:** Use the product yourself in front of them if possible. Benchmark partners want to feel the magic, not just see metrics about it.

### Lightspeed Venture Partners
- **Emphasis:** Enterprise GTM efficiency, large market with clear wedge
- **Style preferences:** Data-driven, structured presentations. Lightspeed appreciates rigor in market analysis and go-to-market planning.
- **What resonates:** Clear ICP definition, repeatable sales playbook, expansion revenue mechanics, multi-product vision from a single wedge.
- **What to avoid:** Vague market definitions or hand-wavy GTM plans.
- **Meeting prep:** Understand their sector focus areas and portfolio companies to avoid competitive overlap.

### General Principles for All Firms
- Research the specific partner you are meeting. Tailor examples to their domain expertise and past investments.
- Bring a printed version of the deck. Some partners prefer physical copies.
- The deck is a conversation guide, not a script. Be prepared to go deep on any slide and skip slides based on partner interest.
- Send the deck 24-48 hours before the meeting. Partners who pre-read will have better questions, leading to a more productive conversation.
- Follow up within 24 hours with any data points requested during the meeting.
