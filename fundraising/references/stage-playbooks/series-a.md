# Series A Fundraising Playbook

## Overview
- **Typical raise amount:** $5M-$20M (median ~$10M-$15M in 2024-2025)
- **Common structures:** Priced equity round (Series A Preferred Stock). SAFEs and convertible notes are not used at this stage.
- **Typical timeline:** 6-14 weeks from first partner meeting to close; preparation should begin 3-6 months before going to market

## What Investors Expect at This Stage
- **Traction benchmarks:** Proven product-market fit. For B2B SaaS: $1M-$2.5M ARR with strong net revenue retention (110%+). For consumer: 50K-500K MAU with strong engagement and retention curves. For marketplace: GMV traction with clear unit economics per transaction.
- **Team composition:** 8-20 people. Functional leadership emerging (head of engineering, first sales hire or VP Sales, head of product). Founders demonstrating ability to recruit senior talent.
- **Market proof needed:** Repeatable customer acquisition in at least one channel. Clear ICP (ideal customer profile) defined and validated. Evidence of expansion revenue or strong organic growth.
- **Product maturity:** Core product is stable and delivers measurable value. Customers are retained and expanding. Product roadmap is clear and driven by customer feedback.

## Series A Investor Evaluation Weights

When evaluating a Series A company, investors weight these factors:

1. **Metrics and PMF proof** (40% of decision)
   - ARR, NRR, cohort retention, unit economics -- data replaces narrative at this stage
   - Cohort charts and retention curves are the centerpiece of every partner meeting
   - Investors model your business to $100M+ ARR; the data must support that path

2. **Market opportunity** (25% of decision)
   - Credible path to $100M+ ARR and $1B+ outcome
   - Competitive position: why you win and why the moat deepens over time
   - Expansion vectors: can you grow beyond the initial wedge?

3. **Team and organization** (20% of decision)
   - Ability to recruit senior talent (VP Eng, VP Sales, Head of Product)
   - Organizational design: have you moved beyond founders doing everything?
   - Culture that attracts A-players and retains them

4. **GTM efficiency** (15% of decision)
   - Is growth efficient (CAC payback <12 months, burn multiple <2x)?
   - Is the acquisition channel repeatable, or was growth brute-forced?
   - Can you name the specific channel and explain why it scales?

## Readiness Benchmarks (for /product-metrics)

These thresholds power the diagnostic grading system:

- **Metric: ARR (B2B SaaS)**
  - GREEN: $1.5M+ ARR
  - YELLOW: $800K-$1.5M ARR
  - RED: Under $800K ARR

- **Metric: ARR Growth Rate (YoY)**
  - GREEN: 3x+ year-over-year (or 15%+ MoM sustained)
  - YELLOW: 2x-3x YoY growth
  - RED: Under 2x YoY growth

- **Metric: Net Revenue Retention (NRR)**
  - GREEN: 120%+ NRR
  - YELLOW: 100-120% NRR
  - RED: Under 100% NRR (net churn)

- **Metric: Gross Margin**
  - GREEN: 70%+ gross margin
  - YELLOW: 50-70% gross margin
  - RED: Under 50% gross margin

- **Metric: Monthly Active Users (Consumer/PLG)**
  - GREEN: 100K+ MAU with healthy engagement
  - YELLOW: 50K-100K MAU
  - RED: Under 50K MAU

- **Metric: Retention (Month 6)**
  - GREEN: 30%+ M6 retention (consumer) or 85%+ logo retention (B2B annual)
  - YELLOW: 20-30% M6 (consumer) or 75-85% logo retention (B2B)
  - RED: Under 20% M6 (consumer) or under 75% logo retention (B2B)

- **Metric: CAC Payback Period**
  - GREEN: Under 12 months
  - YELLOW: 12-18 months
  - RED: Over 18 months or unknown

- **Metric: LTV:CAC Ratio**
  - GREEN: 3:1 or better
  - YELLOW: 2:1 to 3:1
  - RED: Under 2:1 or not calculated

- **Metric: Team Size**
  - GREEN: 10-25 people with key functional leads in place
  - YELLOW: 5-10 people, some key roles unfilled
  - RED: Under 5 people or missing critical functions (no dedicated sales/eng lead)

- **Metric: Runway at Time of Raise**
  - GREEN: 9+ months runway remaining
  - YELLOW: 6-9 months runway
  - RED: Under 6 months (raising from a position of weakness)

- **Metric: Burn Multiple**
  - GREEN: Under 2x burn multiple
  - YELLOW: 2x-4x burn multiple
  - RED: Over 4x burn multiple

## Typical Terms
- **Valuation ranges (pre-money):** $25M-$80M (median ~$35M-$50M). Top-tier companies with exceptional metrics can command $60M-$100M+.
- **Dilution norms:** 15-25% (target ~20%)
- **Common structures and tradeoffs:**
  - **Series A Preferred Stock:** Standard. Includes liquidation preference (1x non-participating is most founder-friendly), anti-dilution protection, and protective provisions.
  - **1x non-participating preferred:** Industry standard. Investor gets their money back OR converts to common -- whichever is greater. Avoid participating preferred.
  - **Participating preferred:** Investor gets money back AND their pro-rata share of remaining proceeds. Unfavorable for founders. Push back hard.
- **Board seat expectations:** 5-person board is standard: 2 founders/common seats, 2 investor seats (lead + sometimes a seed investor), 1 independent. Some leads accept a 3-person board (2 common, 1 investor).
- **Pro-rata rights:** Standard for all institutional investors. Major investors may negotiate super pro-rata rights.
- **Anti-dilution provisions:** Broad-based weighted average is standard. Avoid full ratchet anti-dilution.
- **Other common provisions:** Information rights, registration rights, right of first refusal (ROFR) on secondary sales, drag-along rights, founder vesting refresh (4-year vesting with 1-year cliff on new grants).

## Typical Spending Allocation

How Series A capital is typically deployed over 18-24 months:

- **Go-to-Market / Sales: 40-50%** — VP Sales, AEs, SDRs, demand gen, marketing team. This is the scaling stage: pour fuel on proven acquisition channels.
- **Engineering & Product: 25-35%** — Grow the eng team to support product roadmap, platform hardening, and scaling infrastructure. Product org grows but GTM gets the lion's share.
- **G&A / Operations: 10-15%** — VP Finance or fractional CFO, HR lead, legal, compliance. Operational infrastructure for a 20-50 person company.
- **Buffer: 5-10%** — Less buffer than seed because the business model is more predictable.

**Key principle:** At Series A, the capital allocation flips from product-heavy to GTM-heavy. You've proven the product works; now prove you can sell it repeatedly. If engineering is still consuming 50%+ of your budget, you may not have the PMF conviction needed for this stage.

## Key Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Raising without proven product-market fit.** The most common Series A failure. If you can't clearly articulate your ICP, repeatable acquisition channel, and retention metrics, you're not ready. VCs pass on "almost there" stories at this stage.

2. **Running a sloppy process.** Series A is a structured fundraise. Running it without a target list, data room, financial model, and coordinated meeting schedule signals lack of operational rigor -- exactly what VCs are evaluating.

3. **Accepting the first term sheet without leverage.** Run a competitive process to get good terms:
   - Schedule all first partner meetings within a 2-week window so term sheets arrive in the same period
   - When asked "who else are you talking to?", name firms honestly -- VCs talk to each other and verify
   - Set a soft close deadline ("we're targeting a decision by [date]") to create natural urgency
   - With 2-3 term sheets in hand, negotiate on board composition and protective provisions, not just valuation
   - Accepting the first offer without alternatives leaves money and governance terms on the table

4. **Underestimating board dynamics.** Your board will influence major decisions for years. Choose investors who complement your skills, have relevant operating experience, and who founders in their portfolio recommend enthusiastically. Call references.

5. **Not having a credible path to $100M ARR.** Series A investors underwrite to a venture outcome: $1B+ company. If your market, business model, and growth rate don't credibly support a path to $100M+ ARR, the math doesn't work for institutional VCs.

## The Narrative Shift

At Series A, the pitch shifts from early traction to **repeatable, scalable growth**.

- **Lead with:** Proven product-market fit and a clear, defensible go-to-market motion. Show the machine: how you acquire customers, how they expand, and why they stay. Unit economics and cohort data are the centerpiece.
- **What investors are actually evaluating:** Can this company become a market leader? They look at: strength of PMF (retention + NPS), efficiency of growth (burn multiple, CAC payback), quality of the team, and size of the market opportunity. The question shifts from "does this work?" to "how big can this get?"
- **How this differs from seed:** Seed investors bet on potential and early signals. Series A investors bet on proof. You need data, not just narratives. Cohort charts, unit economics, and pipeline data replace vision slides.
- **How this differs from Series B:** At Series B, you'll need to show the growth engine works at scale. At Series A, you need to show it works and is ready to be scaled with capital.

## Preparing for Series B (Transition Guide)

What changes between Series A and Series B -- start building toward these during your Series A:

| Series A Metric | Series B Equivalent | What Changes |
|----------------|-------------------|--------------|
| ARR $1.5M+ | ARR $8M+ (~5x higher) | Must show sustained growth, not just a moment in time |
| NRR 120%+ | NRR 130%+ | Expansion revenue engine must be proven and predictable |
| LTV:CAC 3:1+ | LTV:CAC 4:1+ | Efficiency must improve with scale, not stay flat |
| Burn multiple <2x | Burn multiple <1.5x | Capital efficiency becomes a defining metric |
| Functional leads emerging | Full VP-level executive team | VP Eng, VP Sales, VP Marketing, VP Product, Head of Finance |
| Not tracked | Revenue per employee ($150K+) | Organizational efficiency becomes visible |
| Not tracked | Magic number (>0.75) | Sales efficiency: net new ARR / prior quarter S&M spend |
| Not tracked | M12 retention | Long-term retention replaces short-term retention as proof |

**Hire ahead:** VP-level team must be in place or actively recruiting before you go to market. Series B investors evaluate the management team as much as the metrics.

**Narrative shift preview:** At Series A, your story is "the product works and the growth engine is emerging." At Series B, it must become "the business model works at scale and we're building a category-defining company."

## Timeline and Process

- **When to start:** 6+ months before you need the capital. Begin relationship-building with target partners 3-6 months before formally going to market. Have your metrics trending up for at least 2-3 consecutive quarters.
- **How long it typically takes:** 8-14 weeks from first partner meeting to close. Add 4-8 weeks for preparation.
- **Typical conversion rates:**
  - Outreach to partner meeting: ~40-50% (warm intro), ~5-10% (cold)
  - First partner meeting to deep dive / second meeting: 40-50%
  - Deep dive to term sheet: 30-40%
  - End-to-end: expect 15-25 first partner meetings to generate 2-3 term sheets (~10-15% overall)
  - **Diagnostic:** below 40% first-to-second-meeting conversion signals a pitch problem, not a volume problem -- fix the pitch before adding more meetings
- **Key milestones and preparation:**
  1. **3-6 months before:** Build relationships with 15-25 target firms. Take "get to know you" meetings. Share quarterly updates to build narrative momentum.
  2. **4-6 weeks before launch:** Prepare materials: pitch deck (15-20 slides with appendix), detailed financial model (3-year), customer references (5-8 referenceable customers), and a complete data room:
     - Cap table (fully diluted, including all SAFEs, options, and warrants)
     - Certificate of incorporation, bylaws, and board consents
     - Monthly P&L, balance sheet, and cash flow (last 12+ months)
     - Metrics dashboard: MRR/ARR, cohort retention, unit economics, pipeline
     - Key customer contracts (top 5-10 by ARR)
     - IP assignment agreements from all founders, employees, and contractors
     - Org chart with planned hires for next 12 months
     - Employment agreements and option grant summaries
     - For comprehensive DD preparation, run `/due-diligence`
  3. **Week 1-2:** Launch process. Send deck to 15-20 firms simultaneously. Schedule partner meetings in tight 2-week windows. Do NOT stagger -- run a parallel process.
  4. **Week 2-4:** Partner meetings and deep dives. Expect 2-hour sessions with data review, customer calls, and product demos. VCs will do back-channel references on founders.
  5. **Week 3-5:** Term sheets arrive. Evaluate on: valuation, board composition, investor value-add, portfolio conflicts, and partner chemistry.
  6. **Week 4-6:** Negotiate and sign term sheet. Begin legal due diligence (2-4 weeks).
  7. **Week 6-10:** Legal process: definitive documents, reps and warranties, closing conditions.
  8. **Week 8-14:** Close and fund.

## Who to Talk To

- **Types of investors active at this stage:**
  - Institutional venture capital firms (Series A specialists)
  - Multi-stage firms doing Series A
  - Corporate venture arms (strategically, not as lead)

- **Relevant firms:**
  - **a16z (Andreessen Horowitz):** $15M-$25M Series A checks. Strong platform team (recruiting, marketing, BD). Sector-focused partners.
  - **Sequoia Capital:** Premier Series A investor. $10M-$20M checks. Arc-to-Series-A pipeline. Highest brand signal.
  - **Benchmark:** $10M-$15M checks. Equal partnership model, deeply hands-on partners. Strong reputation for founder alignment.
  - **Accel:** $10M-$20M Series A. Strong in enterprise SaaS, developer tools, and European companies.
  - **First Round Capital:** Some First Round investments extend to Series A (leading or co-leading). Deep community and platform.
  - **Lightspeed Venture Partners:** $10M-$20M checks. Active across enterprise, consumer, and health.
  - **Greylock Partners:** $10M-$20M. Strong in enterprise and consumer. Operator-partners with deep company-building experience.
  - **Index Ventures:** $10M-$25M. Strong in both US and European markets.

- **Preparing for investor meetings:**
  - Request warm introductions from founders in the firm's portfolio
  - VCs respond to warm intros 5-10x more than cold outreach
  - Target the specific partner whose expertise matches your category
