# IPO Playbook

## Overview
- **Capital raised:** $200M-$5B+ (median for tech IPOs ~$500M-$2B)
- **Common structures:** Traditional underwritten IPO (book-built offering), direct listing, or SPAC merger. Book-built IPOs via investment banks dominate for large tech companies. Direct listings (Spotify 2018, Coinbase 2021, Roblox 2021) suit companies that don't need primary capital.
- **Timeline:** S-1 filing to first day of trading: 4-6 months (confidential S-1) or 3-4 months (public S-1). Total IPO preparation from engagement to close: 12-18 months.

## The IPO is Not a Fundraise — It's a Liquidity Event

The IPO is categorically different from any prior round:

- **The audience changes entirely:** You are no longer pitching VCs who are accustomed to startup risk profiles. You are presenting to institutional public market investors (mutual funds, hedge funds, pension funds) who compare you to hundreds of existing public companies. They will underwrite you against real public comps.
- **The pricing mechanism changes:** The investment bank syndicate runs a "book build" — taking indications of interest from institutional investors to set the final IPO price. The price isn't negotiated; it's discovered through demand.
- **The governance becomes permanent:** Public company obligations (quarterly earnings, SEC reporting, Regulation FD, insider trading policies, SOX compliance) begin immediately. There is no reversal.
- **The employees get paid — or don't.** If the stock falls below IPO price, employee morale and retention suffer profoundly. This is the most underappreciated risk of an aggressive IPO valuation.

## IPO Success: Companies That Held and Rose

These companies priced conservatively, had strong fundamental stories, and gave public investors room to win.

### Tesla (TSLA) — June 29, 2010
- **IPO price:** $17/share | **Raised:** ~$226M | **Valuation:** ~$1.7B
- **Day 1 close:** $23.89 (+40.5%)
- **By 2020 (split-adjusted):** Over $200/share — ~12x the IPO price
- **What they got right:**
  - Priced conservatively (market was skeptical about EVs; Tesla didn't oversell)
  - Clear founder vision with tangible product already in market (Roadster)
  - Category creation narrative: first American car company IPO since Ford
  - Strong institutional anchors including strategic investors

**Lesson:** Conservative pricing in a skeptical market creates upside for public investors — turning them into advocates. Tesla's retail investor base grew organically because early buyers made money.

### Snowflake (SNOW) — September 16, 2020
- **IPO price:** $120/share (above $100-110 initial range) | **Raised:** ~$3.4B | **Valuation:** ~$33B
- **Day 1 open:** $245 (doubled from IPO price); closed at $253.93
- **Landmark moment:** Warren Buffett's Berkshire Hathaway and Salesforce Ventures each invested $250M at IPO price — rare institutional anchor signal
- **What they got right:**
  - Revenue growing 121% YoY at IPO — undeniable growth story
  - Cloud data platform with consumption-based model created expansion revenue story (NRR 158%)
  - Strong institutional demand allowed pricing above range without killing day-1 performance
  - Timing: ZIRP environment + cloud tailwinds

**Lesson:** When growth metrics are exceptional, pricing above range is justified — but you still need to leave room for public investors. Snowflake's 121% NRR meant institutional investors could model durable growth.

### Airbnb (ABNB) — December 10, 2020
- **IPO price:** $68/share | **Raised:** ~$3.5B | **Valuation:** ~$47B
- **Day 1 close:** $144.71 (+112.8%)
- **Context:** IPO'd during COVID despite travel devastation; revenue down 30% in 2020
- **What they got right:**
  - Recovery narrative was credible and eventually proved accurate
  - Asset-light business model with strong unit economics at scale
  - Strong brand recognition globally
  - Conservative pricing relative to peak private valuation ($31B pre-COVID)
  - Revenue turned FCF positive during COVID through cost cuts — showed capital discipline

**Lesson:** A credible recovery narrative backed by strong unit economics can overcome near-term revenue headwinds. Airbnb's decision to use COVID to restructure its cost base made the IPO story actually stronger.

### ARM Holdings (ARM) — September 14, 2023
- **IPO price:** $51/share (top of $47-51 range) | **Raised:** ~$4.87B | **Valuation:** ~$54B
- **Day 1 close:** $63.59 (+24.7%)
- **Largest IPO of 2023** — successfully opened an IPO window after a dry 2022
- **What they got right:**
  - AI narrative: ARM chips are fundamental to AI inference workloads
  - SoftBank retained ~90% post-IPO; signal that the controlling shareholder had conviction
  - Conservatively priced relative to chip sector comps; investors could construct the bull case
  - Technical moat is genuinely defensible (instruction set architecture licensing)

**Lesson:** Strategic sector timing (AI tailwind in 2023) can offset fundamental questions. ARM's valuation was debatable, but the AI narrative created enough institutional demand to support the price.

### Reddit (RDDT) — March 21, 2024
- **IPO price:** $34/share (top of $31-34 range) | **Raised:** ~$748M | **Valuation:** ~$6.4B
- **Day 1 close:** $50.44 (+48%)
- **Note:** Priced at $10B private valuation (2021) — deliberately IPO'd at a "down round" to the last private price to give public investors upside
- **What they got right:**
  - Honest repricing from peak private valuation created credible floor
  - Unique community/data moat (AI training data licensing is a novel revenue stream)
  - Allocated shares to loyal users and moderators — genuine community alignment
  - Priced on fundamentals, not peak hype

**Lesson:** Going public at a significant discount to your last private round is not failure — it's the right setup for a durable public company. Reddit's willingness to reset expectations gave public investors a reason to own the stock.

### Circle (CRCL) — 2025 (Pending at knowledge cutoff)
- **S-1 filed:** January 2025 with the SEC
- **Planned exchange:** NYSE, ticker CRCL
- **Lead underwriters:** JPMorgan Chase, Citigroup
- **Revenue:** ~$1.7B in 2024 (per S-1), primarily from USDC reserve yield
- **IPO not yet priced** as of early 2025 knowledge cutoff; outcome not confirmed
- **Circle's story:** Second attempt at going public after abandoning a SPAC merger in 2022 (SPAC partner Circle agreed to merge with Concord Acquisition Corp for $9B — deal collapsed Dec 2022). Filed clean S-1 in 2025 with transparent financials.
- **What they've done right so far:**
  - Abandoned broken SPAC path; chose traditional IPO for credibility
  - S-1 is detailed and transparent, including risks from USDC competition and regulatory exposure
  - Revenue growth and profitability narrative is clear
  - Regulatory clarity on stablecoins improving in 2025

**Lesson (preliminary):** Circle's decision to abandon the SPAC and come back with a clean S-1 reflects a more mature approach to public markets. Companies that go public the right way — with audited financials, transparent risk factors, and realistic valuations — build durable investor trust.

## IPO Failure: Companies That Broke Issue Price

These companies either overpriced, had structural flaws, or mis-timed their IPO.

### WeWork — Failed IPO 2019; SPAC 2021; Bankrupt 2023
- **2019 IPO attempt:** Filed at $47B valuation; IPO pulled after investor backlash
- **S-1 revealed:** $1.9B net loss in 2018, "community-adjusted EBITDA" metric that excluded all losses, extensive related-party transactions (Adam Neumann leasing properties to WeWork), and governance structure giving Neumann 20-to-1 voting rights
- **SPAC (Oct 2021):** Went public at ~$9B via BowX Acquisition Corp SPAC. Stock steadily declined
- **Chapter 11 (Nov 2023):** Filed for bankruptcy at near-zero value
- **What went wrong:**
  - S-1 revealed metrics that were manufactured to hide fundamental unit economics failure
  - Governance failures were severe and publicly embarrassing
  - SoftBank's $18.5B+ investment created a single-investor dynamic that removed market discipline
  - Revenue was growing but each location lost money at maturity — the business model didn't scale

**Lesson for founders:** Governance matters at IPO. A single egregious related-party transaction in your S-1 can collapse investor confidence. And custom metrics ("community-adjusted EBITDA") that exist to obscure losses will be publicly dissected by every journalist and short-seller.

### Robinhood (HOOD) — July 29, 2021
- **IPO price:** $38/share | **Raised:** ~$2.1B | **Valuation:** ~$32B
- **Day 1 close:** $34.82 (-8.4%) — rare negative first-day performance for a major IPO
- **Low of ~$6.81 by Jun 2022 (-82% from IPO price)**
- **What went wrong:**
  - Revenue heavily concentrated in PFOF (payment for order flow) — regulators were publicly considering banning it
  - GameStop incident (Jan 2021) created massive negative press coverage and Congressional scrutiny
  - Allocated ~35% of IPO shares to its own retail customers — unprecedented; institutional investors were skeptical of retail-heavy book
  - Priced for a fintech growth multiple that assumed PFOF was safe, which investors didn't believe
  - Valuation implied 25x revenue at a time of rising rates — left no margin of safety

**Lesson:** Know your regulatory exposure before IPO. Robinhood's core revenue model was under active regulatory threat at the time of IPO. No valuation multiple can compensate for fundamental business model risk.

### Uber (UBER) — May 10, 2019
- **IPO price:** $45/share (below $44-50 range) | **Raised:** ~$8.1B | **Valuation:** ~$82B
- **Day 1 close:** $41.57 (-7.6%) — largest IPO to drop on day 1 in years
- **Within 6 months:** Down to ~$25; market cap nearly halved
- **Eventually recovered:** Profitable by 2023, shares traded above $70 by 2024
- **What went wrong at IPO:**
  - No path to profitability — burning $1B/quarter with no clear model for when losses stopped
  - Lyft's IPO had failed two weeks prior, spooking institutional investors for ride-sharing as a category
  - Driver/labor concerns (employee classification) created open-ended legal liability
  - Trade war headlines hit broader market on Uber's IPO day
  - Investment banks underpriced to $45 vs. original $48-55 target range — already a markdown before pricing

**Long-term lesson:** Uber eventually worked because the business model matured, but the IPO investors were right to be skeptical — it took 4+ years for the thesis to play out. Don't ask public investors to underwrite a business model that isn't proven yet.

### Lyft (LYFT) — March 29, 2019
- **IPO price:** $72/share | **Raised:** ~$2.3B
- **Day 1:** Opened at $87 (+21%), then closed at $78.29 — fell below IPO price within 3 trading days
- **Low of ~$9 (2022)** — down 87% from IPO price
- **What went wrong:**
  - Permanent #2 in ride-sharing: no plausible path to market leadership
  - Operating losses with no path to profitability narrative
  - Priced at IPO as if it could challenge Uber — investors never believed the story
  - IPO set the wrong precedent for Uber (which IPO'd weeks later and dropped immediately)

**Lesson:** IPO only if you have a credible market leadership narrative. Second-place market positions with high capital intensity and no moat are difficult to value at public market multiples.

### Robinhood Companion Case — Instacart (CART) — September 19, 2023
- **IPO price:** $30/share (priced at low end of $26-28 range, then raised) | **Raised:** ~$660M | **Valuation:** ~$10B
- **Day 1 open:** $42 (+40%) — appeared successful
- **Within 3 months:** Fell below $30 IPO price; below $24 by early 2024
- **Down from $39B peak private valuation (2021)**
- **What went wrong:**
  - IPO'd as COVID grocery delivery tailwinds were clearly fading
  - Intensive competition from Amazon Fresh, Walmart Grocery, DoorDash
  - Gross margin in grocery delivery is structurally difficult; unit economics never improved post-COVID
  - Day-1 pop was driven by IPO scarcity in 2023 (limited supply), not sustainable institutional conviction

**Lesson:** Day-1 pops in thin IPO markets can mask weak fundamentals. Instacart's post-lockup performance reflected what institutional investors actually thought of the business model after holding it for 90 days.

### Rivian (RIVN) — November 10, 2021
- **IPO price:** $78/share | **Raised:** ~$11.9B** — largest IPO of 2021
- **Within days:** Surged to $179.47; market cap briefly exceeded $150B (more than Ford + GM combined)
- **By 2022:** Below IPO price. By 2024: ~$12-15
- **What went wrong:**
  - Zero vehicles delivered to paying customers at IPO (only ~1,000 total, all to employees)
  - Capital requirements of EV manufacturing massively underestimated
  - EV hype in late 2021 masked fundamental production risk
  - Amazon delivery van contract created false impression of commercial validation
  - IPO'd into peak ZIRP market; when rates rose, high-burn capital-intensive businesses repriced harshly

**Lesson:** Hardware businesses require actual production capability before IPO. Investors who bought Rivian at $179 were paying $150B for a company with no proven manufacturing ability. The "Tesla of delivery trucks" narrative couldn't survive contact with operational reality.

### Blue Apron (APRN) — June 29, 2017
- **IPO price:** $10/share (slashed from $15-17 range) | **Raised:** ~$300M
- **Fell below IPO price almost immediately**
- **Low: ~$1.50 (2019)**; eventually acquired for ~$103M in 2023 — a 97% loss from IPO valuation
- **What went wrong:**
  - Amazon announced Whole Foods acquisition the week before Blue Apron's S-1 — instantly redefined competitive landscape
  - Brutal unit economics: customer acquisition costs exceeded lifetime value for most cohorts
  - Churn was severe; retained customers declined even as the company spent to acquire new ones
  - Management was already cutting IPO range by 33% before trading began — a very bad signal

**Lesson:** Timing matters. A major competitive announcement (Amazon + Whole Foods) days before your S-1 announcement is a genuine reason to delay. And if your own bankers are slashing the IPO range 33% before pricing, reconsider whether the business is ready.

## What Separates IPOs That Hold From IPOs That Break

The research across successful and failed IPOs reveals consistent patterns:

| Dimension | Held / Rose | Broke Issue Price |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Pricing approach** | Conservative; left upside for public investors | Aggressive; priced for perfection |
| **Profitability** | Profitable or clear, credible path within 12 months | Indefinite losses with no proven path |
| **Market position** | #1 in category or clear path to it | #2 or competing with giant (Amazon, Google) |
| **Institutional demand** | Oversubscribed from quality institutions; anchor orders | Retail-heavy demand; institutions hedging positions |
| **Unit economics** | Strong and improving | Weak or deteriorating at scale |
| **Competitive timing** | IPO'd before or after competitive threats materialized | IPO'd directly into competitive headwind (Blue Apron, Instacart) |
| **Revenue concentration** | Diversified, recurring | Concentrated in one product / regulatory-exposed |
| **Governance** | Clean, independent board, transparent S-1 | Related-party transactions, custom metrics |
| **Market timing** | Favorable macro / sector tailwind | Peak hype cycle (2021 vintage) or rising rates |
| **Secondary market** | Disciplined pre-IPO secondary pricing | Secondary at large discount to last round (bad signal) |

## IPO Process Overview

### Phase 1: Pre-Filing Preparation (Months -18 to -6)
- Engage Big 4 auditor; complete 3 years of PCAOB-compliant audited financials
- Conduct underwriter bake-off; select bookrunners and co-managers
- Recruit independent board directors; form audit and compensation committees
- Engage IR advisory firm; begin quarterly earnings cadence preparation
- SOX 404(a) management assessment of internal controls
- Begin S-1 drafting with outside counsel (typically Latham & Watkins, Wilson Sonsini, Cooley, or Fenwick)

### Phase 2: Confidential S-1 Filing (Months -6 to -3)
- File confidential S-1 (Emerging Growth Companies can file confidentially)
- Begin SEC review/comment process (typically 2-3 rounds, 6-12 weeks)
- Draft roadshow deck and presentation; begin analyst education
- Update financials with most recent quarter data
- Conduct investor education "testing the waters" meetings (permitted for EGCs)

### Phase 3: Public S-1 and Roadshow (Weeks -6 to 0)
- File public S-1 (or amend confidential filing)
- Begin analyst roadshow (sell-side analysts write initiation reports)
- Management roadshow: 8-10 days, 40-50 institutional investor meetings (1-on-1 and group formats)
- Investment bank syndicate builds book of demand (indications of interest)
- Price IPO night before trading; allocate shares to institutional investors
- Day 1 trading: NYSE or NASDAQ

### Phase 4: Post-IPO (Month 0+)
- **Day 1:** Stock begins trading; underwriters may support price if needed (stabilization)
- **First 25 days:** Quiet period (no forward-looking statements from company)
- **25-40 days:** Analyst research reports publish (first major sell-side coverage)
- **90 days:** First earnings call as a public company
- **180 days:** Employee lockup expires — often a stock price headwind. Plan around this.
- **Year 1:** Establish quarterly earnings cadence; investor relations becomes a full-time function

## Readiness Benchmarks (for /product-metrics)

These thresholds power the diagnostic grading system:

- **Metric: Revenue and Growth**
  - GREEN: $200M+ ARR growing 30%+ YoY; or consumer revenue with clear path to $1B
  - YELLOW: $100M-$200M ARR or 20-30% growth
  - RED: Under $100M ARR or under 20% growth (public market multiples too compressed for compelling story)

- **Metric: Profitability / Path to Profitability**
  - GREEN: FCF positive or non-GAAP operating profitable with credible path to GAAP profitability within 12 months
  - YELLOW: FCF negative but clear path to positive within 24 months; improving margins
  - RED: FCF deeply negative with no credible model for profitability

- **Metric: NRR (B2B)**
  - GREEN: 120%+ NRR with stable trends
  - YELLOW: 105-120% NRR
  - RED: Under 105% NRR (approaching net neutral — danger for SaaS public company story)

- **Metric: Gross Margin**
  - GREEN: 70%+ gross margin
  - YELLOW: 55-70% gross margin
  - RED: Under 55% gross margin (very difficult to support software-like public valuation)

- **Metric: Competitive Position**
  - GREEN: Undisputed #1 in category, moat is clear and measurable
  - YELLOW: #1 or #2 with strong differentiation
  - RED: #3 or below, or direct competition with Amazon/Google/Microsoft in core market

- **Metric: Audited Financials**
  - GREEN: 3 years of Big 4 PCAOB-compliant audited financials; SOX 404 compliant
  - YELLOW: 2 years audited (Big 4); SOX 404(a) complete but 404(b) pending
  - RED: Less than 2 years audited or non-Big-4 auditor

- **Metric: Board Independence**
  - GREEN: Majority independent board; audit, compensation, and nominating/governance committees fully operational with qualified chairs
  - YELLOW: Board partially independent; committees forming
  - RED: VC-dominated board without independent majority

- **Metric: Secondary Market Pricing**
  - GREEN: Secondary market trades at or above last private round price; liquid
  - YELLOW: 10-20% discount to last private round
  - RED: >20% discount to last private round or illiquid secondary market

- **Metric: IPO Window / Market Conditions**
  - GREEN: Comparable public companies trading well; recent IPOs in sector have held; markets stable
  - YELLOW: Market volatility elevated but manageable; sector peers have mixed performance
  - RED: Sector in correction; IPO window effectively closed; recent comparable IPOs have broken issue price

## Typical Terms and Economics

### IPO Pricing Economics
- **IPO discount to intrinsic value:** Typically 10-20% discount to fair value to ensure day-1 pop and institutional demand. A 10-20% day-1 pop is considered optimal — enough to reward institutional investors without giving away too much.
- **Too small a pop (<5%):** Often means the company was overpriced; institutional investors exit immediately; stock falls below IPO price (WeWork path)
- **Too large a pop (>50%):** Money left on table; company could have raised more. But not always: extreme pops (Airbnb +112%, Snowflake +111%) reflect demand undersupply more than mispricing

### Underwriter Economics
- **Gross spread:** Typically 3.5-7% of IPO proceeds (lower for mega-deals). Split: ~60% to selling concession, ~20% management fee, ~20% underwriting fee
- **Overallotment option (Green Shoe):** Underwriters receive option to sell 15% more shares at IPO price within 30 days. If stock rises: underwriters exercise the option (more shares sold). If stock falls: underwriters buy shares in market to stabilize price.
- **Lockup period:** All company insiders are locked up for 180 days post-IPO. Plan around the 180-day cliff — stock often drops 10-20% when lockup expires as insiders sell.

### Employee Equity at IPO
- **RSU cliff:** If employees have RSUs, the IPO triggers a taxable event. The company must withhold shares for taxes — creating immediate sell pressure. Pre-IPO tender offers mitigate this by giving employees pre-IPO liquidity to pay taxes.
- **Option holders:** Employees with stock options get to participate in the public market premium. But if the stock falls below the strike price, options are underwater — destroying retention.
- **Post-IPO grant strategy:** Companies should refresh equity for key employees 6-12 months post-IPO. The best talent will have competing offers from FAANG once they're liquid.

## Key Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Pricing above what the market will sustain.** The most common IPO mistake. Investors who paid $38 for Robinhood and $78 for Rivian became enemies of the stock — writing negative coverage, selling short, and lobbying for lower valuation targets. Your IPO price is not a mark of success; your 12-month post-IPO stock price is. Leave room for public investors to win.

2. **IPO-ing with a known regulatory or competitive existential threat.** Robinhood's PFOF dependency was under public regulatory scrutiny at IPO time. Blue Apron's Amazon/Whole Foods competitor announced days before S-1. If a credible existential threat exists, investors will price it into the stock — often punitively. Fix or disclose before going public.

3. **Ignoring the lockup cliff.** 180 days after IPO, insiders can sell. If you haven't communicated a compelling stock story, every institutional holder will front-run the lockup — selling before insiders flood the market. The stock often falls 15-30% in the weeks before lockup expiry. Counter with: strong earnings, positive guidance, and insider retention selling programs.

4. **Choosing underwriters for relationships, not capability.** A bank with weak distribution into quality institutional investors will produce a retail-heavy book — exactly what Robinhood suffered. The right underwriting syndicate has: (1) deep relationships with top-100 mutual fund families who hold for years, (2) strong research analysts in your sector, (3) a track record of IPOs that held 12 months post-trading.

5. **Under-investing in investor relations before and after IPO.** The sell-side analyst community shapes narrative. Ensure your IR team is building relationships with analysts at all bookrunner banks 6-12 months before filing. Post-IPO, quarterly earnings calls, investor days, and conference participation are now mandatory — under-resource this function at your peril.

6. **Using aggressive non-GAAP metrics to mask GAAP losses.** Post-WeWork, institutional investors are deeply skeptical of custom metrics. Present GAAP numbers prominently. If you need to explain non-GAAP adjustments, explain them clearly and conservatively. The best IPOs use GAAP-positive metrics as the headline.

## The Narrative at IPO

Your IPO narrative must answer four questions that every institutional investor will ask:

1. **"Why is this company a market leader, and why will it stay one?"** — Competitive moat, market share, and switching costs. Not just "we're growing fast" — why does the moat deepen with scale?

2. **"How does this company make money, and does the model get better with scale?"** — Gross margins, NRR, operating leverage. Show that each incremental dollar of revenue is more profitable than the last.

3. **"What is the addressable market, and how much of it can this company realistically capture?"** — TAM, but grounded in bottoms-up analysis, not top-down assertions. Tesla convinced investors the global auto market was addressable. Circle must convince investors stablecoin infrastructure is a massive market.

4. **"Why is now the right time to go public, and what does the company do with public market access?"** — Use of proceeds, strategic rationale, and why the IPO creates value for all stakeholders.

## Who to Talk To (Underwriter Selection)

- **Lead-left bookrunner (most important):** Sets the valuation range, runs the book, has primary relationship with institutional investors. For large tech IPOs:
  - **Goldman Sachs:** Premier large-cap tech IPO franchise. Required for $2B+ raises.
  - **Morgan Stanley:** Strong technology IPO track record (Snowflake, ARM, Airbnb). Excellent equity research.
  - **JP Morgan:** Scale and distribution; strong for $1B+ IPOs.

- **Co-bookrunners:**
  - **Citigroup:** Active in large tech (Circle's IPO); strong fixed income and global distribution.
  - **Allen & Co.:** Boutique with deep founder relationships in consumer and media tech.
  - **BofA Securities:** Strong institutional distribution; active in consumer tech.

- **Co-managers (3-5 firms):** Smaller allocations, regional or specialist distribution. Jefferies, Cowen, Piper Sandler, Needham for specialist technology coverage.

- **Evaluation criteria for bookrunners:**
  - Quality of equity research analyst covering your sector (will they write positive initiation?)
  - Track record with comparable IPOs (ask for list of last 10 tech IPOs and 12-month performance)
  - Distribution capability: what % of their last 5 IPO books were institutional vs. retail?
  - Stabilization track record: how many of their recent IPOs required stabilization (bad signal)?
  - Relationship quality: which partner will be on your deal day-to-day?
