# Series B Fundraising Playbook

## Overview
- **Typical raise amount:** $15M-$60M (median ~$30M-$40M in 2024-2025)
- **Common structures:** Priced equity round (Series B Preferred Stock). Structured terms and growth equity features become more common.
- **Typical timeline:** 8-16 weeks from first meeting to close; relationship-building with target investors should begin 6-12 months ahead

## What Investors Expect at This Stage
- **Traction benchmarks:** Repeatable growth engine. For B2B SaaS: $5M-$15M+ ARR with clear path to $50M+. For consumer: millions of MAU with strong retention and monetization. For marketplace: significant GMV with improving take rates and unit economics.
- **Team composition:** 30-80+ people. Full executive team in place or being recruited: VP Engineering, VP Sales, VP Marketing, VP Product, Head of Finance. Middle management layer forming. Clear organizational structure.
- **Market proof needed:** Dominant position in initial market segment. Evidence of ability to expand (new segments, geographies, or product lines). Competitive moat becoming visible (network effects, data advantages, switching costs, brand).
- **Product maturity:** Multi-product or multi-segment capability. Enterprise-grade reliability and security (if B2B). Platform potential emerging. Product org is shipping predictably.

## Readiness Benchmarks (for /diagnose)

These thresholds power the diagnostic grading system:

- **Metric: ARR (B2B SaaS)**
  - GREEN: $8M+ ARR
  - YELLOW: $5M-$8M ARR
  - RED: Under $5M ARR

- **Metric: ARR Growth Rate (YoY)**
  - GREEN: 2.5x+ year-over-year (T2D3 trajectory)
  - YELLOW: 1.5x-2.5x YoY growth
  - RED: Under 1.5x YoY growth

- **Metric: Net Revenue Retention (NRR)**
  - GREEN: 130%+ NRR
  - YELLOW: 110-130% NRR
  - RED: Under 110% NRR

- **Metric: Gross Margin**
  - GREEN: 75%+ gross margin
  - YELLOW: 65-75% gross margin
  - RED: Under 65% gross margin

- **Metric: CAC Payback Period**
  - GREEN: Under 12 months
  - YELLOW: 12-18 months
  - RED: Over 18 months

- **Metric: LTV:CAC Ratio**
  - GREEN: 4:1 or better
  - YELLOW: 3:1 to 4:1
  - RED: Under 3:1

- **Metric: Burn Multiple**
  - GREEN: Under 1.5x burn multiple
  - YELLOW: 1.5x-3x burn multiple
  - RED: Over 3x burn multiple

- **Metric: Monthly Active Users (Consumer)**
  - GREEN: 1M+ MAU with monetization
  - YELLOW: 500K-1M MAU
  - RED: Under 500K MAU

- **Metric: Retention (Month 12)**
  - GREEN: 20%+ M12 retention (consumer) or 90%+ annual logo retention (B2B)
  - YELLOW: 12-20% M12 (consumer) or 80-90% logo retention (B2B)
  - RED: Under 12% M12 (consumer) or under 80% logo retention (B2B)

- **Metric: Team Size**
  - GREEN: 40+ employees with VP-level leadership across functions
  - YELLOW: 20-40 employees, some VP roles unfilled
  - RED: Under 20 employees or missing critical executive hires

- **Metric: Revenue per Employee**
  - GREEN: $150K+ ARR per employee
  - YELLOW: $100K-$150K ARR per employee
  - RED: Under $100K ARR per employee

- **Metric: Sales Efficiency (Magic Number)**
  - GREEN: Magic number > 0.75 (net new ARR / sales & marketing spend in prior quarter)
  - YELLOW: 0.5-0.75
  - RED: Under 0.5

- **Metric: Runway at Time of Raise**
  - GREEN: 12+ months runway
  - YELLOW: 9-12 months runway
  - RED: Under 9 months runway

## Typical Terms
- **Valuation ranges (pre-money):** $80M-$300M (median ~$120M-$200M). Exceptional companies (top-decile growth + retention) can command $250M-$500M+.
- **Dilution norms:** 10-20% (target ~15%)
- **Common structures and tradeoffs:**
  - **Series B Preferred Stock:** Standard. 1x non-participating liquidation preference is still the norm for top-tier companies. Weaker companies may face participating preferred or higher liquidation preferences.
  - **Structured terms to watch:** In tighter markets, investors may push for 1.5x-2x liquidation preferences, participating preferred, or ratchets. Resist these unless your alternatives are limited.
  - **Secondary sales:** Common for founders and early employees to sell 10-20% of their vested shares. Healthy signal -- aligns long-term incentives.
- **Board seat expectations:** 7-person board: 2 common/founder seats, 2-3 investor seats (Series A lead, Series B lead, possibly seed), 1-2 independents. Board governance becomes substantive -- monthly board meetings with formal materials.
- **Pro-rata rights:** All institutional investors will exercise pro-rata. Major investors may negotiate for super pro-rata allocation.
- **Anti-dilution provisions:** Broad-based weighted average is standard. Full ratchet is a red flag -- only accept under extreme circumstances.
- **Other provisions:** D&O insurance required. Quarterly board reporting with standardized financial packages. Employee option pool refresh (typically 5-10% top-up). Right of first refusal on secondary sales.

## Key Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Raising without clear unit economics.** Series B investors model your business to $100M+ ARR. If you can't show that each dollar invested in growth returns multiples predictably, the investment thesis falls apart. Know your fully-loaded CAC, gross margin, NRR, and payback period cold.

2. **Growing headcount faster than revenue.** The single most common Series B trap. Companies hire aggressively post-Series A, burn rate spikes, and efficiency metrics deteriorate. Investors at this stage scrutinize burn multiple and revenue per employee closely.

3. **Neglecting the competitive narrative.** At Series B, you will face pointed questions about competition, defensibility, and market position. "We have no competitors" is not credible. Show clear differentiation, win rates, and why your moat deepens over time.

4. **Weak executive team.** Series B investors expect you to have (or be actively recruiting) experienced VPs across sales, engineering, marketing, and product. If the founders are still doing everything, it signals inability to scale. Hire ahead of the curve.

5. **No credible path to profitability.** While Series B companies are not expected to be profitable, investors need to see a clear path. What does the P&L look like at scale? At what ARR do you break even? Companies that can't answer this face tough terms or no deal.

## The Narrative Shift

At Series B, the pitch shifts from product-market fit to **building a category-defining company**.

- **Lead with:** Your growth engine and proof it scales. Show that you've built a repeatable, efficient go-to-market machine. Present your path from current ARR to $50M-$100M+ ARR with specific assumptions and evidence for each.
- **What investors are actually evaluating:** Can this become a $1B-$5B+ company? They look at: efficiency of the growth engine (burn multiple, magic number), quality of the management team, market expansion opportunity, competitive dynamics, and path to profitability. They're underwriting the business model, not just the product.
- **How this differs from Series A:** Series A proved the product works and people want it. Series B proves the business model works at scale. The conversation moves from product-market fit to go-to-market fit, org scaling, and market leadership.
- **How this differs from later stages:** At Series C and beyond, the focus shifts to market dominance, international expansion, and pre-IPO positioning. Series B is the bridge -- you're proving you can scale, not that you already have.

## Timeline and Process

- **When to start:** Begin relationship-building 6-12 months before going to market. Formally launch the process when you have 12+ months of runway and metrics are in an uptrend. Avoid launching during seasonal dips in metrics.
- **How long it typically takes:** 10-16 weeks from first partner meeting to close. The full timeline including preparation is 4-6 months.
- **Key milestones and preparation:**
  1. **6-12 months before:** Identify 15-20 target growth-stage firms. Build relationships through portfolio introductions, industry events, and quarterly investor updates. Share metrics transparently to build trust.
  2. **6-8 weeks before launch:** Prepare comprehensive materials: pitch deck (20-25 slides), detailed financial model (5-year with bottoms-up assumptions), data room (audited or reviewed financials, contracts, cap table, org chart, customer metrics dashboard, cohort analyses), 8-10 referenceable customers.
  3. **Week 1-2:** Launch process to 10-15 firms simultaneously. Schedule partner meetings within a 2-week window. Coordinate with your Series A lead on introductions and signaling.
  4. **Week 2-5:** Partner meetings, management presentations, and deep dives. Expect 4-6 hour sessions with full team presentations, detailed financial review, customer reference calls (investors will call 10-15 customers), and technical architecture reviews.
  5. **Week 4-7:** Term sheets arrive. Evaluate on: valuation, structure (beware structured terms), board composition, investor platform and value-add, partner track record, and fund size (ensure your check size is meaningful to their fund).
  6. **Week 5-8:** Negotiate term sheet. Legal due diligence begins (more thorough than Series A -- expect 3-5 weeks).
  7. **Week 8-14:** Legal process, definitive agreements, closing conditions.
  8. **Week 10-16:** Close and fund.

## Who to Talk To

- **Types of investors active at this stage:**
  - Growth-stage venture capital firms
  - Multi-stage firms with dedicated growth teams
  - Crossover funds (public/private)
  - Late-stage specialists
  - Strategic corporate venture arms (as co-investors, not leads)

- **Relevant firms:**
  - **a16z (Andreessen Horowitz):** $20M-$60M Series B checks. Growth team with dedicated operational support. Strong in enterprise, fintech, and consumer.
  - **Sequoia Capital:** $15M-$50M+ Series B. Deep bench of company-building partners. Strong signal for future rounds.
  - **Tiger Global:** $20M-$100M checks. Fast decision-making, minimal governance demands. Less hands-on but efficient capital.
  - **Lightspeed Venture Partners:** $20M-$50M Growth fund. Active across sectors with both US and international reach.
  - **Accel:** $20M-$50M Series B. Strong track record in scaling enterprise companies. Global presence.
  - **IVP (Institutional Venture Partners):** $20M-$60M. Growth-stage specialists with strong IPO track record.
  - **General Catalyst:** $20M-$60M. Active across enterprise and consumer with strong platform support.
  - **Insight Partners:** $20M-$100M. Software-focused, ScaleUp program for operational support.
  - **Addition (Lee Fixel):** $20M-$80M. Sector-agnostic, founder-friendly, fast process.

- **Preparing for this stage:**
  - Engage an experienced CFO or VP Finance before going to market
  - Consider hiring a banker for rounds above $30M (Qatalyst, Allen & Co, Morgan Stanley for larger rounds; boutiques like Code Advisors for $15M-$40M)
  - Your Series A investors should be active advocates -- their enthusiasm (or lack thereof) sends a strong signal
